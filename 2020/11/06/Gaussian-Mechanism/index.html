Posted on 2020-11-06 Edited on 2022-12-23

Background

Let (X,d)(\mathcal{X}, d)(X,d) be a metric space, and fff be a function X→Rn\mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^nX→Rn.
A Gaussian mechanism adds Gaussian noise to the output of fff, such that for all neighboring pairs x,y∈Xx, y \in \mathcal{X}x,y∈X with d(x,y)=1d(x, y) = 1d(x,y)=1, it is hard to distinguish the outputs, i.e., their outputs distributions are similar.

Definition. The sensitivity Δ\DeltaΔ of fff is defined as

Δ=max⁡x,y∈X,d(x,y)=1∥f(x)−f(y)∥2, \Delta = \max_{x, y \in \mathcal{X}, d(x, y) = 1 } \Vert f(x) - f(y) \Vert_2, Δ=x,y∈X,d(x,y)=1max​∥f(x)−f(y)∥2​,

where ∥⋅∥2\Vert \cdot \Vert_2∥⋅∥2​ is the ℓ2\ell_2ℓ2​-norm of a vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn.

Definition. Given fff and a variance parameter σ2\sigma^2σ2, the output of the Gaussian mechanism is a random variable defines as

g(x)=f(x)+X, g(x) = f(x) + X, g(x)=f(x)+X,

where X=(X1,X2,...,Xn)∼N(0,σ2I)X = (X_1, X_2, ..., X_n) \sim N(0, \sigma^2 I)X=(X1​,X2​,...,Xn​)∼N(0,σ2I).

Privacy Guarantee

Theorem. For δ,ϵ∈(0,1)\delta, \epsilon \in (0, 1)δ,ϵ∈(0,1), if σ2=Δ2ϵ2(2ln⁡1δ+ϵ)\sigma^2 = \frac{ \Delta^2 }{ \epsilon^2 } \Big( 2 \ln \frac{1}{\delta} + \epsilon \Big)σ2=ϵ2Δ2​(2lnδ1​+ϵ), then ∀x,y∈X\forall x, y \in \mathcal{X}∀x,y∈X, s.t., d(x,y)=1d(x, y) = 1d(x,y)=1, and for all measurable subset S⊂RnS \subset \mathbb{R}^nS⊂Rn, it holds

Pr⁡[g(x)∈S]≤eϵ⋅Pr⁡[g(y)∈S]+δ \Pr[ g(x) \in S] \le e^\epsilon \cdot \Pr[ g(y) \in S] + \delta Pr[g(x)∈S]≤eϵ⋅Pr[g(y)∈S]+δ

It is obvious that if we set σ→∞\sigma \rightarrow \inftyσ→∞, then the Gaussian distribution converge to a uniform one and it holds that Pr⁡[g(x)∈S]=Pr⁡[g(y)∈S]\Pr[ g(x) \in S] = \Pr[ g(y) \in S]Pr[g(x)∈S]=Pr[g(y)∈S].
Therefore, we are interested in how small σ\sigmaσ can be.

We claim that σ=O(Δϵ)\sigma = O(\frac{\Delta}{\epsilon } )σ=O(ϵΔ​) suffices.
This is based on three observations:

  1. Most of the probability mass of a Gaussian distribution concentrates on a ball centered at its mean with radius O(σ)O(\sigma)O(σ).
  2. The density ratio of any two points within the ball is bounded by a constant.
  3. The distance between the distribution center of g(x)g(x)g(x) and g(y)g(y)g(y) is bounded by Δ\DeltaΔ.

Proof. To ease the notations, we write g(y)=f(y)+Yg(y) = f(y) + Yg(y)=f(y)+Y to distinguish it from g(x)g(x)g(x).
By definition,

g(x)∼N(f(x),σ2I),g(y)∼N(f(y),σ2I).g(x) \sim N(f(x), \sigma^2 I), \quad g(y) \sim N( f(y), \sigma^2 I). g(x)∼N(f(x),σ2I),g(y)∼N(f(y),σ2I).

Let pxp_xpx​ and pyp_ypy​ the density functions of g(x)g(x)g(x) and g(y)g(y)g(y) respectively.
The sketch of the proof is as follows: we partition Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn into two parts:

If Pr⁡[g(x)∈Y2]≤δ\Pr[ g(x) \in \mathcal{Y}_2 ] \le \deltaPr[g(x)∈Y2​]≤δ (\big(( here we measure the size of Y2\mathcal{Y}_2Y2​ by the probability measure induced by g(x)g(x)g(x) )\big)), then

Pr⁡[g(x)∈S]=Pr⁡[g(x)∈S∩Y1]+Pr⁡[g(x)∈S∩Y2]≤Pr⁡[g(x)∈S∩Y1]+Pr⁡[g(x)∈Y2]≤Pr⁡[g(x)∈S∩Y1]+δ=∫S∩Y1px(t) dt+δ≤∫S∩Y1eϵ⋅py(t) dt+δ=eϵ⋅Pr⁡[g(y)∈S∩Y1]+δ.≤eϵ⋅Pr⁡[g(y)∈S]+δ.\begin{aligned} \Pr[ g(x) \in S] &= \Pr[ g(x) \in S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1] + \Pr[ g(x) \in S \cap \mathcal{Y}_2] \\ &\le \Pr[ g(x) \in S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1] + \Pr[ g(x) \in \mathcal{Y}_2] \\ &\le \Pr[ g(x) \in S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1] + \delta \\ &= \int_{S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1 } p_x(t) \ d t + \delta \\ &\le \int_{S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1 } e^\epsilon \cdot p_y(t) \ d t + \delta \\ &= e^\epsilon \cdot \Pr[ g(y) \in S \cap \mathcal{Y}_1] + \delta. \\ &\le e^\epsilon \cdot \Pr[ g(y) \in S ] + \delta. \\ \end{aligned} Pr[g(x)∈S]​=Pr[g(x)∈S∩Y1​]+Pr[g(x)∈S∩Y2​]≤Pr[g(x)∈S∩Y1​]+Pr[g(x)∈Y2​]≤Pr[g(x)∈S∩Y1​]+δ=∫S∩Y1​​px​(t) dt+δ≤∫S∩Y1​​eϵ⋅py​(t) dt+δ=eϵ⋅Pr[g(y)∈S∩Y1​]+δ.≤eϵ⋅Pr[g(y)∈S]+δ.​

As py(t)>0p_y(t) > 0py​(t)>0 for all t∈Rnt \in \mathbb{R}^nt∈Rn, we can rewrite

Y1={t∈Rn:ln⁡px(t)py(t)≤ϵ}Y2={t∈Rn:ln⁡px(t)py(t)>ϵ}. \mathcal{Y}_1 = \Big\{ t \in \mathcal{R}^n : \ln \frac{p_x(t) }{ p_y(t) } \le \epsilon \Big\} \\ \mathcal{Y}_2 = \Big\{ t \in \mathcal{R}^n : \ln \frac{p_x(t) }{ p_y(t) } > \epsilon \Big\}. Y1​={t∈Rn:lnpy​(t)px​(t)​≤ϵ}Y2​={t∈Rn:lnpy​(t)px​(t)​>ϵ}.

In literature, the ratio ln⁡px(t)py(t)\ln \frac{p_x(t) }{ p_y(t) }lnpy​(t)px​(t)​ is know as privacy loss. Substituting px(t)p_x(t)px​(t) and py(t)p_y(t)py​(t) with their definitions, and letting t′=t−f(x)t' = t - f(x)t′=t−f(x) and v=f(x)−f(y)v = f(x) - f(y)v=f(x)−f(y), we get

ln⁡px(t)py(t)=ln⁡12πσ2exp⁡(−∥t−f(x)∥22σ2)12πσ2exp⁡(−∥t−f(y)∥22σ2)=−∥t′∥2−∥t′+v∥22σ2=2vTt′+∥v∥22σ2.\begin{aligned} \ln \frac{ p_x( t) }{ p_y(t)} &= \ln \frac{ \frac{1}{ \sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2} } \exp \Big( -\frac{ \Vert t - f(x)\Vert ^2 }{ 2 \sigma^2 } \Big) }{ \frac{1}{ \sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2} } \exp \Big( -\frac{ \Vert t - f(y) \Vert ^2 }{ 2 \sigma^2 } \Big) } \\ &= -\frac{ \Vert t' \Vert ^2 - \Vert t' + v \Vert ^2 }{ 2 \sigma^2 } \\ &= \frac{ 2 v^T t' + \Vert v \Vert ^2 }{ 2 \sigma^2 } . \end{aligned} lnpy​(t)px​(t)​​=ln2πσ2​1​exp(−2σ2∥t−f(y)∥2​)2πσ2​1​exp(−2σ2∥t−f(x)∥2​)​=−2σ2∥t′∥2−∥t′+v∥2​=2σ22vTt′+∥v∥2​.​

Now,

∥v∥22σ2+∥v∥σZ≥ϵ⟷Z≥σ∥v∥ϵ−∥v∥2σ \frac{ \Vert v\Vert ^2 }{ 2 \sigma^2 } + \frac{ \Vert v\Vert }{ \sigma } Z \ge \epsilon \\ \longleftrightarrow Z \ge \frac{ \sigma }{ \Vert v\Vert } \epsilon - \frac{ \Vert v\Vert }{ 2 \sigma } 2σ2∥v∥2​+σ∥v∥​Z≥ϵ⟷Z≥∥v∥σ​ϵ−2σ∥v∥​
