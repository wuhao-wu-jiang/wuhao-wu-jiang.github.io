Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Posted on 2021-07-18 Edited on 2021-07-25

Let \(\Omega \subseteq \mathcal{C}\) be a open set and \(f : \Omega \rightarrow \mathcal{C}\) be a complex function. \(f\) is holomorphic at \(z \in \Omega\), if the following limit exits: \[ \begin{array}{lr} \lim_{z + h \in \Omega, h \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + h) - f(z) }{ h }. & (1) \end{array} \]

The limit is denoted as \(f'(z)\) and is called the derivative of \(f\) at \(z\). Note that \(h\) can approach \(0\) from any direction.

Define \[ { \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} (z) \doteq \lim_{x \in \mathbb{R}, z + x \in \Omega, x \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + x) - f(z) }{ x }, } \] which is the limit of \((1)\) when \(h\) approaches to \(0\) from the real axis.

Similarly, define when limit when \(h\) is purely imaginary: \[ \small \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y} (z) \doteq \lim_{y \in \mathbb{R}, z + y \in \Omega, y \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + i \cdot y) - f(z) }{ i \cdot y }. \]

If the derivate of \(f\) exists at \(z\), we have shown that \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y}. &\qquad (2) \end{aligned} \] Writing \(f = u + iv\), we get \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} &= \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x} + i \cdot \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x}, \\ \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y} &= \frac{1}{i} \cdot \left( \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y} + i \cdot \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y} \right) = -i \cdot \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y} + \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}. \end{aligned} \]

Equation (2) implies that \[ \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}, \qquad \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x} = - \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}. \]

These are the Cauchy-Riemann equations. The next theorem states that the converse is also true, i.e., when the equations are satisfies at some point \(z\), then the derivative of \(f = u + iv\) exists.

Theorem. Let \(u, v : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) and \(f = u + iv\). If \(u\) and \(v\) are continuously differentiable at \(z \in \Omega\) and satisfy Cauchy-Riemann equations, then the derivative of \(f\) exits.

Proof. Since \(u\) and \(v\) are continuously differentiable at \(z = (x, y)\), for small \(\Delta z = (\Delta x, \Delta y) \in \mathbb{R}^2\), \[ \begin{aligned} u( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) &= \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \Delta x + \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \Delta y + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Phi_u( \Delta z), \\ v( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) &= \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \Delta x + \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \Delta y + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Phi_v( \Delta z) \end{aligned} \]

where \(\Phi_u( \Delta z) \rightarrow 0\) and \(\Phi_v( \Delta z) \rightarrow 0\) as \(\Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \rightarrow 0\).

Therefore, \[ \small \begin{aligned} f( z + \Delta z ) - f( z ) &= u( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) + i \Big( v( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) \Big) \\ &= \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \big( \Delta x + i \Delta y \big) + \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \big( \Delta y - i \Delta x \big) + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Big( \Phi_u( \Delta z) + \Phi_v( \Delta z) \Big) \\ &= \Big( \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) - i \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \Big) \cdot \big( \Delta x + i \Delta y \big) + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Big( \Phi_u( \Delta z) + \Phi_v( \Delta z) \Big), \end{aligned} \]

which finishes the proof.

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] E. M. Stein and R. Shakarchi, Complex analysis. Princeton, N.J: Princeton University Press, 2003.
