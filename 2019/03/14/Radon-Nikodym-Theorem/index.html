Radon Nikodym Theorem

Posted on 2019-03-14 Edited on 2022-01-26

We consider a measure space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)\), where \(\Omega\) is a set of elements, \(\mathcal{F}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field, and \(\mu : \mathcal{F} \rightarrow R\) a \(\sigma\)-finite measure defined on \(\mathcal{F}\).

Definition (Mutually Singular). Measures \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) on a measurable space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) are by definition mutually singular if they have disjoint supports, i.e., if \[ \exists S_\mu, S_\nu \in \mathcal{F},\, \text{ s.t. }\, \begin{cases} S_\mu \cap S_\nu = \emptyset,\, \\ \mu(\bar S_\mu) = \nu(\bar S_\nu) = 0. \end{cases} \]

Definition (Absolute Continuous). Measure \(\nu\) is absolute continuous with \(\mu\) (or dominated by \(\mu\)), denoted as \(\nu \ll \mu\), if
\[ \mu(E) = 0 \implies \nu(E) = 0, \qquad \forall E \in \mathcal{F}. \]

Motivation. Let \(f\) be a nonnegative and measurable function. Then the measure defined by \[ \nu(E) = \int_E f \, d \mu, \qquad \forall E \in \mathcal{F}, \] is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\mu\). The The Radon-Nikodym Theorem establishes the opposite direction.

The Radon-Nikodym Theorem.
Let \(\mu, \nu\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measures such that \(\nu \ll \mu\). There exists a nonnegative measurable function \(f\), a density, such that
\[ \nu(E) = \int_E f \, d \mu, \qquad \forall E \in \mathcal{F}. \] For two such function \(f\) and \(f'\), \(\mu[ f \neq f' ] = 0\). \(f\) is called the Radon-Nikodym derivative of \(\nu\) with respect to \(\mu\) and is denoted \(d \mu / d \nu\).

The proof of the Theorem relies on the following lemma, the proof of which is deferred to the end of the article.

Lemma. Let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be two finite measures that are not mutually singular. Then there exits \(A \in \mathcal{F}\) and \(\epsilon > 0\), such that \(\mu(A) > 0\), and for each measurable set \(E\) in \(A\), it holds that \(\epsilon \cdot \mu(E) \le \nu(E)\).

Proof of the Theorem:

Since both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite, there is a disjoint sequence \(E_n \in \mathcal{F}\) such that \(\cup_{n \ge 1} E_n = \Omega\) and that \(\mu(E_n) < \infty\), \(\nu(E_n) < \infty\) for each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\).

Define finite measures \(\mu_n\) and \(\nu_n\) by \[ \mu_n (E) = \mu(E \cap E_n),\quad \nu_n (E) = \nu(E \cap E_n),\quad \forall E \in \mathcal{F}. \]

Suppose that we can find densities \(f_n, n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), such that \(\nu_n(E) = \int_E f_n \, d \mu_n\). As \(\mu_n\) has density \(\mathbf{1}_{E_n}\), for each \(E \in \mathcal{F}\), it holds that \(\int_E f_n \, d \mu_n = \int_E f_n \cdot \mathbf{1}_{E_n} \, d \mu\). Via monotone convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \mu(E) &= \sum_{n \ge 1} \mu_n(E) = \sum_{n \ge 1} \int_E f_n \, d \mu_n \\ &= \sum_{n \ge 1} \int_E f_n \cdot \mathbf{1}_{E_n} \, d \mu = \int_E \sum_{n \ge 1} f_n \cdot \mathbf{1}_{E_n} \, d \mu. \end{aligned} \]

WLOG, we can assume that both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are finite.

Define the collection of functions
\[ \mathcal{G} = \left\{ g \text{ is nonnegative, measurable and } \int_E g \, d \mu \le \nu(E), \quad \forall E \in \mathcal{F} \right\}. \]

Note that \(\mathcal{G}\) is non-empty, since at least \(g \equiv 0\) satisfies the conditions and belongs to \(\mathcal{G}\). Moreover,

  • \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under finite maxima: let \(g_1, g_2 \in \mathcal{G}\), then for each \(E \in \mathcal{F}\), \[ \begin{aligned} \int_E \max (g_1, g_2) \, d \mu &= \int_{E \cap [g_1 \ge g_2]} g_1 \, d \mu + \int_{E \cap [g_1 < g_2]} g_2 \, d \mu \\ &\le \nu \big( E \cap [g_1 \ge g_2] \big) + \nu \big( E \cap [g_1 < g_2] \big) = \nu (E). \end{aligned} \]

  • \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under non-decreasing limit: let \(g_n \in \mathcal{G}\) such that \(g_n \uparrow g\), then by monotone convergence theorem it holds for each \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) that \[ \lim_n \int_E g_n \, d \mu = \int_E \lim_n g_n \, d \mu \le \nu(E). \]

Next, consider the supremum of integrals of function in \(\mathcal{G}\):
\[ \alpha \doteq \sup_{g \in \mathcal{G} } \int g \ d \mu \le \nu(\Omega) < \infty. \]

Let \(g_n \in \mathcal{G}, n \in \mathbb{N}^+\) be a sequence of functions such that $ _n g_n  d = . $

For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), define \(f_n = \max_{i \in [n]} g_i\). Then the \(f_n\) is a non-decreasing sequence of functions in \(\mathcal{G}\). Denote \(f = \lim_n f_n\). We have \(f \in \mathcal{G}\), and \[ \int f \, d \mu = \lim_n \int g_n \ d \mu = \alpha. \]

It is left to show that \(\lambda(E) = \int_E f\) for each measurable set \(E\). Define function \(\lambda : \mathcal{F} \rightarrow R\) as

\[ \lambda(E) = \nu(E) - \int_E f \ d \mu \]

We complete the proof by showing that \(\lambda(E) \equiv 0\).

It is easy to verify that \(\lambda\) is a measure (\(\lambda \ge 0\)) as \(\nu(E) \ge \int_E f d \mu\). Further, \(\int_E f \ d \mu \ll \mu\) and \(\nu \ll \mu\) imply that \(\lambda \ll \mu\).

Suppose \(\lambda \ne 0\). By assumption \(\mu(\Omega) < \infty\), then there exists \(\epsilon > 0\), such that \(\lambda(\Omega) - \epsilon \mu(\Omega) > 0\). Let \(\{ A, B\}\) be the Hahn decomposition of \(\lambda - \epsilon \mu\) such that \(A\) is positive. Let \(g = f + \epsilon \chi_{A}\). It satisfies

  1. \(g \in \mathcal{G}\), since \(\forall E \in \mathcal{F}\),
    \[ \lambda(E) - \int_E g \ d \mu = \lambda(E) - \int_E f \ d \mu - \int_E \epsilon \chi_{A} \ d \mu \ge 0 \]

  2. \(\int g \ d \mu \ge \alpha\),
    \[ \int g \ d \mu = \int f + \epsilon \chi_{A} \ d \mu = \int f \ d \mu + \int \epsilon \chi_A \ d \mu = \alpha + \epsilon \mu(A) \ge \alpha \]

If follows that \(\mu(A) = 0\). Now \(0 < (\lambda - \epsilon \mu)(\Omega) = (\lambda - \epsilon \mu)(A) + (\lambda - \epsilon \mu)(B) = 0 + (\lambda - \epsilon \mu)(B) \le 0\). Contradiction. We thus have proved that \(\lambda = 0\).

\(\square\)

Proof of the Lemma.

For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(\nu - (1 / n) \cdot \mu\) is a signed measure. Let \(S_n^+, S_n^-\) be its Hahn decomposition. Define \(S^+ = \cup_{n \ge 1} S_n^+\), and \(S^- = \cap_{n \ge 1} S_n^-\). Since \(S^- \subset S_n^-\), we have \[ \nu(S^-) \le \frac{1}{n} \cdot \mu(S^-), \qquad \forall n \in \mathbb{N}^+. \] It follows that \(\nu(S^-) = 0\). Since \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are not mutually singular, \(S^-\) does not support \(\mu\). Hence \(\mu(S^+) > 0\). There exits some \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), such that \(\mu(S_n^+) > 0\). But \[ \nu(E) \ge \frac{1}{n} \cdot \mu(E), \qquad \forall E \subset S_n^+, E \in \mathcal{F}. \]

\(\square\)
