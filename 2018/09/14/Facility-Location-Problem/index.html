Facility Location Problem

Posted on 2018-09-14 Edited on 2020-05-13

Update on May/7th/2020.

Suppose we have a set of facilities FFF and a set of clients CCC. We would like to open some facilities from $F $ to serve the clients. The cost consists of two parts: 1) the one for opening the selected facilities; 2) the one for connecting each client to its closest facility.

Formally, denote fif_ifi​ the cost of facility i∈Fi \in Fi∈F and ci,jc_{i,j}ci,j​ the distance from a client j∈Cj \in Cj∈C to a facility i∈Fi \in Fi∈F. Let S⊂FS \subset FS⊂F be a subset of facilities, and let μ:C→S\mu : C \rightarrow Sμ:C→S be the corresponding function that assigns each client j∈Cj \in Cj∈C to a facility in SSS, i.e.,

μ(j)≐arg⁡min⁡i∈Sci,j\mu(j) \doteq \arg\min_{i \in S} c_{i,j} μ(j)≐argi∈Smin​ci,j​

The cost of SSS is given by

∑i∈Sfi+∑j∈Ccμ(j),j\sum_{i \in S} f_i + \sum_{j \in C} c_{\mu(j), j} i∈S∑​fi​+j∈C∑​cμ(j),j​

The problem is to find a set SSS that minimizes this cost.

LP Formulation

It can be formulated as linear program and then relaxed as as a linear one:

Primal:min⁡∑i∈F,j∈Cci,jxi,j+∑i∈Ffiyis.t.,∑i∈Fxij≥1∀j∈C(1)yi−xi,j≥0∀i∈F,j∈C(2)\begin{array}{lrllr} \text{Primal:} &&\min & \sum_{i \in F, j \in C} c_{i, j} x_{i, j} + \sum_{i \in F} f_i y_i & & \\ &&s.t., & \sum_{i \in F} x_{i j} \ge 1 & \forall j \in C & (1) \\ && & y_i - x_{i,j} \ge 0 & \forall i \in F, j \in C & (2) \end{array} Primal:​​mins.t.,​∑i∈F,j∈C​ci,j​xi,j​+∑i∈F​fi​yi​∑i∈F​xij​≥1yi​−xi,j​≥0​∀j∈C∀i∈F,j∈C​(1)(2)​

The first constraint says that each client is assigned to at least one facility. The second one can be interpreted as that a facility must be open if there is a client assigned to it.

To obtain the dual, let αj\alpha_jαj​ be the dual variable associated with constraint (1) and βi,j\beta_{i,j}βi,j​ be the one with constraint (2). We need to ensure that weak duality holds.

Weak Duality

The dual can be formulated such that the above inequalities hold.

Dual Program

Dual:max⁡∑j∈Cαjs.t.,αj−βi,j≤ci,j∀i∈F,∀j∈C∑j∈Cβi,j≤fi∀i∈F\begin{array}{lllr} \text{Dual:} \qquad &\max & \sum_{j \in C} \alpha_j \\ &s.t.,& \alpha_j - \beta_{i,j} \le c_{i,j} & \forall i \in F, \forall j \in C\\ & & \sum_{j \in C} \beta_{i,j} \le f_i & \forall i \in F\\ \end{array} Dual:​maxs.t.,​∑j∈C​αj​αj​−βi,j​≤ci,j​∑j∈C​βi,j​≤fi​​∀i∈F,∀j∈C∀i∈F​

In this case, each client is assigned to its nearest facility. However we ignore facility cost entirely.

To incorporate the facility cost, for each facility iii we divide its cost fif_ifi​ among the clients, such that client jjj needs to pay βi,j\beta_{i, j}βi,j​ if it is assigned facility iii. Note that βi,j\beta_{i, j}βi,j​ can be arbitrary non-negative number as long as it satisfies that ∑j∈Cβi,j=fi\sum_{j \in C} \beta_{i,j} = f_i∑j∈C​βi,j​=fi​ for all i∈Fi \in Fi∈F.

Let x∗,y∗x^*, y^*x∗,y∗ and α∗,β∗\alpha^*, \beta^*α∗,β∗ be the optimal primal and dual solution respectively. For these set of variables, weak duality holds with equalities. By complementary slackness, it holds that

Deterministic Rounding [1]

As a warm up, we introduce the first constant factor approximation algorithm for the un-capacitated facility location problem, proposed in 1997 [1]. It is a deterministic rounding algorithm.

Algorithm 1:

  1. S←∅S \leftarrow \emptysetS←∅.
  2. D←∅D \leftarrow \emptysetD←∅.
  3. While there exists some client not assigned to any facility
  4. \quad Let jjj be the unassigned one with smallest αj∗\alpha^*_jαj∗​, add it to DDD.
  5. \quad Open the cheapest facility iii in N(j)N(j)N(j), add it to SSS.
  6. \quad Assign jjj to iii: μ(j)←i\mu(j) \leftarrow iμ(j)←i
  7. \quad For each un-assigned client j′∈N2(j)j' \in N^2(j)j′∈N2(j) do
  8. \quad \quad \quad Assign j′j'j′ to iii: μ(j′)←i\mu(j') \leftarrow iμ(j′)←i

The example above shows one iteration of the algorithm. The vertex with minimum value of αj∗\alpha^*_jαj∗​ (i.e. 1) is opened, highlighted by the red cycle. There are two facilities connected to it, the cheap opening cost of which is 1. Then all un-assigned clients neighboring these two facilities are assigned to the cost 1 facility, as shown by the purple dashed arrows.

Intuitively, each iteration of the algorithm creates a clusters, which contains three kinds of vertices:

  1. The selected un-assigned client jjj with minimum αj∗\alpha^*_jαj∗​.
  2. All jjj's neighboring facilities.
  3. All un-assigned clients neighboring to these facilities.

It is left to analyze the cost of the solution returned.

Facility Cost

At each iteration, a client jjj is selected. In the primal LP, constraint (1) requires that

∑i∈Fxi,j∗≥1\sum_{i \in F} x^*_{i,j} \ge 1 i∈F∑​xi,j∗​≥1

Note that although jjj can assigned to a client fractionally, the total amount of assignment must be at least 1. Further, although a facility can be fractionally open in primal LP, the constraint (2)

implies that the open amount must be at least the amount a client assign to it. By the selection of μ(j)\mu(j)μ(j), we conclude that

Connection Cost

Now we analyze the assignment cost. When a client jjj with smallest αj∗\alpha^*_jαj∗​ is selected and assigned to the cheapest facility iii in N(j)N(j)N(j). Unassigned clients j′∈N2(j)j' \in N^2(j)j′∈N2(j) are also assigned to iii. There are two possible cases:

Combing assignment cost and facility cost, algorithm 1 is 4-approximation.

How happy are we with the 4-approximation algorithm? Probably not. In the previous example, when a facility is opened, only one client contributes to its opening cost. However, in the primal solution, many other clients assigned to it do not contribute to the opening cost, which implies that the current analysis may not be tight.

Randomized Rounding [3]

3-Approximation Rounding

In the section, we improve the approximation ratio of 4 to an expected value of 3. In the previous rounding, we bound the opening cost as

∑j∈Dmin⁡i∈N(j)fi≤OPT\sum_{j \in D} \min_{i \in N(j) } f_i \le OPT j∈D∑​i∈N(j)min​fi​≤OPT

  1. We pick a client that minimize αj∗+Aj\alpha^*_j + A_jαj∗​+Aj​;
  2. We pick a facility with probability xi,j∗x^*_{i,j}xi,j∗​.

(1+3/e)(1 + 3/e)(1+3/e)-Approximation Rounding [3]
