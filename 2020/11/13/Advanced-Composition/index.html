Advanced Composition

Posted on 2020-11-13 Edited on 2020-11-15

Once we have designed some basic differentially private algorithms, it is a natural idea to combine them and analysis the privacy loss. We begin with an illustrative example that sets up the mathematical model step by step.

Image yourself in front of the door of a safe vault protected by a password lock. To open the door, you need the correct password PPP. If tried with the wrong password, the lock would destroy itself automatically.

Luckily, you know two candidate passwords P1P_1P1​ and P2P_2P2​, with one of them being the correct one. Further, you notice that the designer of the lock left a collection of boxes near the door, which contain information on how they decide the correct passwords. Obtaining complete information of anyone of them gives you the correct password.

But the boxes are also protected and you don’t have legally access to them. However, you can hack into the boxes. Hacking into the box won’t give you all its information, but a random message. In particular, each box BBB is associated with a set RB\mathcal{R}_BRB​, which is a finite collection of messages (in English). When BBB is hacked, it returns a message YBY_BYB​ generated randomly from RB\mathcal{R}_BRB​, whose distribution depends on the correct password PPP.

Let DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ be the distribution of YBY_BYB​ if the correct password is P1P_1P1​, and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​ be the one if the correct password is P2P_2P2​. If there is a huge difference between DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​, then you might be able to guess the correct password.

E.g., suppose RB=\mathcal{R}_B =RB​= { “Dog bites.”, “Cat scratches.” } and the distributions are given as

DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​
“Dog bites.” 0.99 0.01
“Cat scratches.” 0.01 0.99

Hence, when you get YB=Y_B=YB​= “Dog bites.”, you prefer P1P_1P1​ over P2P_2P2​ and vice versa.

Anticipating such potential information leakage, the designer of the lock equips the boxes with a defense mechanism, called (ϵ,0)(\epsilon, 0)(ϵ,0)-mechanism. It guarantees the distributions DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​ are similar so that it is hard for you to infer the correct password from the message. In particular, ∀S⊂RB\forall S \subset \mathcal{R}_B∀S⊂RB​, if SSS is measurable, it holds that

Pr⁡[YB∈S ∣ P=P1]≤eϵ⋅Pr⁡[YB∈S ∣ P=P2]Pr⁡[YB∈S ∣ P=P2]≤eϵ⋅Pr⁡[YB∈S ∣ P=P1]\Pr[ Y_B \in S \ | \ P = P_1 ] \le e^\epsilon \cdot \Pr[ Y_B \in S \ | \ P = P_2 ] \\ \Pr[ Y_B \in S \ | \ P = P_2 ] \le e^\epsilon \cdot \Pr[ Y_B \in S \ | \ P = P_1 ] \\ Pr[YB​∈S ∣ P=P1​]≤eϵ⋅Pr[YB​∈S ∣ P=P2​]Pr[YB​∈S ∣ P=P2​]≤eϵ⋅Pr[YB​∈S ∣ P=P1​]

When these inequalities are satisfied, we say that DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​ are (ϵ,0)(\epsilon, 0)(ϵ,0) close. The inequalities require DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​ to have the same support on RB\mathcal{R}_BRB​. Therefore, throughout our discussion below, we assume that both DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ and DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​ assign positive probability to each element in RB\mathcal{R}_BRB​. Otherwise, we can just replace RB\mathcal{R}_BRB​ with the support of DB,P1\mathcal{D}_{B, P_1}DB,P1​​ (which is also the support of DB,P2\mathcal{D}_{B, P_2}DB,P2​​).

Now, hacking one box is unlikely to help you to guess the correct password. You want to hack more boxes, with the hope that the information combined will assist you. Due to resource limit, you can’t hack all boxes but only a finite number of them. You choose the first box randomly. Then you choose every new box based on the information obtained from the hacked boxes. The figure below shows an example of hacking five boxes.

Suppose that your resource enables you to hack nnn boxes and let Y⃗n=(Y1,Y2,...,Yn)\vec Y_n = (Y_1, Y_2, ..., Y_n)Yn​=(Y1​,Y2​,...,Yn​) be the output messages you obtained. Similar to the situation of hacking one box, if the distribution of Y⃗n\vec Y_nYn​, conditioned on P=P1P = P_1P=P1​, is utterly distant from that conditioned on P=P2P = P_2P=P2​, then there could be some cases when you can confidently infer the true password. Conversely, to prevent severe information leakage, the designer needs to ensure there isn’t such case, i.e., the two distributions should be similar.

What makes things even more complicated is that, the distribution of Y⃗n\vec Y_nYn​ depends not only on the output distributions of the boxes, but also on your strategy of choosing the boxes to hack. Let’s use symbol AAA to denote your strategy. Whatever AAA is, the designer need to guarantee that the distribution of Y⃗n\vec Y_nYn​ conditioned P=P1P = P_1P=P1​ should be similar to that conditioned on P=P2P = P_2P=P2​.

Surprisingly, this is in some sense achievable, as long as for each box, its output distribution conditioned on P=P1P = P_1P=P1​ is (ϵ,0)(\epsilon, 0)(ϵ,0) close to that conditioned on P=P2P = P_2P=P2​.

Let R\mathcal{R}R be the set of possible possible messages of all boxes.

Theorem. ∀A\forall A∀A, ∀S⊂Rn\forall S \subset \mathcal{R}^n∀S⊂Rn, it holds that ∀δ′∈(0,1)\forall \delta' \in (0, 1)∀δ′∈(0,1),
