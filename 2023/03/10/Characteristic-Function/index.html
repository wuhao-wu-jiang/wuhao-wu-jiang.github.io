Characteristic Function

Posted on 2023-03-10

Definition

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\) be a probability space, and \(X\) be a random variable on the space. Further, denote \(\mu\) the distribution of \(X\), s.t., \[ \begin{aligned} &\mu(A) = \mathbb{P} \Big( X^{-1}(A) \Big), &\forall A \in \mathcal{B}, \end{aligned} \] where \(\mathcal{B}\) is the collection of Borel set on \(\mathbb{R}\).

Definition. The characteristic function r.w.t. \(\mu\) is given by \[ \varphi(t) = \int_\mathbb{R} e^{itx} \, \mu (dx) . \]

Theorem. \[ \varphi(t) = \mathbb{E} \left[ e^{itX} \right]. \]

Proof. Recall that, by definition of expectation, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \left[ e^{itX} \right] &\doteq \int_\Omega e^{it X(\omega) } \, \mathbb{P} (d \omega) \\ &\doteq \int_\Omega \Bigg( \sin \big( t X(\omega) \big) + i \cdot \cos \big( t X(\omega) \big) \Bigg) \, \mathbb{P} (d \omega) \\ &\stackrel{(a)}{=} \int_\mathbb{R} \sin \big( t x \big) \, \mu (dx) + i \cdot \int_\mathbb{R} \cos \big( t x \big) \, \mu (d x) \\ &= \int_\mathbb{R} e^{itx} \, \mu (dx) \\ &= \varphi(t), \end{aligned} \] where in Equation \((a)\), we have applied the fact that for each function \(f\) that is measurable \(\mathcal{B} / \mathcal{B}\), \[ \int_\Omega f(X(\omega)) \, \mathbb{P}(d\omega) = \int_\mathbb{R} f(x) \, \mu(dx). \] \(\square\)

Independence

Theorem. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent random variables. Then \[ \varphi(X + Y) = \varphi(X) \varphi(Y). \]

Proof. Since \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, the following pairs of random variables are independent

  1. \(\sin \big( t X(\omega) \big)\), \(\sin \big( t Y(\omega) \big)\);
  2. \(\cos \big( t X(\omega) \big)\), \(\cos \big( t Y(\omega) \big)\);
  3. \(\sin \big( t X(\omega) \big)\), \(\cos \big( t Y(\omega) \big)\);
  4. \(\cos \big( t X(\omega) \big)\), \(\sin \big( t Y(\omega) \big)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi(X + Y) &= \mathbb{E} \left[ e^{it(X + Y)} \right] \\ &= \mathbb{E} \left[ \Bigg( \sin \big( t X(\omega) \big) + i \cdot \cos \big( t X(\omega) \big) \Bigg) \Bigg( \sin \big( t Y(\omega) \big) + i \cdot \cos \big( t Y(\omega) \big) \Bigg) \right] \\ &= \mathbb{E} \left[ \sin \big( t X(\omega) \big) \right] \mathbb{E} \left[ \sin \big( t Y(\omega) \big) \right] - \mathbb{E} \left[ \cos \big( t X(\omega) \big) \right] \mathbb{E} \left[ \cos \big( t Y(\omega) \big) \right] \\ &\qquad + i \mathbb{E} \left[ \sin \big( t X(\omega) \big) \right] \mathbb{E} \left[ \cos \big( t Y(\omega) \big) \right] + i \mathbb{E} \left[ \cos \big( t X(\omega) \big) \right] \mathbb{E} \left[ \sin \big( t Y(\omega) \big) \right] \\ &= \varphi(X) \varphi(Y). \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Uniqueness

Theorem. Let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be probability measures on \(\mathbb{R}\). If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) has the same characteristic functions, then \[ \begin{aligned} &\mu(A) = \nu(A), &\forall A \in \mathcal{B}. \end{aligned} \]

The proof follows from the following lemma: > Lemma. Let \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\), s.t. \(a < b\). > If \(\mu(\{a\}) = \mu(\{b\}) = 0\), then > \[ \mu \Big( (a, b] \Big) = \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-T}^T \frac{e^{-ita} - e^{-itb}}{it} \varphi(t) \, d t. > \]

Proof of the Theorem. For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}_+\), define \(E_n \doteq \{ x \in \mathbb{R} : \mu( \{ x \} ) \ge 1 / n \}\). It is easy to see that \(|E_n| \le n\). It follows that the \(E \doteq \cup_{n \in \mathbb{N}_+} E_n\) is countable.

Consider the set \(S = \{ (a, b] : a < b, \mu(\{a\}) = \mu(\{b\}) = 0 \}\). Obviously this is a \(\pi\) system. Further, it generates \(\mathcal{B}\). To see this, for each point \(c \in E\), we can find

  1. a sequence \(a_1, a_2, \ldots\) from \(\bar{E}\) that increases to \(c\),
  2. a sequence \(b_1, b_2, \ldots\) from \(\bar{E}\) that decreases to \(c\).

Then \((a_i, b_i] \in S\), and \(\cap_{i \ge 1} (a_i, b_i] = [c, c]\).

It follows that every interval \((a, b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) can be generated by intervals in \(S\).

According to the lemma, \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) agrees on a \(\pi\) system that generates \(\mathcal{B}\). By \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, they agree on each set in \(\mathcal{B}\).

\(\square\)

Proof of the Lemma. We are going to prove that, for each \(a < b\), \[ \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-T}^T \frac{e^{-ita} - e^{-itb}}{it} \varphi(t) \, d t = \mu(a, b) + \mu \big( \{a, b \} \big). \] Define \[ \begin{aligned} I_T &\doteq \int_{-T}^T \frac{e^{-ita} - e^{-itb}}{it} \varphi(t) \, d t \\ &= \int_{-T}^T \int_\mathbb{R} \frac{e^{-it(a - x)} - e^{-it(b - x)}}{it} \,\mu(dx) \, d t \\ &\stackrel{(a)}{=} \int_\mathbb{R} \int_{-T}^T \frac{e^{-it(a - x)} - e^{-it(b - x)}}{it} \, d t \, \mu(dx) \\ &\stackrel{(b)}{=} \int_\mathbb{R} \int_{-T}^T \left[ \frac{\sin \big( (x - a) t \big) }{t} - \frac{\sin \big( (x - b) t \big) }{t} \right] \, d t \, \mu(dx) \\ &\stackrel{(c)}{=} 2 \int_\mathbb{R} \int_{0}^T \left[ \frac{\sin \big( (x - a) t \big) }{t} - \frac{\sin \big( (x - b) t \big) }{t} \right] \, d t \, \mu(dx) \end{aligned} \] where

  • Equation \((a)\) holds: since \(e^{-i x} = 1 + \int_0^x e^{-is} \, d s\), \[ \begin{aligned} \left| \frac{e^{-it(a - x)} - e^{-it(b - x)}}{it} \right| = \left| \int_{(b - x)}^{(a - x)} e^{-is} \, d s \right| \le |b - a| \end{aligned} \] is therefore bounded. Hence, by Fubini's theorem, we can exchange the order of the double integral over finite product measure.

  • Equation \((b)\) holds: since \(\cos \big( (x - a) t \big) / (it)\) and \(\cos \big( (x - b) t \big) / (it)\) are odd functions over \([-T, T]\)

  • Equation \((c)\) holds: since \(\sin \big( (x - a) t \big) / (it)\) and \(\sin \big( (x - b) t \big) / (it)\) are even functions over \([-T, T]\)

Next, define \(S(T) \doteq \int_0^T \tfrac{\sin x}{x} \, dx\). Introducing the function \[ \mathsf{sgn}(\theta) = \begin{cases} 1, &\text{ if }\, \theta > 0, \\ 0, &\text{ if }\, \theta = 0, \\ -1, &\text{ if }\, \theta < 0, \end{cases} \] we can write \[ R(\theta, T) \doteq \int_0^T \frac{\sin \theta x}{x} \, dx = \mathsf{sgn}(\theta) \cdot S(|\theta| T). \]

Since \({T \rightarrow \infty}\), \(S(T) \rightarrow \pi / 2\), we have \(R(\theta, T) \rightarrow \mathsf{sgn}(\theta) \cdot \pi / 2\). Hence, \[ \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \Big\{ R(x - a, T) - R(x - b, T) \Big\} = \begin{cases} 0, & x < a, \\ \pi / 2, & x = a, \\ \pi, & a < x < b, \\ \pi / 2, & x = b, \\ 0, & x > b. \end{cases} \]

Finally, \(|R(\theta, T)| = \Big| S \big( |\theta| T \big) \Big| \le \sup_t S(t) < \infty\), by bounded convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} I_T &= 2 \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \int_\mathbb{R} \int_{0}^T \left[ \frac{\sin \big( (x - a) t \big) }{t} - \frac{\sin \big( (x - b) t \big) }{t} \right] \, d t \, \mu(dx) \\ &= 2 \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \int_\mathbb{R} \Big\{ R(x - a, T) - R(x - b, T) \Big\} \, \mu(dx) \\ &= 2 \int_\mathbb{R} \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \Big\{ R(x - a, T) - R(x - b, T) \Big\} \, \mu(dx) \\ &= 2 \pi \cdot \mu (a, b) + \pi \mu (\{a, b\}), \end{aligned} \] which concludes the proof.

Remark. Here, let \(f_T(x) \doteq R(x - a, T) - R(x - b, T)\), and \(f(x) \doteq \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} f_T(x)\). Since \(f_T(x)\) is bounded, assume that \(|f_T(x)| \le B, \forall x, T\).

Let \(\epsilon > 0\). For each \(T \in \mathbb{R}^+\), define \(E_T \doteq \{ x \in \mathbb{R} : | f_T(x) - f(x) | \le \epsilon \}\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \left| \int_\mathbb{R} f_T(x) - f(x) \, \mu (dx) \right| &\le \int_\mathbb{R} \left| f_T(x) - f(x) \right|\, \mu (dx) \\ &= \int_{E_T} \left| f_T(x) - f(x) \right|\, \mu (dx) + \int_{\overline{E_T}} \left| f_T(x) - f(x) \right| \, \mu (dx) \\ &\le \epsilon + 2B \cdot \mu \Big( \overline{E_T} \Big). \end{aligned} \]

Finally, for \(x \notin (a - \epsilon, a + \epsilon) \cup (b - \epsilon, b + \epsilon)\), we have \(|x - a| T, |x - b| T \ge \epsilon T\). Hence, \[ |f_T(x) - f(x)| \le 2 \cdot \left| S(\epsilon T) - S(\infty)\right|, \] which approaches \(0\) as \(T \rightarrow \infty\). So \[ \begin{aligned} \left| \int_\mathbb{R} f_T(x) - f(x) \, \mu (dx) \right| \rightarrow (1 + 2B) \cdot \epsilon. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

[2] R. Durrett, “Probability : theory and examples,” p. 432.
