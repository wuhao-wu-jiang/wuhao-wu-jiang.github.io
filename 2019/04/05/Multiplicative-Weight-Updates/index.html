Multiplicative Weight Updates

Posted on 2019-04-05 Edited on 2020-12-14

Consider a problem that might arises from many application scenarios. We have a sequence of TTT binary variables indexed by time X1,X2,...,XTX_1, X_2, ..., X_TX1​,X2​,...,XT​, where Xt∈{0,1},∀t∈[T]X_t \in \{0, 1\}, \forall t \in [T]Xt​∈{0,1},∀t∈[T]. We don’t make decision on our own. Instead, we resort a group of nnn experts. At each time ttt, each expert iii gives a prediction pitp_i^tpit​ of XtX_tXt​. Based on the experts’ prediction, we make prediction our prediction pAtp_A^tpAt​. The value of XtX_tXt​ is then revealed and we make a mistake if our prediction is wrong. The goal to minimize the number of mistakes.

Multiplicative Weight Updates

We are in good shape so far. Let’s extend the problem to more general settings. In this cases, the experts interact with the environment in round-robin fashion. At each round ttt,

  1. The expert iii performs action, termed aita_i^tait​.
  2. The environment returns the loss of performing aita_i^tait​, denoted as lit∈[0,1]l_i^t \in [0, 1]lit​∈[0,1].

Although there is no restriction of the experts’ actions and their losses, we can come up with some strategy the expected loss of which is almost as good as the best expert:

min⁡i∈[n]Li+2Tln⁡n, \min_{i \in [n] } L_i + 2 \sqrt{ T \ln n }, i∈[n]min​Li​+2Tlnn​,

where Li≐∑t∈[T]litL_i \doteq \sum_{t \in [T] } l_i^tLi​≐∑t∈[T]​lit​ is the loss of expert iii. This implies that the average expected loss converges at a rate of 1T\sqrt \frac{1}{T}T1​​ to the best one.
