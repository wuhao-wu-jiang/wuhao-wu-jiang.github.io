Minimax Theorem

Posted on 2019-10-28 Edited on 2021-07-14

A zero-sum game is a game in which a player’s payoff implies the loss of another player.
There is a payoff a matrix A∈Rn×mA \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}A∈Rn×m.
One player chooses from rows {1,2,...,n}\{1, 2, ..., n\}{1,2,...,n} and the other chooses from columns {1,2,...,m}\{1, 2, ..., m\}{1,2,...,m}.
The former is called the row player and the later is called the column player.
The entry aija_{ij}aij​ of AAA is the payoff when the row player chooses iii and the column player chooses jjj. In such case the column player loses aija_{ij}aij​.

If one player makes a choice first and the other play follows, then the second player can always respond according the first player choice, to maximize its payoff or minimize its loss.

To make the game more fair, suppose that there are two huge black boxes.
We require the row player to go into one black box, and write down her choice iii on a note, and then leave the black box.
We require the column player to do the same in another black box.
Suppose the row player chooses his row index according to some distribution x∈Rnx \in \mathbb{R}^nx∈Rn, and the column player chooses according to distribution y∈Rmy \in \mathbb{R}^my∈Rm.
The xxx and yyy are called strategies and the expected payoff is (for the row player) is

xTAy. x^T A y. xTAy.

What is one player knows the other’s strategy? In this scenario, the row player choose its strategy xxx first and the column player responds by a strategy yyy, or vise versa.
Obvious, the strategy of the player who plays first can not be deterministic, i.e., have probability 111 on one index and 000 on others.

The question is, whether the order of choosing a strategy results in different expected payoff.

First we write down what the expected payoff is. If the row player commit to a strategy xxx first, the column player will choose a distribution yyy minimize it expected loss:

min⁡yxTAy. \min_y x^T A y. ymin​xTAy.

As xxx is known, this is equivalent to choosing the column with minimum value:

Understanding the expected behavior of the column player, the row player should choose a strategy xxx, such that in the worst case, its expected payoff is maximized:

max⁡x(min⁡yxTAy)=max⁡x(min⁡j∈[m]xTAej). \max_{x} (\min_y x^T A y) = \max_{x} (\min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j). xmax​(ymin​xTAy)=xmax​(j∈[m]min​xTAej​).
