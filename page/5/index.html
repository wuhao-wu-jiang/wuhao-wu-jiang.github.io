Application of Fubini's Theorem

Posted on 2022-12-13

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \lambda)\) be a probability space, and \(X, Y : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be independent random variables.

Define the distributions of \(X\) and \(Y\) by \[ \begin{aligned} \mu(A) \doteq \Pr[ X \in A ] \doteq \lambda \Big(X^{-1}(A) \Big), \quad \forall A \in \mathcal{B}, \\ \nu(B) \doteq \Pr[ Y \in B ] \doteq \lambda \Big(Y^{-1}(B) \Big), \quad \forall B \in \mathcal{B}. \end{aligned} \]

Hence, both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are probability measures on \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B})\). Denote \(\mu \times \nu\) the product measure of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) on \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2)\).

By independence of \(X\) and \(Y\), \(\forall A, B \in \mathcal{B}\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ X \in A, Y \in B ] &= \Pr[ X \in A ] \cdot \Pr[ Y \in B ] \\ &= \mu(A) \cdot \nu(B) \\ &= (\mu \times \nu) (A \times B). \end{aligned} \]

Since \(\mathcal{S} \doteq \{ A \times B: A, B \in \mathcal{B} \}\) generates \(\mathcal{B}^2\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{S}) = \mathcal{B}^2\), applying \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, we get \[ \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] = (\mu \times \nu) (M), \quad \forall M \in \mathcal{B}^2. \]

It follows that \(\mu \times \nu\) is the distribution of \((X, Y)\).

We list some interesting corollaries based on Fubini's theorem.

Corollary One. For each \(M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), it holds that \[ \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \mu( dx ). \] Similarly, for each \(A \in \mathcal{B}, M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), \[ \Pr[ X \in A, (X, Y) \in M ] = \int_{A} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \mu( dx ). \]

Proof. Via Fubini's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] &= \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ] \, \, (\mu \times \nu)( d (x, y) ). \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ] \, \, \nu( dy ) \, \, \mu( dx ) \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \nu( \{ y : (x, y) \in M \} ) \, \, \mu( dx ) \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \, \mu( dx ), \end{aligned} \] where \(\Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ]\) is a shorthand for \[ Y \in M_x \doteq \{ y : (x, y) \in M \}. \]

Substituting the function \(\mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ]\) with \[ \mathbb{I}[ x \in A ] \cdot \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ]. \] proves the second claim.

\(\square\)

Another corollary is a direct application of the Fubini's theorem.

Corollary Two. Assume that \(f: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a function measurable \(\mathcal{B}^2 / \mathcal{B}\). Define \[ g(x) \doteq \mathbb{E}[ f(x, Y) ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x, y) \,\, \nu (dy), \quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R}. \] Then we have: \[ \mathbb{E}[ g(X) ] = \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ], \] i.e., \[ \int_{\mathbb{R}} \left( \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x, y) \,\, \nu (dy) \right) \, \, \mu (dx) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x, y) \, \, (\mu \times \nu)(d (x,y)) \]

Remarks:

  1. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are not independent, then the above claim may not hold, since the product measure \(\mu \times \nu\) may not be the joint distribution of \((X, Y)\).

  2. In the above, we operate on the product space \((\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B}, \mu \times \nu) = (\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)\), instead of \((\Omega \times \Omega, \mathcal{F} \times \mathcal{F}, \lambda \times \lambda) = (\Omega^2, \mathcal{F}^2, \lambda^2)\).

  3. There are various interpretations of \(\mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ]\):

  • \(f(X, Y)\) can be viewed as a function from \(\Omega\) to \(\mathbb{R}\), given by \(\omega \rightarrow f( X(\omega), Y(\omega) )\). It is easy to see that it is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{B}\), and therefore is a random variable. Then \[ \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ] = \int_\Omega f( X(\omega), Y(\omega) ) \, \, \lambda (d \omega). \]

  • Alternative, we can view \(f(x, y)\) as a measurable function from the probability space \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)\), to \(\mathbb{R}\). It is a random variable, and \[ \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x, y) \, \, (\mu \times \nu)(d (x,y)). \]

  • It can be proven that the two integrals are equal:

    • If \(f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in A \times B]\), for some \(A, B \in \mathcal{B}\), then by independence of \(X\) and \(Y\), both integrals equal \(\mu(A) \cdot \nu(B)\).

    • Since \(\mathcal{S} \doteq \{ A \times B: A, B \in \mathcal{B} \}\) generates \(\mathcal{B}^2\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{S}) = \mathcal{B}^2\), applying \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, if \(f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M]\) for some \(M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), the two integral are equal.

    • By linearity of integration, if \(f(x, y)\) is \(\mathcal{B}^2\) simple function, the two integral are equal.

    • By monotone convergence theorem, if \(f(x, y)\) is a non-negative measurable function, the two integral are equal.

    • By decomposing \(f\) into non-negative and negative parts, if \(f(x, y)\) is an integrable function, the two integral are equal.

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.

Beta Distribution

Posted on 2022-11-25 Edited on 2022-12-01

We start with a possibly biased coin which lands on head with unknown probability \(p\) when tossed. Suppose we obtain \(Y = a\) heads and \(Z = b\) tails out of \(a + b\) tosses. Conditioned on \(p = x\) for some \(x \in (0, 1)\), we have \[ \Pr[ Y = a, Z = b \mid p = x ] = \binom{a + b}{a} \cdot x^a (1 - x)^b. \]

Assume further that we have equal prior of \(p\) over \([0, 1]\), then the posterior probability density function \(f(x)\) of \(p\) satisfies \[ f(x) \propto x^a (1 - x)^b, \qquad \forall x \in (0, 1). \]

This motivates the definition of Beta distribution.

Definition (Beta distribution). A random variable \(X\) follows the Beta distribution with parameters \(a > 0\) and \(b > 0\), written as \(X \sim \mathcal{B}(a, b)\), if its density is \[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\beta(a, b)} \cdot x^{a−1} (1 − x)^{b−1}, \qquad 0 < x < 1, \] where \(\beta(a, b)\) is a constant.

Note that it is just a convention that we define \(f(x) \propto x^{a - 1} (1 - x)^{b - 1}\) for \(\mathcal{B}(a, b)\), instead of \(f(x) \propto x^{a - 1} (1 - x)^{b - 1}\).

The fact that \(f(x)\) integrates to \(1\) implies that \[ \beta(a, b) = \int_0^1 x^{a−1} (1 − x)^{b−1} \, dx. \]

Property. Given \(n, k \in \mathbb{N}\), with \(k \le n\), \[ \int_0^1 \binom{n}{k} x^k (1 - x)^{n - k} \, dx = \frac{1}{n + 1}. \]

Proof.

Procedure One. Consider throwing independently and uniformly at random \(n\) red balls and \(1\) blue ball onto the interval \([0, 1]\). Denote \(Z\) the number of red balls to the left of the blue ball. The integration gives the probability \(\Pr[ Z = k ]\).

Procedure Two. On the other hand, if we 1) throw \(n + 1\) red balls uniformly at random onto the interval \([0, 1]\); 2) pick one red ball at random; 3) and replace it by a blue one, then the number of red balls to the left of the blue one follows the same distribution as in procedure one. Hence \[ \Pr[ Z = k ] = \frac{1}{n + 1}. \]

\(\square\)

Corollary. Given \(a, b \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \[ \begin{aligned} \beta(a, b) = \int_0^1 x^{a - 1} (1 - x)^{b - 1} \, dx = \frac{(a - 1)! (b - 1)!}{(a + b - 1)!} = \frac{\Gamma(a) \Gamma(b)}{ \Gamma(a + b) }. \end{aligned} \]

Remark: the result can be generalized to \(a, b > 0\).

Property. \[ \mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{a}{a + b}. \]

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[X] &= \frac{1}{\beta(a, b)} \cdot \int_0^1 x^{a} (1 − x)^{b−1} \, dx \\ &= \frac{\beta(a + 1, b)}{\beta(a, b)} \\ &= \frac{\Gamma(a + 1) \Gamma(b)}{ \Gamma(a + b + 1)} \cdot \frac{ \Gamma(a + b) }{\Gamma(a) \Gamma(b)} \\ &= \frac{a}{a + b}. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Property. \[ \mathbb{Var}[X] = \frac{ ab }{(a + b + 1)(a + b)^2}. \]

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[X^2] &= \frac{1}{\beta(a, b)} \cdot \int_0^1 x^{a + 1} (1 − x)^{b−1} \, dx \\ &= \frac{\beta(a + 2, b)}{\beta(a, b)} \\ &= \frac{\Gamma(a + 2) \Gamma(b)}{ \Gamma(a + b + 2)} \cdot \frac{ \Gamma(a + b) }{\Gamma(a) \Gamma(b)} \\ &= \frac{(a + 1) a}{(a + b + 1)(a + b)}. \end{aligned} \]

Hence, $$ \[\begin{aligned} \mathbb{Var}[X] &= \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \big( \mathbb{E}[X] \big)^2 \\ &= \frac{(a + 1) a}{(a + b + 1)(a + b)} - \left( \frac{a}{a + b} \right)^2 \\ &= \frac{(a + 1) a (a + b) - a^2 (a + b + 1) }{(a + b + 1)(a + b)^2} \\ &= a \cdot \frac{ a^2 + ab + a + b - a^2 - a b - a }{(a + b + 1)(a + b)^2} \\ &= \frac{ ab }{(a + b + 1)(a + b)^2}. \end{aligned}\]

$$

\(\square\)

Property (Beta Binomial Conjugacy). Let \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\). Suppose that \(X \sim \mathcal{B}(a, b)\), and \(Z \sim \textnormal{Bin}(n, X)\), then for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(k \le n\), then the posterior probability distribution of \(X\) equals \(\mathcal{B}(a + k, b + n - k)\).

Proof. Let \(f(x \mid Z = k)\) be the posterior density function. Then for each \(x \in [0, 1]\), \[ \begin{aligned} f(x \mid Z = k) &= \frac{ \binom{n}{k} x^k (1 - x)^{n - k} f(x) }{ \Pr[ Z = k ] } \\ &= \frac{ \binom{n}{k} x^{a + k - 1} (1 - x)^{b + n - k - 1} }{ \Pr[ Z = k ] \cdot \beta(a, b) } \\ &\propto x^{a + k - 1} (1 - x)^{b + n - k - 1}. \end{aligned} \]

The last ''\(\propto\)'' holds since \(\Pr[ Z = k ]\) is a constant.

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] J. Blitzstein and J. Hwang, Introduction to Probability.

Integrating sin(x)/x from zero to infinity

Posted on 2022-11-19 Edited on 2023-03-08

We want to prove the following improper integral

Theorem. \[ \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

Existence of Limit

Since \(\int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \sin x \, dx = (-1)^{n} \cdot 2\), \[ \int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx \in \Theta \left( \frac{ (-1)^{n} }{n} \right). \]

Hence \(\lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx\) exists. On the other hand, \(\frac{\sin x}{x}\) is not Lebesgue integrable over \([0, \infty)\).

Proving the Limit

Observation. \[ \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, dy = \frac{\sin x}{x}. \]

We need two technical lemmas to finish the proof.

Lemma 1. \(e^{-xy} \sin x\) is integrable on \([0, a] \times [0, \infty)\).

Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= \frac{ - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a }{1 + y^2} \\ &= \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2}. \end{aligned} \]

We defer the proof of the lemmas to the end of the article. Denote \(\mathcal{F}\) the collection of Lebesgue measurable sets on \(\mathbb{R}\), and \(\lambda\) the Lebesgue measure defined on \(\mathcal{F}\). Let \(\mu \doteq \lambda \times \lambda\) be the product measure on \((\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{F})\). Now, via Fubini's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} dx &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, d y dx \\ &=\int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} e^{-xy} \sin x \, d \mu \\ &= \int_0^\infty \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx d y \\ &=\int_0^\infty \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \arctan y \mid_0^\infty - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2} - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y. \end{aligned} \]

The right hand side converges to \(0\) as \(a\) approaches infinity: \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty \left| \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \right| \, d y &\le \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty (1 + y) e^{-ay} \, d y \\ &= \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} -\frac{1}{a} \left( (1 + y) e^{-ay} + \frac{1}{a} e^{-ay} \right) \Bigg|_0^\infty = 0. \end{aligned} \]

Proof of Lemma 1. Via Tonelli's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d \mu &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d y dx \\ &\le \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \, d y dx \\ &= \int_0^a \frac{1}{x} \, d y dx \\ &= \ln a \\ &\le \infty. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Proof of Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \cos x \, dx \\ &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \left( e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a + y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx \right). \end{aligned} \]

Another proof of Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} e^{ix} \, dx &= \int_0^a e^{-x (y - i)} \, dx \\ &= \frac{-1}{y - i} \cdot e^{-x (y - i)} \mid_0^a \\ &= \frac{1}{y - i} \cdot \left( 1 - e^{- a(y - i)} \right) \\ &= \frac{y + i}{1 + y^2} \cdot \left( 1 - e^{-ay} (\cos a + i \sin a ) \right). \end{aligned} \]

Hence, \[ \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx = \Im \left( \int_0^a e^{-xy} e^{ix} \, dx \right) = \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2}. \]

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure Anniversary Edition, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.
