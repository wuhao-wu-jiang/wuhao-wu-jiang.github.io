Ball in Bins

Posted on 2020-04-01 Edited on 2021-11-06

Suppose we would like to throw \(n\) balls into \(n\) bins uniformly at random. We claim that the bin with maximum load has \(\Theta( \frac{\ln n}{\ln \ln n})\) ball with high probability.

Preliminary

For all \(x \ge 0\), it holds that \[ e^x = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + ...\ge \frac{x^k}{ k! }. \]

For all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), \(1 + x \le \exp (x)\). Therefore for all \(n \in \mathbb{N}_+\), \[ \left( 1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^n \le \exp \left( \frac{1}{n} \cdot n \right) = e, \] and for \(n > 1\), \[ \left( \frac{ n - 1}{n} \right)^{n - 1} = \frac{1}{ \left( 1 + \frac{1}{n - 1} \right)^{n - 1} } \ge \frac{1}{e} . \]

For \(n \in \mathbb{N}_+\), \(k \in [n]\), \[ \binom{n}{k} \frac{k^k}{n^k} \le \left( 1 + \frac{k}{n} \right)^n = e^k. \]

For \(n \in \mathbb{N}_+\), \(k \in [n]\), \[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n (n - 1) \ldots (n - k + 1) }{k (k - 1) \ldots 1} \ge \frac{n^k}{k^k}. \]

Upper Bound

Via union bound, the probability that a particular bin receives at least \(k\) balls is given by \[ \Pr[ \exists k \text{ balls in the bin} ] \le \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \le \frac{1}{k!} \le \frac{ e^k }{ k^k}, \] We want to find a \(k\), such that \[ \left( \frac{e}{ k} \right)^k = \frac{1}{n^2}. \] Taking \(\ln\) of both sides, \[ \begin{aligned} k \ln k - k = 2 \ln n\, \implies k \ln k \in \Theta( \ln n)\, \implies \ln k \in \Theta( \ln \ln n). \end{aligned} \] (By using asymptotically notations, the relationship only holds for reasonably large enough \(k\) and \(n\).)

Substituting \(\ln k \in \Theta( \ln \ln n)\) into \(k \ln k \in \Theta( \ln n)\), we get \[ k \in \Theta\left( \frac{\ln n}{\ln \ln n} \right). \]

Finally, by union bound over the number of possible bins, the probability that there is a bin which receives \(\ge k\) ball is bounded by \(1 / n\).

Lower Bound

Given \(k \in \mathbb{N}_+\), define the indicator variables \(X_i\) for the event that the \(i\)-th bin receives exactly \(k\) balls, and let \(X = \sum X_i\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[X_i = 1] = \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} \ge \frac{n^k}{k^k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} \cdot \left( \frac{ n - 1}{n} \right)^{n - 1} \ge \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e}. \end{aligned} \] and \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[X_i = 1] = \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} \le \binom{n}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} \le \frac{ (en)^k }{ k^k } \frac{1}{n^k} = \frac{e^k}{k^k}. \end{aligned} \]

We shall prove that \(\mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) < 0,\, \forall i \neq j\). Intuitively, that bin \(i\) obtains ball makes it more difficult for bin \(j\) to obtain ball. It follows that \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[X] &= \sum \mathbb{E}[X_i] \ge n \cdot \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e}. \\ \mathbb{Var}[X] &= \sum_{ i \in [n] } \mathbb{Var}[X_i] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) \\ &\le \sum_{ i \in [n] } \mathbb{Var}[X_i] \le \sum_{ i \in [n] } \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] \\ &= \sum_{ i \in [n] } \mathbb{E}[X_i] \le n \cdot \frac{e^k}{k^k}. \end{aligned} \]

By Chebyshev Inequality, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ X \le 0] &\le \Pr[ |X - \mathbb{E}[X]| \ge \mathbb{E}[X] ] \\ &\le \frac{ \mathbb{Var}[X] }{ \mathbb{E}[X]^2} \le \left( n \cdot \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e} \right) / \left( n \cdot \frac{e^k}{k^k} \right)^2 = \left( \frac{ k }{ e } \right)^k \cdot \frac{1}{en}. \end{aligned} \]

If we set \(k = (1 / 3) \cdot ( \ln n ) / ( \ln \ln n )\), then \((k / e)^k \le ( \ln n)^k = n^{1/3}\) and \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ X \le 0 ] \le e^{ -1 } \cdot n^{ - 2 / 3}. \end{aligned} \]

Remark. For this setting of \(k\), \[ \mathbb{E}[X] \ge \left( n \cdot \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e} \right) \ge e^{-1} n^{2/ 3}. \]

As in expectation there are \(e^{-1} n^{2/ 3}\) such bins, it seems that we are likely to find one. But this alone is not enough to guarantee that \(X\) does not deviate from its expectation much. To see why this might cause a problem, consider the random variable \(Y\) such that \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[Y = e^{-1} n^{ 5 / 3} ] = \frac{1}{n}, \quad \Pr[Y = 0 ] = 1 - \frac{1}{n}. \\ \end{aligned} \]

\(Y\) has expectation \(\mathbb{E}[Y] = e^{ - 1} n^{ 2 / 3}\), but is very likely to be \(0\). The problem with \(Y\) is that it has large variance.

Fact. For \(i \neq j\), \(\mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) < 0\)

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) &= \mathbb{E} [ X_i X_j ] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \Pr[X_i = 1, X_j = 1] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \binom{n}{k} \binom{n - k}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} \frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &< \binom{n}{k} \binom{n}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} \frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= 0. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

The last inequality follows from that \[ \begin{aligned} \binom{n - k}{k} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} &= \binom{n}{k} \frac{(n - k) \cdots (n - k + 1)}{n \cdots (n - k + 1)} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} \\ \end{aligned} \] and that (assuming that \(k \ge 2\)) \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{(n - k) \cdots (n - k + 1)}{n \cdots (n - k + 1)} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} &\le \left( \frac{n - k}{n} \right)^k \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} \\ &\le \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - k} \\ &\le \left( \Big( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \Big)^2 \right)^{n - k} \\ &= \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k}. \end{aligned} \]
