Integrating sin(x)/x from zero to infinity

Posted on 2022-11-19 Edited on 2022-11-20

We want to prove the following improper integral

Theorem. \[ \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

Existence of Limit

Since \(\int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \sin x \, dx = (-1)^{n} \cdot 2\), \[ \int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx \in \Theta \left( \frac{ (-1)^{n} }{n} \right). \]

Hence \(\lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx\) exists. On the other hand, \(\frac{\sin x}{x}\) is not Lebesgue integrable over \([0, \infty)\).

Proving the Limit

Observation. \[ \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, dy = \frac{\sin x}{x}. \]

We need two technical lemmas to finish the proof.

Lemma 1. \(e^{-xy} \sin x\) is integrable on \([0, a] \times [0, \infty)\).

Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= \frac{ - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a }{1 + y^2} \\ &= \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2}. \end{aligned} \]

We defer the proof of the lemmas to the end of the article. Denote \(\mathcal{F}\) the collection of Lebesgue measurable sets on \(\mathbb{R}\), and \(\lambda\) the Lebesgue measure defined on \(\mathcal{F}\). Let \(\mu \doteq \lambda \times \lambda\) be the product measure on \((\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{F})\). Now, via Fubini's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} dx &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, d y dx \\ &=\int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} e^{-xy} \sin x \, d \mu \\ &= \int_0^\infty \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx d y \\ &=\int_0^\infty \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \arctan y \mid_0^\infty - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2} - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y. \end{aligned} \]

The right hand side converges to \(0\) as \(a\) approaches infinity: \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty \left| \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \right| \, d y &\le \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty (1 + y) e^{-ay} \, d y \\ &= \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} -\frac{1}{a} \left( (1 + y) e^{-ay} + \frac{1}{a} e^{-ay} \right) \Bigg|_0^\infty = 0. \end{aligned} \]

Proof of Lemma 1. Via Tonelli's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d \mu &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d y dx \\ &\le \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \, d y dx \\ &= \int_0^a \frac{1}{x} \, d y dx \\ &= \ln a \\ &\le \infty. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Proof of Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \cos x \, dx \\ &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \left( e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a + y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx \right). \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure Anniversary Edition, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.

Complex Integral

Posted on 2022-11-13 Edited on 2022-11-19

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)\) be a measure space.Let \(f: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\) be a complex valued function. It can be written as \(f(\omega) \doteq g(\omega) + i h(\omega)\), where \(g, h\) are both real functions on \(\Omega\).

Definition [Measurability]. A function \(f\) is measurable \(\mathcal{F}\) if both \(g\) and \(h\) are.

Definition [Integral]. \(f\) is integrable if both \(g\) and \(h\) are, and its integral is defined by \[ \int f \, d \mu \doteq \int g \, d \mu + i \int h \, d \mu. \]

Theorem. \(f\) is integrable if and only if \(|f|\) is integrable.

Remark: the integral of the former is complex-valued, while the later is real-valued.

Proof. The claim follows from that \[ \max \{ |g|, |h| \} \le |f| = \sqrt{|g|^2 + |h|^2} \le |g| + |h|. \]

\(\square\)

Theorem [Linearity]. Let \(f_1, f_2\) be integrable. Then \(f_1 + f_2\) is integrable, and \[ \int f_1 + f_2 \, d \mu = \int f_1 \, d \mu + \int f_2 \, d \mu. \]

Proof. The integrability of \(f_1 + f_2\) follows from the one of \(|f_1 + f_2|\), which is integrable as \(|f_1 + f_2| \le |f_1| + |f_2|\).

To prove linearity, let \(f_1 = g_1 + i h_1\) and \(f_2 = g_2 + i h_2\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \int f_1 + f_2 \, d \mu &\doteq \int g_1 + g_2 \, d \mu + i \int h_1 + h_2 \, d \mu \\ &= \int g_1 \, d \mu + i \int h_1 \, d \mu + \int g_2 \, d \mu + i \int h_2 \, d \mu \\ &\doteq \int f_1 \, d \mu + \int f_2 \, d \mu. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Theorem. If \(f\) is integrable, then \[ \left| \int f \, d \mu \right| \le \int |f| \, d \mu. \]

Proof. Let \(f = g + i h\). Consider first that \(g\) and \(h\) are simple functions. W.L.O.G., assume \(g = \sum_{k \in [n]} x_k \mathbb{I}_{A_k}\), \(h = \sum_{k \in [n]} y_k \mathbb{I}_{A_k}\), where \(A_k \in \mathcal{F}, k \in [n]\) constitute a partition of \(\Omega\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \left| \int f \, d \mu \right| &= \left|\int g \, d \mu + i \int h \, d \mu \right| \\ &= \left| \sum_{k \in [n]} x_k \cdot \mu (A_k) + i \sum_{k \in [n]} y_k \cdot \mu (A_k) \right| \\ &= \left| \sum_{k \in [n]} \big( x_k + i y_k \big) \cdot \mu (A_k) \right| \\ &\le \sum_{k \in [n]} \left| \big( x_k + i y_k \big) \cdot \mu (A_k) \right| \\ &= \sum_{k \in [n]} \left| \big( x_k + i y_k \big) \right| \cdot \mu (A_k) \\ &= \int |f| \, d \mu. \end{aligned} \]

For general integrable \(f = g + i h\), there exists simple functions \(g_n, h_n\) such that

  1. \(\lim_n g_n = g\) and \(\lim_n h_n = h\);

  2. \(|g_n| \le |g|\) and \(|h_n| \le |h|\) for all \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\);

  3. \(\lim_n \int g_n \, d \mu = \int g \, d \mu\) and \(\lim_n \int h_n \, d \mu = \int h \, d \mu\).

It follows that \(|g_n + i h_n| \uparrow |f|\). Hence, \[ \begin{aligned} \left| \int f \, d \mu \right| &= \left|\int g \, d \mu + i \int h \, d \mu \right| \\ &= \left| \lim_n \int \left( g_n + i h_n \right)\, d \mu \right| \\ &= \lim_n \left| \int g_n + i h_n \, d \mu \right| \\ &\stackrel{(a)}{\le} \lim_n \int \left| g_n + i h_n \right| \, d \mu \\ &\stackrel{(b)}{=} \int |f| \, d \mu, \end{aligned} \] where Inequality \((a)\) follows from previous claim, and Inequality \((b)\) follows from monotone convergence theorem.

\(\square\)

Theorem. Suppose that \(f_n = g_n + i h_n\) are complex integrable functions satisfying \(\sum_{n \ge 1} \int |f_n| \, d \mu < \infty\). Then \(\sum_{n \ge 1} f_n\) is integrable, and \[ \int \sum_{n \ge 1} f_n \, d \mu = \sum_{n \ge 1} \int f_n \, d \mu. \]

Proof. By monotone convergence theorem, we have \[ \int \sum_{n \ge 1} |f_n| \, d \mu = \sum_{n \ge 1} \int |f_n| \, d \mu < \infty. \]

Since \[ \Bigg| \sum_{n \ge 1} g_n \Bigg| \le \sum_{n \ge 1} |g_n| \le \sum_{n \ge 1} |f_n|, \] \(\sum_{n \ge 1} g_n\) is integrable. Via dominated convergence theorem, \[ \int \sum_{n \ge 1} g_n \, d \mu = \sum_{n \ge 1} \int g_n \, d \mu. \]

Similar argument shows that \(\sum_{n \ge 1} h_n\) is integrable, and \[ \int \sum_{n \ge 1} h_n \, d \mu = \sum_{n \ge 1} \int h_n \, d \mu. \]

Therefore, \(\sum_{n \ge 1} f_n\) is integrable, and \[ \begin{aligned} \int \sum_{n \ge 1} f_n \, d \mu &\doteq \int \sum_{n \ge 1} g_n \, d \mu + i \int \sum_{n \ge 1} h_n \, d \mu \\ &= \sum_{n \ge 1} \int g_n \, d \mu + \sum_{n \ge 1} i \int h_n \, d \mu \\ &\doteq \sum_{n \ge 1} \int f_n \, d \mu. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure Anniversary Edition, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.

Khintchine Inequality

Posted on 2022-11-08 Edited on 2022-11-10

Definition (\(p\)-norm). Given \(p \in \mathbb{N}^+\) and a random variable \(X \in L_p\), define \[ \Vert X \Vert_p \doteq \Big( \mathbb{E} \big[ |X|^p \big] \Big)^{1 / p}. \]

We verify that this is a norm. Denote \(a = \Vert X \Vert_p\), \(b = \Vert Y \Vert_p\) and \(s = a + b\). Via the convexity of \(| \cdot |^p\), \[ \begin{aligned} \left\Vert \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{X}{a} + \frac{b}{s} \cdot \frac{Y}{b} \right\Vert_p^p &= \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \left\vert \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{X}{a} + \frac{b}{s} \cdot \frac{Y}{b} \right\vert^p \Bigg] \\ &\le \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \frac{a}{s} \cdot \left\vert \frac{X}{a} \right\vert^p + \frac{b}{s} \cdot \left\vert \frac{Y}{b} \right\vert^p \Bigg] \\ &= \frac{a}{s} + \frac{b}{s} \\ &= 1. \end{aligned} \]

Further, for \(q, p \in \mathbb{N}^+, q \le p\), we have \(\Vert X \Vert_q \le \Vert X \Vert_p\), since via the convexity of \(| \cdot |^{p / q}\), \[ \Vert X \Vert_q^p = \Big( \mathbb{E} \big[ |X|^q \big] \Big)^{p / q} \le \mathbb{E} \Big[ \big( |X|^q \big)^{p / q} \Big] = \Vert X \Vert_p^p. \]

Khintchine Inequality. Given \(p \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(\vec{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and independent Rademacher random variable \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\), \[ \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_p \le \frac{\sqrt{p + 1}}{\sqrt{p}} \cdot \sqrt{p} \cdot \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2 \in O( \sqrt{p} \cdot \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2 ). \]

Proof. Suppose that this holds when \(p\) is even. Then for odd \(p\), by monotonicity of random variable norms, \[ \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_p \le \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_{p + 1} \in O \left( \sqrt{p + 1} \cdot \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2 \right) = O \left( \sqrt{p} \cdot \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2 \right). \]

It is left to consider that \(p\) is even. Let \(Z_1, \ldots, Z_n\) be independent standard normal random variable. We compare \(\left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_p\) with \(\left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i \right\Vert_p\). Observe that both \[ \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \Bigg| \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \Bigg|^p \Bigg] = \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \Bigg( \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \Bigg)^p \Bigg], \quad \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \Bigg| \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i \Bigg|^p \Bigg] = \mathbb{E} \Bigg[ \Bigg( \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i \Bigg)^p \Bigg] \] can be expanded into a sum of expectation of monomials.

Each monomial with odd exponents has expectation \(0\). Meanwhile, for non-negative even integers \(m_1, \ldots, m_n\) that sum up to \(p\), we have \[ \prod_{i \in [n]} (a_i)^{m_i} \mathbb{E}[ \left( X_i \right)^{m_i} ] = \prod_{i \in [n]} (a_i)^{m_i} \le \prod_{i \in [n]} (a_i)^{m_i} \mathbb{E}[ \left( Z_i \right)^{m_i} ], \] where the last inequality holds since \((a_i)^{m_i}\) and \(\mathbb{E}[ \left( Z_i \right)^{m_i} ] = (m_i - 1)!! \ge 1\).

Therefore \(\left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_p^p\) is dominated term-by-term by \(\left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i \right\Vert_p^p\). But \(\sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i\) follows the distribution \(N(0, \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2)\). Therefore, \[ \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot X_i \right\Vert_p \le \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i \cdot Z_i \right\Vert_p = \Vert \vec{a} \Vert_2 \cdot \big( \left( p - 1\right)!! \big)^{1 / p} \]

Finally, note that \((p - 1) !! \le p^{p / 2} \Longrightarrow \big( \left( p - 1\right)!! \big)^{1 / p} \le \sqrt{p}\).

\(\square\)

Remark. See Wikipedia for obtaining a tighter estimation for odd \(p\).

Facts. Suppose that \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \Vert Z \Vert_p^p &\doteq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty |z|^p \exp \left( - \frac{1}{2} z^2 \right) \, d z \\ &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} \int_{0}^\infty z^p \exp \left( - \frac{1}{2} z^2 \right) \, d z \\ &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} \int_{0}^\infty (\sqrt{2u})^p \exp \left( - u \right) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2u}} \, d u, && u = \frac{z^2}{2} \\ &= \frac{ 2^{p / 2} }{ \sqrt{\pi} } \int_{0}^\infty u^{(p + 1) / 2 - 1} \exp \left( - u \right) \, d u \\ &= \frac{ 2^{p / 2} }{ \sqrt{\pi} } \cdot \Gamma \left( \frac{p + 1}{2} \right). \end{aligned} \]

Note that, when \(p\) is odd, \[ \Gamma \left( \frac{p + 1}{2} \right) = \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right)!, \] and when \(p\) is even, \[ \Gamma \left( \frac{p + 1}{2} \right) = \frac{p - 1}{2} \cdots \frac{1}{2} \cdot \Gamma \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) = \frac{p - 1}{2} \cdots \frac{1}{2} \cdot \sqrt{\pi}. \]

Therefore, \[ \frac{ 2^{p / 2} }{ \sqrt{\pi} } \cdot \Gamma \left( \frac{p + 1}{2} \right) = \begin{cases} \begin{aligned} &\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} (p - 1)!! &\text{ if }\, p \,\text{ is odd },\, \\ & (p - 1)!! &\text{ if }\, p \,\text{ is even}. \end{aligned} \end{cases} \]
