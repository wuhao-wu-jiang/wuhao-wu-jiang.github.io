Multivariate Fubini's Theorem

Posted on 2022-12-25 Edited on 2023-02-01

Let \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(k \ge 2\), and \((X_i, \mathcal{F}_i, \mu_i), i \in [k]\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces.

Produce Measure Space

Consider the product space \(\Omega = \times_{i \in [k]} X_i\).

Definition. A measurable rectangle is a product \(\times_{i \in [k]} A_i\) for which \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, i \in [k]\).

Denote the collection of measurable rectangles as \[ \mathcal{S} \doteq \begin{Bmatrix} \times_{i \in [k]} A_i, A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, \forall i \in [k] \end{Bmatrix}. \]

Let \(\times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i \doteq \sigma(\mathcal{S})\) be the \(\sigma\)-field generated by \(\mathcal{S}\). Following similar vein, for each \(I \subseteq [k]\), we can define \(\times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i\) to be the \(\sigma\)-field generated by measurable rectangles \(\{ \times_{i \in I} A_i, A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, \forall i \in I \}\).

Further, define the operation ordered product \(\vec{\times}\) on two measurable set \(A \in \left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right)\) and \(B \in \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right)\) as \[ A \vec{\times} B \doteq \begin{Bmatrix} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) : \begin{matrix} x_i \in A, \forall i \in I \\ x_i \in B, \forall i \in \overline{I} \end{matrix} \end{Bmatrix}, \] and let \(\left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right)\) to be the \(\sigma\)-field generated by measurable rectangles \[ \mathcal{S}_{I, \overline{I}} \doteq \left\{ A \vec{\times} B, A \in \left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right), B \in \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \right\}. \]

Theorem. Let \(I \subseteq [k]\), and consider \[ \left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right) = \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i \]

Proof. Since \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{S}_{I, \overline{I}}\), clearly it holds that \[ \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i \subseteq \left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right). \]

On the other hand, for each \(A \vec{\times} B \in \mathcal{S}_{I, \overline{I}}\), we claim that \(A \vec{\times} B \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\). Hence \(\times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\) is a \(\sigma\)-field containing \(\mathcal{S}_{I, \overline{I}}\). Via the definition of \(\left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right)\), we conclude that \[ \left( \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I}} \mathcal{F}_i \right) \subseteq \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i. \]

To prove the claim, we will show \(A \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\) and \(\left( \times_{i \in I } X_i \right) \vec{\times} B \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\). By symmetry, we need only to prove the former.

Consider \[ \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i} \doteq \{ A_I : A_I \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i \} \] Then \(\mathcal{S}_I \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\). Further,

  1. \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\);

  2. If \(A_I \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\), then \(\bar A_I \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\), as \(\bar A_I \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\)

  3. If \(A_I^{(n)} \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\) for \(n \in \N^+\), then \(\cup_{n \ge 1} A_I^{(n)} \in \mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\), as \[ \left( \cup_{n \ge 1} A_I^{(n)} \right) \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) = \cup_{n \ge 1} \left( A_I^{(n)} \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) \right) \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i \]

It follows that \(\mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field, and therefore \(\mathcal{L}_{\times_{i \in I} X_i} \supseteq \times_{i \in I} \mathcal{F}_i\). It concludes that \(A \vec{\times} \left( \times_{i \in \overline{I} } X_i \right) \in \times_{i \in [k]} \mathcal{F}_i\).

\(\square\)

We would like to construct a measure \(\mu\) on \(\sigma(\mathcal{S})\), thus

  1. For each \(\times_{i \in [k]} A_i \in \mathcal{S}\), it's natural to define \(\mu (\times_{i \in [k]} A_i) = \prod_{i \in [k]} \mu_i(A_i)\).

  2. We prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semialgebra.

  3. We show that \(\mu\) is a measure on \(\mathcal{S}\).

  4. Finally, it follows by the Caratheodory’s Extension Theorem, \(\mu\) has a unique extension on \(\sigma(\mathcal{S})\).

Lemma 1. \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semialgebra.

Proof. * \(\mathcal{S}\) is close under intersection: if \(\times_{i \in [k]} A_i, \times_{i \in [k]} B_i \in \mathcal{S}\), then \[ (\times_{i \in [k]} A_i) \cap (\times_{i \in [k]} B_i) = \times_{i \in [k]} (A_i \cap B_i). \] * The complement of element in \(\mathcal{S}\) is a finite disjoint union of elements in \(\mathcal{S}\): if \(\times_{i \in [k]} A_i \in \mathcal{S}\), since \[ \Omega = \times_{i \in [k]} \big( A_i +\bar{A_i} \big), \] it holds that \[ \overline{ \times_{i \in [k]} A_i } = \sum \big( \times_{i \in [k]} B_i \big), \] where \(B_i = A_i\) or \(B_i = \bar{A_i}\), and \(\times_{i \in [k]} B_i \neq \sum_{i \in [k]} A_i\).

\(\square\)

Theorem. There is a unique extension \(\bar \mu\) of \(\mu\), that is a measure on \(\sigma( \mathcal{S} )\).

Proof. We prove the theorem by induction. For each \(\ell \in [k]\), define \[ \mathcal{S}_{1:\ell} \doteq \begin{Bmatrix} \times_{i \in [\ell]} A_i, A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, \forall i \in [\ell] \end{Bmatrix}, \] and \[ \mu_{1:\ell} (\times_{i \in [\ell]} A_i) = \prod_{i \in [\ell]} \mu_i(A_i). \]

Our goal is to show that \(\mu_{1:\ell}\) is a \(\sigma\)-finite measure on \(\mathcal{S}_{1:\ell}\). Note that \(\mathcal{S}_{1:\ell}\) is a semialgebra and \(\mu\) is a \(\sigma_{1:\ell}\)-finite measure. Applying Caratheodory’s Extension Theorem, we see that there is a unique extension \(\bar \mu_{1:\ell}\) of \(\mu_{1:\ell}\), that is a measure on \(\sigma( \mathcal{S}_{1:\ell} )\).

The claim clearly holds for \(\ell = 1\).

Next, we prove that if the claim holds for \(\ell < k\), then it holds for \(\ell + 1\). The \(\sigma\)-finiteness follows directly from the finiteness of \(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_{\ell + 1}\). It suffices to prove \(\sigma\)-additivity of \(\mu_{1 : \ell + 1}\).

Suppose \(\times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} A_i = +_{m \ge 1} (\times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} B_{m, i})\) is a countable disjoint union of elements in \(\mathcal{S}_{1: \ell + 1}\).

Consider an \(x_{1 : \ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} A_i\). For each \(x_{\ell + 1} \in A_{\ell + 1}\), we have \((x_1, \ldots, x_{\ell + 1}) \in \times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} A_i\). As the sets \(\begin{Bmatrix} \times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} B_{m, i} \end{Bmatrix}\) consists of a partition of \(\times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} A_i\), it follows that \((x_1, \ldots, x_{\ell + 1})\) belongs to exactly one \(\times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} B_{m, i}\). Denote \[ \begin{aligned} I(x_{1:\ell}) &\doteq \begin{Bmatrix} m : \exists x_{\ell + 1} \in B_{m,\ell + 1},\, \text{s.t.},\, (x_1, \ldots, x_{\ell + 1}) \in \times_{i \in [\ell + 1]} B_{m, i} \end{Bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{Bmatrix} m : x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} B_{m, i} \end{Bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \] Then \(A_{\ell + 1} = +_{m \in I(x)} B_{m, \ell + 1}\).

As \(\mu_{\ell + 1}\) is a measure on \((X_{\ell + 1}, \mathcal{F}_{\ell + 1})\), it is countably additive: \[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb{I} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell ]} A_i \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mu_{\ell + 1} (A_{\ell + 1}) = \sum_{m \in I(x_{1:\ell}) } \mu_{\ell + 1} (B_{m, \ell + 1}) \\ \implies &\mathbb{I} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} A_i \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mu_{\ell + 1} (A_{\ell + 1}) = \sum_{m \ge 1} \mathbb{I} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} B_{m, i} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mu_{\ell + 1} (B_{\ell + 1}). \end{aligned} \]

By monotone convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} &\int_{\times_{i \in [\ell]} X_i} \mathbb{I} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} A_i \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mu_{\ell + 1} (A_{\ell + 1}) \, d\, \big( \bar \mu_{1:\ell} \big) \\ = &\sum_{m \ge 1} \int_{\times_{i \in [\ell]} X_i} \mathbb{I} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:\ell} \in \times_{i \in [\ell]} B_{m, i} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mu_{\ell + 1} (B_{\ell + 1}) \, d\, \big( \bar \mu_{1:\ell} \big) \\ \end{aligned} \]

which is \[ \mu_1(A_1) \cdots \mu_{\ell + 1} (A_{\ell + 1}) = \sum_{i \ge 1} \mu_1(B_{m, 1}) \cdots \mu_{\ell + 1} (B_{m, \ell + 1}). \]

\(\square\)

Fubini's Theorem

Integration is a generalization of discrete sum. Given a measure space \((\Omega, \sigma( \mathcal{S} ), \bar \mu)\), and a measure function \(f\), the integral of \(f\) exists, if

  1. \(f \ge 0\);
  2. Or \(\int |f| d\, \bar \mu < \infty\).

In case that \((\Omega, \sigma( \mathcal{S} ), \bar \mu)\) is a produce measure space, the Fubini's theorem states that if the integral exists, it can be computed by iterated integrals.

Theorem (Fubini's Theorem). If \(f \ge 0\) or \(\int |f| d\, \bar \mu < \infty\), then there exists \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, i \in [k]\), s.t., \(\mu_i (A_i) = 0\), and for each permutation \(\pi\) of \([k]\), \[ \int_{\Omega } f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu = \int_{X_{\pi(1)} - A_{\pi(1)}} \ldots \int_{X_{\pi(k - 1)} - A_{\pi(k - 1)}} \int_{X_{\pi(k)}} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{\pi(k)} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{\pi(1)}. \]

Remark. For the case concerning \(f \ge 0\), we have \(A_i = \emptyset, i \in [k]\), and the Theorem is called Tonelli’s theorem.

Proof of Fubini's Theorem

By symmetry, it suffices to prove that \[ \int_{\Omega } f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu = \int_{X_{1} - A_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1}. \]

Sketch of the Proof

Before proving that the iterated integral equals the double integral, we need to make sure that the first iterated integral is well-defined. Specifically,

  • When \(x_{1 : k - 1}\) is fixed, the function \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}} : X_k \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined by \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq f(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable, and the integral \(\int_{X_k} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_k\) exists.

  • For each \(\ell \in [k - 1]\), there is \(\mathcal{F}_\ell\) measurable function \(g_\ell : X_\ell \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) such that \[ g_\ell (x_\ell) = \int_{X_{\ell + 1} - A_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_k \, \ldots \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1}, \qquad a.e.. \]

We will first establish the theorem for indicator functions \(\mathbb{I}_E\) for sets \(E \in \sigma( \mathcal{S} )\), which serves as a basic building blocks for extending the theorem for more general functions.

Lemma. Given \(E \in \sigma( \mathcal{S} )\), let \(\mathbb{I}_E\) be the indicator for \((x_1, \ldots, x_k) \in E\). Then

  1. For each \(x_k \in X_k\), the function \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable.

  2. The function \(g_\ell (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_k \, \ldots \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1}\) is \(\mathcal{F}_{\ell}\) measurable.

  3. \[ \int_{\Omega } \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu = \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1}. \]

The proof of the lemma is relegated to the end of the article.

Proof of the Theorem

For now, we apply it to prove Theorem for the following three increasingly more general cases.

Case 1. \(f\) is a simple function on \((\Omega, \sigma(\mathcal{S}))\).

Case 2. \(f \ge 0\) and is \(\sigma(\mathcal{S})\) measurable.

Case 3. \(f\) is measurable and integrable.

Remark: this follows exactly the same order with which we define Lebesgue integrals.

Case 1. Suppose that \(f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) = \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is a simple function on \((\Omega, \sigma(\mathcal{S}))\).

  1. For fixed \(x_{1 : k - 1} \in X_{1 : k - 1}\), according the lemma, the functions \(f_{x_{1 : k - 1}}^{ (i) } (x_k) \doteq \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k), i \in [n]\) are \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable. It follows that function \(f_{x_{1 : k - 1}} (x_k) = \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot f_x^{ (i) } (x_k)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable.

  2. For each \(\ell \in [k - 1]\), since \(g_\ell^{(i)} (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_k} \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_k \ldots d\, \mu_\ell\) is \(\mathcal{F}_\ell\) measurable, so is the function \[ g_\ell (x_\ell) = \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot g_\ell^{(i)} (x_1, \ldots, x_k). \]

  3. Since \(\int_{\Omega } \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu = \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1}\), \[ \begin{aligned} &\int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ =& \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ =& \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot \int_{\Omega } \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu \\ =& \int_{\Omega } \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot\mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu \\ =& \int_{\Omega } f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu. \end{aligned} \]

Case 2. Suppose that \(f \ge 0\) and is \(\sigma(\mathcal{S})\) measurable. Then there exists simple, nonnegative functions \(f^{ (n) } \uparrow f\).

  1. For fixed \(x_k \in X_K\). The functions \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}}^{ (n) } (x_k) \doteq f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \uparrow f_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq f(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\). Since they are \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable, so is \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}} (x_k)\).

  2. For each \(\ell \in [k - 1]\), let \(g_\ell^{(n)} (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_k} f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_k \ldots d\, \mu_\ell\). We prove by induction that \(0 \le g_{\ell + 1}^{(n)} (x_{\ell + 1}) \uparrow g_{\ell + 1} (x_{\ell + 1})\). The claim is true when \(\ell + 1 = k\). Next, if this is true for \(\ell + 1\), we show that it is true for \(\ell\). By monotone convergence theorem: \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_n g_\ell^{(i)} (x_\ell) &= \lim_n \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} g_{\ell + 1}^{(n)} (x_{\ell + 1}) d\, \mu_\ell \\ &= \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} g_{\ell + 1} (x_{\ell + 1}) d\, \mu_\ell = g_\ell (x_\ell). \end{aligned} \] Hence \(g_\ell^{(n)} (x_\ell) \uparrow g_\ell (x_\ell)\). Since they are \(\mathcal{F}_\ell\) measurable, so is \(g_\ell (x_\ell)\).

  3. Since \(g_1^{(n)} (x_1) \uparrow g_1 (x_1) \,\), \(\int_{X_1} g_1^{(n)} (x_1) d\, \mu_1 = \int_{\Omega } f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu \,\), and \(f^{(n)} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \uparrow f(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\), by monotone convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} &\int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &= \lim_n \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &= \lim_n \int_{\Omega } f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu \\ &= \int_{\Omega } \lim_n f^{ (n) } (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu \\ &= \int_{\Omega } f (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu. \end{aligned} \]

Case 3. Suppose that \(f(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is a measurable and integrable function on \(\Omega\), i.e., \(\int_{\Omega } |f(x_1, \ldots, x_k)| d\, \bar \mu < \infty\). Write \(f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) = f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) - f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\), such that \(f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k), f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \ge 0\) are measurable and integrable.

  1. For a fixed \(x \in X\), since both \(f^+_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k),\, f^-_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) are \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable, so is the function \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}} (x_k) \doteq f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) = f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) - f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_k\).

  2. Since \(f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k), f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \ge 0\) are measurable and integrable, we have
    \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} = \int_{\Omega } f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu < \infty, \\ \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} = \int_{\Omega } f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \bar \mu < \infty. \end{aligned} \] It follows that \(\exists A_\ell \subseteq \mathcal{F}_\ell\), s.t., \(\mu_\ell (A_\ell) = 0\) for each \(\ell \in [k - 1]\), and \(g_\ell^{+} (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1} - A_\ell} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} f^{+} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_k \ldots d\, \mu_\ell\) and \(g_\ell^{-} (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1} - A_\ell} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} f^{-} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) d\, \mu_k \ldots d\, \mu_\ell\) are finite on \(X_\ell \setminus A_\ell\). Define \[ g_\ell (x_\ell) = \begin{cases} g_\ell^{+} (x_\ell) - g_\ell^{-} (x_\ell), & \text{if } x_\ell \in X_\ell \setminus A_\ell \\ 0, & \text{if } x_\ell \in A_\ell \end{cases}. \] Then \(g_\ell (x_\ell)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_\ell\) measurable.

  3. We have \[ \begin{aligned} &\int_{X_{1} - A_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &= \int_{X_{1} - A_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &- \int_{X_{1} - A_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1} - A_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &= \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &- \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1} \\ &= \int_{\Omega } f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu - \int_{\Omega } f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu \\ &= \int_{\Omega } f^+(x_1, \ldots, x_k) - f^-(x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu \\ &= \int_{\Omega } f (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Proof of the Lemma

Lemma. Given \(E \in \sigma( \mathcal{S} )\), let \(\mathbb{I}_E\) be the indicator for \((x_1, \ldots, x_k) \in E\). Then

  1. For each \(x_{1:k - 1} \in X_{1: k - 1}\), the function \(f_{x_{1:k - 1}}(x_k) \doteq \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) is \(\mathcal{F}_k\) measurable.

  2. The function \(g_\ell (x_\ell) \doteq \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_k \, \ldots \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1}\) is \(\mathcal{F}_{\ell}\) measurable.

  3. \[ \int_{\Omega } \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu = \int_{X_{1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_{k}} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{1}. \]

Proof of (1).

Definition. Let \(E_{x_{1:k - 1}} \doteq \begin{Bmatrix} x_k : (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \in E \end{Bmatrix}\) be the cross-section at \(x_{1: k - 1}\).

It suffices to show that

Claim. For each \(x_{1: k - 1} \in X_{1: k - 1}\) and \(E \in \sigma(\mathcal{S})\), \(E_{x_{1 : k - 1}} \in \mathcal{F}_k\).

Proof of the Claim. Fix an \(x_{1:k - 1} \in X_{1:k - 1}\). Define the function \(T_{x_{1:k - 1}} : X_k \rightarrow \times_{i \in [k]} X_i\) by \(T_{x_{1:k - 1}} (x_k) \doteq (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\). Then for each \(E = \times_{i \in [k]} A_i \in \mathcal{S}\), \[ T_{x_{1:k - 1}}^{-1} (E) = E_{x_{1:k - 1}} = \begin{cases} A_k, &\text{ if } x_{1:k - 1} \in \times_{i \in [k - 1]} A_i, \\ \emptyset, & \text{ otherwise }. \end{cases} \]

Therefore \(T_{x_{1:k - 1}}^{-1} (E) = E_{x_{1:k - 1}} \in \mathcal{F}_k\). Since \(\mathcal{S}\) generates \(\sigma(\mathcal{S})\), we conclude that \(T_{x_{1 : k - 1}}\) is measurable \(\mathcal{F}_k / \sigma(\mathcal{S})\).

\(\square\)

Proof of (2) and (3).

In what follows, to indicate that \(g_\ell(x_\ell)\) is associated with an \(E \in \sigma( \mathcal{S} )\), we write it as \(g_{E, \ell} (x_\ell)\).

We are going to prove that, for each \(E \in \sigma( \mathcal{S} )\), and each \(\ell \in [k]\) , \[ g_{E, \ell} (x_\ell) \doteq \begin{cases} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k), & \text{ if }\ell = k, \\ \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \ldots \int_{X_{k - 1}} \int_{X_k} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_k \, \ldots \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1}, & \text{ otherwise }. \end{cases} % = \bar \mu_{\ell + 1 : k} ( E_{x_{1:\ell}}) \] is \(\mathcal{F}_{\ell}\) measurable, and
\[ \begin{aligned} \bar{\mu}_{\ell : k} (E_{x_{1: \ell - 1}}) &= \int_{ X_\ell \times \cdots \times X_k } \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \bar \mu_{\ell : k} = \int_{X_{\ell}} g_{E, \ell} (x_\ell) \mu_{\ell}, % &= \int_{X_{\ell}} \ldots \int_{X_{k}} \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) \, d\, \mu_{k} \, \ldots d\, \mu_{\ell}, \end{aligned} \] where \(\bar{\mu}_{\ell : k}\) is the unique measure defined on \((X_\ell \times \cdots \times X_k, \times_{i = \ell}^k \mathcal{F}_i)\), s.t., \[ \bar{\mu}_{\ell : k} ( \times_{i = \ell}^k A_i ) = \prod_{i = \ell}^k \mu_i (A_i ), \quad \forall \, \big( \times_{i = \ell}^k A_i \big) \in \Big\{ \times_{i = \ell}^k B_i, B_i \in \mathcal{F}_i, \forall i \in [\ell \, . \, . k] \Big\}, \] and \[ E_{x_{1: \ell - 1}} \doteq \begin{Bmatrix} (x_\ell' \ldots, x_k') : (x_1, \ldots, x_{\ell - 1}, x_\ell' \ldots, x_k') \in E \end{Bmatrix}. \]

Define the set \(\mathcal{L} \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal{S})\) as the collection of \(E\) for which the conditions hold.

  1. Clearly it holds that \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{L}\);
  2. We show that \(\mathcal{L}\) is a \(\lambda\) system.

Since \(\mathcal{S}\) is a \(\pi\) system, via Dynkin's \(\pi-\lambda\) theorem, \(\mathcal{L}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and hence \(\mathcal{L} = \sigma(\mathcal{S})\).

We will now check that \(\mathcal{L}\) is a \(\lambda\)-system. We can suppose without loss of generality that \(\mu(\Omega) < \infty\).

  1. \(\Omega \in \mathcal{L}\).

  2. If \(E \in \mathcal{L}\), we want to prove that \(\bar E \in \mathcal{L}\). In particular, we need to show that \(\bar{E}\) satisfies the equations for each \(\ell\) by induction.

    Base Case: \(\ell = k\). This holds due to the previous claim.

    Inductive Case: \(\ell < k\). First, observe that \[ (\bar{E})_{x_{1:\ell}} = \Omega_{x_{1:\ell}} - E_{x_{1:\ell}} = X_{\ell + 1} \times \cdots \times X_k - E_{x_{1:\ell}} \in \mathcal{F}_{\ell + 1} \times \ldots \times \mathcal{F}_{k}. \] Since \(\bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k}(X_{\ell + 1} \times \cdots \times X_k) < \infty\), \[ \begin{aligned} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} ( (\bar{E})_{x_{1:\ell}} ) &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (X_{\ell + 1} \times \cdots \times X_k - E_{x_{1:\ell}}) \\ &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (X_{\ell + 1} \times \cdots \times X_k) - \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (E_{x_{1:\ell}}) \\ &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (X_{\ell + 1} \times \cdots \times X_k) - g_{E, \ell} (x_{\ell}). \end{aligned} \] Since \(g_{E, \ell} (x_{\ell})\) is \(\mathcal{F}_{\ell}\) measurable, so is \(g_{\bar{E}, \ell} (x_{\ell}) = \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (X_k) - \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (E_{x_{1:\ell}})\). Integrating over \(x_{\ell}\) gives \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{X_{\ell}} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} ( (\bar{E})_{x_{1:\ell}} ) \, d\, \bar{\mu}_{\ell} &= \int_{X_{\ell}} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (X_k) \, d\, \bar{\mu}_{\ell} - \int_{X_{\ell}} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} (E_{x_{1: \ell}}) \, d\, \bar{\mu}_{\ell} \\ &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell: k} (X_{\ell} \times \cdots \times X_k) - \bar{\mu}_{\ell: k} (E_{x_{1:\ell - 1}}). \end{aligned} \]

    Since \(\bar{\mu}_{\ell: k} (X_{\ell} \times \cdots \times X_k) < \infty\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{X_{\ell}} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1 : k} ( (\bar{E})_{x_{1:\ell}} ) \, d\, \mu_{\ell} &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell: k} (X_{\ell} \times \cdots \times X_k - E_{x_{1:\ell - 1}}) \\ &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell : k} ((\bar{E})_{x_{1: \ell - 1}}) . \end{aligned} \]

  3. If \(E_n \in \mathcal{L}, n \in \mathbb{N}^+\) is a disjoint sequence of sets, then \(\cup_{n \ge 1} E_n \in \mathcal{L}\).

    Base Case: \(\ell = k\).
    Then \(g_{E, k} (x_{\ell}) = \mathbb{I}_E (x_1, \ldots, x_k) = \sum_{n \ge 1} \mathbb{I}_{E_n} (x_1, \ldots, x_k) = \sum_{n \ge 1} g_{E_n, k}\).

    Inductive Case: \(\ell < k\). Define \(g_{E_n, \ell} (x_{\ell}) = \mu_{\ell + 1 : k} ((E_n)_{x_{1:\ell}})\). Then \(g_{E, \ell} (x_{\ell}) \doteq \sum_{n \ge 1} g_{E_n, \ell} (x_{\ell})\) is \(\mathcal{F}_\ell\) measurable. By monotone convergence theorem and that \(\mu_\ell\) is a measure, it holds that \[ \begin{align*} g_{E, \ell} &= \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} g_{E, \ell + 1} (x_{\ell + 1}) \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1} \\ &= \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} \sum_{n \ge 1} g_{E_n, \ell + 1} (x_{\ell + 1}) \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1} \\ &= \sum_{n \ge 1} \int_{X_{\ell + 1}} g_{E_n, \ell + 1} (x_{\ell + 1}) \, d\, \mu_{\ell + 1} \\ &= \sum_{n \ge 1} \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1:k} (E_n) \\ &= \bar{\mu}_{\ell + 1:k} (E). \end{align*} \] Therefore, \(E \in \mathcal{L}\).

\(\square\)
