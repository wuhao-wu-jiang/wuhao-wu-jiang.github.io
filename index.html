Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Posted on 2021-07-18 Edited on 2021-07-24

Let \(\Omega \subseteq \mathcal{C}\) be a open set and \(f : \Omega \rightarrow \mathcal{C}\) be a complex function. \(f\) is holomorphic at \(z \in \Omega\), if the following limit exits: \[ \begin{array}{lr} \lim_{z + h \in \Omega, h \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + h) - f(z) }{ h }. & (1) \end{array} \]

The limit is denoted as \(f'(z)\) and is called the derivative of \(f\) at \(z\). Note that \(h\) can approach \(0\) from any direction.

Define \[ { \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} (z) \doteq \lim_{x \in \R, z + x \in \Omega, x \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + x) - f(z) }{ x }, } \] which is the limit of \((1)\) when \(h\) approaches to \(0\) from the real axis.

Similarly, define when limit when \(h\) is purely imaginary: \[ \small \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y} (z) \doteq \lim_{y \in \R, z + y \in \Omega, y \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z + i \cdot y) - f(z) }{ i \cdot y }. \]

If the derivate of \(f\) exists at \(z\), we have shown that \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y}. &\qquad (2) \end{aligned} \] Writing \(f = u + iv\), we get \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial f}{ \partial x} &= \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x} + i \cdot \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x}, \\ \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y} &= \frac{1}{i} \cdot \left( \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y} + i \cdot \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y} \right) = -i \cdot \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y} + \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}. \end{aligned} \]

Equation (2) implies that \[ \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}, \qquad \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x} = - \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}. \]

These are the Cauchy-Riemann equations. The next theorem states that the converse is also true, i.e., when the equations are satisfies at some point \(z\), then the derivative of \(f = u + iv\) exists.

Theorem. Let \(u, v : \Omega \rightarrow \R\) and \(f = u + iv\). If \(u\) and \(v\) are continuously differentiable at \(z \in \Omega\) and satisfy Cauchy-Riemann equations, then the derivative of \(f\) exits.

Proof. Since \(u\) and \(v\) are continuously differentiable at \(z = (x, y)\), for small \(\Delta z = (\Delta x, \Delta y) \in \R^2\), \[ u( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) = \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \Delta x + \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \Delta y + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Phi_u( \Delta z), \\ \begin{aligned} v( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) &= \frac{\partial v}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \Delta x + \frac{\partial v}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \Delta y + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Phi_v( \Delta z) \end{aligned} \]

where \(\Phi_u( \Delta z) \rightarrow 0\) and \(\Phi_v( \Delta z) \rightarrow 0\) as \(\Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \rightarrow 0\).

Therefore, \[ \small \begin{aligned} f( z + \Delta z ) - f( z ) &= u( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) + i \Big( v( x + \Delta x , y + \Delta y ) - u(x, y) \Big) \\ &= \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) \cdot \big( \Delta x + i \Delta y \big) + \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \cdot \big( \Delta y - i \Delta x \big) + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Big( \Phi_u( \Delta z) + \Phi_v( \Delta z) \Big) \\ &= \Big( \frac{\partial u}{ \partial x}(z) - i \frac{\partial u}{ \partial y}(z) \Big) \cdot \big( \Delta x + i \Delta y \big) + \Vert \Delta z \Vert_1 \cdot \Big( \Phi_u( \Delta z) + \Phi_v( \Delta z) \Big), \end{aligned} \]

which finishes the proof.

\(\square\)

Power Series--Radius of Convergence

Posted on 2021-07-17

Theorem (Hadamard's Formula). Let \(f : \mathbb{C} \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\) be a power series defined as \(f(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} a_n z^n\). The radius of convergence of \(f\), denoted as \(R \in [0, \infty]\), is given by \[ \small 1 / R = L \doteq \limsup_{k \ge 1} |a_k|^{1 / k}. \] where we adopt the convention that \(1 / 0 = \infty\) and \(1 / \infty = 0\).

It is guaranteed that 1. If \(|z| < R\), then \(f\) converges absolutely. 2. If \(|z| > R\), then \(f\) diverges.

Remark: The region \(|z| < R\) is called the disc of convergence of \(f\).

Proof.
If \(|z| < R\), then \(L \cdot |z| < 1\). Hence, \[ \small \exists \epsilon > 0, \, r < 1, \, \text{ s.t. } \, (L + \epsilon) \cdot |z| < r. \]

Since \(\sup_{k \ge n} | a_k |^{1 / k} \rightarrow L\), for large enough \(n\), we have \[ \small L + \epsilon \ge \sup_{k \ge n} | a_k |^{1 / k} \ge |a_n|^{1 / n}. \]

Therefore, \[ \small | a_n z^n | = \big( |a_n|^{1 / n} \cdot |z| \big)^n \le \big( (L + \epsilon) \cdot |z| \big)^n \le r^n. \]

It follows that \(f\) converges absolutely.

If \(|z| > R\), then \(L \cdot |z| > 1\). Hence, \[ \small \exists \epsilon > 0, \, r > 1, \, \text{ s.t. } \, (L - \epsilon) \cdot |z| > r. \]

Since \(\sup_{k \ge n} | a_k |^{1 / k}\) decreases to \(L\), for each \(n \in \N^+\), \[ \small \exists k \ge n, \, \text{ s.t. } \, |a_k|^{1 / k} \ge (L - \epsilon). \]

For such \(k\), \[ \small | a_k z^k | = \big( |a_k|^{1 / k} \cdot |z| \big)^k \ge \big( (L - \epsilon) \cdot |z| \big)^k \ge r^k. \]

Since the terms of \(f\) are unbounded, the series is consequently divergent.

\(\square\)

Theorem. Let \(f : \mathbb{C} \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\) be a power series defined as \(f(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} a_n z^n\). Then it is holomorphic in its disc of convergence. Its derivative is given by \[ f'(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} n a_n z_n^{n - 1}. \] Moreover, \(f'(z)\) has the same disc of convergence.

Proof. Let \(b_n = n a_n\). Define \[ \small g(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} b_n z_n^{n - 1} = \sum_{n \ge 1} n a_n z_n^{n - 1}. \] Observe that \[ \begin{array}{rl} \limsup_n | b_n |^{ 1 / n} &= \limsup_n | n a_n |^{ 1 / n} \\ &= \lim_n | n |^{ 1 / n} \cdot \limsup_n | a_n |^{ 1 / n} \\ &= \limsup_n | a_n |^{ 1 / n}. \end{array} \]

It follows that \(g(z)\) has the same disc of convergence as \(f(z)\).

It is left to prove that \(g(z)\) is the derivative of \(f(z)\). For \(k \in \N_+\), write \(f(z) = p_k (z) + r_k(z)\), where \[ p_k(z) = \sum_{n \in [k] } a_n z^n,\quad r_k(z) = \sum_{n > k} a_n z^n. \]

Denote \(R\) the radius of convergence of \(f(z)\). and suppose \(0 \le |z_0| < r < R\). Then for \(h \in \mathbb{C}\), such that \(|z_0 + h| < r\), we have \[ \small \begin{aligned} \frac{ f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0) }{ h } &= \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } + \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } \\ &= \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } + \left( \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } - p_k'(z_0) \right) + p_k'(z_0). \end{aligned} \]

We will prove that as \(k \rightarrow \infty\), \[ p_k'(z_0) \rightarrow g(z_0),\, \quad \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } \rightarrow 0. \]

Moreover a fixed \(k\), as \(h \rightarrow 0\), \[ \left( \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } - p_k'(z_0) \right) \rightarrow 0. \]

Therefore, \(\lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{ f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0) }{ h } = g(z_0)\).

To finish the proof, we see that

\[ \small \begin{aligned} \left| \frac{ f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0) }{ h } - g(z_0) \right| &= \left| \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } + \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } - g(z_0) \right|\\ &\le \left| \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } - p_k'(z_0) \right| \\ &+ \left| p_k'(z_0) - g(z_0 ) \right| \\ &+ \left| \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } \right|. \end{aligned} \]

Since \(g(z_0)\) converges absolutely, for large enough \(k\), \[ \small \left| p_k'(z_0) - g(z_0 ) \right| \le \epsilon. \]

Since \[ a^n - b^n = (a - b) \left( a^{n - 1} + a^{n - 2} b^2 + \ldots + b^{n - 1} \right), \]

and using that \(|z_0 + h| < r\), \(|z_0 | < r\), \[ \small \begin{aligned} \left| \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } \right| &= \left| \frac{ \sum_{n > k} a_n [ (z_0 + h)^n - z ^n ] }{ h } \right| &\le \sum_{n > k} a_n n r^{n - 1}. \end{aligned} \]

Since \(g(r)\) converges absolutely, for large enough \(k\), \[ \left| \frac{ r_k(z_0 + h) - r_k(z_0) }{ h } \right| \le \sum_{n > k} a_n n r^{n - 1} \le \epsilon. \]

Finally, for a fixed \(k\), when \(h \rightarrow 0\), \[ \left| \frac{ p_k(z_0 + h) - p_k(z_0) }{ h } - p_k'(z_0) \right| \rightarrow 0. \]

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] L. V. Ahlfors, Complex analysis: an introduction to the theory of analytic functions of one complex variable, 3d ed. New York: McGraw-Hill, 1979.

Complex Mapping

Posted on 2021-07-16 Edited on 2021-07-23

Suppose we have complex mapping \(f : X \rightarrow Y\), where \(X, Y \subset \mathbb{C}\). How do we visualize it? As, we can't visualize four dimension data in a single plot, we split it into two, one for the domain and one for the codomain.

For example, consider the unit cycle centered at \(2\) \[ S_1 \doteq \{ z \in \mathbb{C} : | z - 2 | < 1 \}. \]

Let \(f(z) \doteq \frac{2}{z - 1}\). Then \(S_1\) is mapped to the set \[ S_2 \doteq \{ z \in \mathbb{C} : \text{Re}(z) > 1 \}. \]

This is shown by the picture below.

We verify this algebraically.

Proof 1. Let \(w = \frac{2}{z - 1}\). As \(w \neq 0\), we have \[ {\small z - 1 = \frac{2}{w} \implies z = \frac{2}{w} + 1. } \] Hence, \[ \begin{aligned} \small |z - 2 | < 1 &\implies \left| \frac{2}{w} - 1 \right| < 1 \\ &\implies \left| \frac{2 - w}{w} \right|^2 < 1 \\ &\implies \left| 2 - w \right|^2 < |w|^2 \\ &\implies \Vert (2 - x, y) \Vert_2^2 < \Vert (x, y) \Vert_2^2 &\text{let } w = (x, y) \\ &\implies 4 - 4 x < 0 \\ &\implies 1 < x. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Proof 2. Denote the border of \(S_1\) as \(z = 2 + e^{i \theta}\), where \(\theta \in [-\pi, \pi)\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} w &= \frac{2}{z - 1} \\ &= \frac{2}{1 + e^{i \theta } } \\ &= \frac{2}{1 + e^{i \theta } } \cdot \frac{e^{- i\theta / 2 } }{ e^{ - i \theta / 2 } } \\ &= \frac{2 e^{ - i \theta / 2} }{ e^{ - i \theta / 2 } + e^{i \theta / 2} } \\ &= 2 \cdot \frac{ \cos (\theta / 2) + i \sin (\theta / 2) }{ 2 \cos (\theta / 2) } \\ &= 1 + i \tan (\theta / 2). \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)
