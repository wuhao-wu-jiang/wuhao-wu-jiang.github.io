Independence

Posted on 2022-08-20 Edited on 2022-09-18

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \Pr)\) be a probability space.

Independence Events

Definition. A finite collection of events \(A_1, \ldots, A_n \in \mathcal{F}\) are independent, if for each collection of events \(B_1, \ldots, B_n\), for which either \(B_i = \Omega\) or \(B_i = A_i\), \(i \in [n]\), it holds that \[ \Pr[ B_1 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] = \Pr[ B_{1} ] \cdots \Pr[ B_{n} ]. \]

Since every \(B_i = \Omega\) can be suppressed on each side of the equation, this is equivalent to that for each set of distinct indices \(i_1 ,i_2 \ldots ,i_k\) between \(1\) to \(n\), \[ \Pr[ A_{i_1} \cap \ldots \cap A_{i_k} ] = \Pr[ A_{i_1} ] \cdots \Pr[ A_{i_k} ]. \]

Definition. An infinite (perhaps uncountable) collection of events \(\{ A_\theta, \theta \in \Theta \}\) are independent if each of its finite sub-collections of events are independent.

Independence Classes

Definition. A finite classes \(\mathcal{A}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{A}_n \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) are independent, if for each choice of \(A_i \in \mathcal{A}_i, i \in [n]\), the events \(A_i, i \in [n]\) are independent.

Definition. An infinite (perhaps uncountable) collection of classes \(\{ \mathcal{A}_\theta : \theta \in \Theta \}\) are independent if for each choice of \(A_\theta \in \mathcal{A}_\theta, \theta \in \Theta\), the events \(\{ A_\theta, \theta \in \Theta \}\) are independent.

Observe that by the definition of independence of infinite collection of event, the independence of \(\{ \mathcal{A}_\theta : \theta \in \Theta \}\) is equivalent to the independence of each finite sub-collection \(\mathcal{A}_{\theta_1}, \ldots, \mathcal{A}_{\theta_k}\) of the class.

Independent \(\sigma\)-fields

The following theorem underpins a number of theorems related to independence of random variables. It states that to decide a collection of \(\sigma\)-fields are independent, it suffices to show that a collection of \(\pi\)-systems generating them are independent.

Theorem. If the classes \(\mathcal{A}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{A}_n \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) are independent and \(\pi\) systems, then \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_1), \ldots, \sigma(\mathcal{A}_n)\) are independent.

Proof. Fix \(A_2 \in \mathcal{A}_2, \ldots, A_n \in \mathcal{A}_n\).

Define \[ \mathcal{L} \doteq \{ E \in \mathcal{F}: E, A_2, \ldots, A_n \text{ are independent.} \}. \]

Lemma. \(\mathcal{L}\) is a \(\lambda\) system.

Since \(\mathcal{L}\) contains the \(\pi\) system \(\mathcal{A}_1\), by \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, it follows that \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_1) \subseteq \mathcal{L}\). Therefore, \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_1), \ldots, \mathcal{A}_n\) are independent.

Repeating the process proves that \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_1), \sigma(\mathcal{A}_2), \ldots, \mathcal{A}_n\) are independent, and so on.

Proof of the Lemma. 1. \(\Omega \in \mathcal{L}\).

  1. If \(E \in \mathcal{F}\), then \(\bar E \in \mathcal{F}\): if \[ \Pr[ E \cap B_{2} \cap \ldots \cap B_{k} ] = \Pr[E] \cdot \Pr[ B_2 ] \cdots \Pr[ B_n ]. \] where \(B_i = A_i\) or \(B_i = \Omega\), \(i \in [2 .\,.\, n]\), then \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ \bar E \cap B_2 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] &= \Pr[ B_2 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] - \Pr[ E \cap B_2 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] \\ &= (1 - \Pr[E]) \cdot \Pr[ B_2 ] \cdots \Pr[ B_n ] \\ &= \Pr[\bar E] \cdot \Pr[ B_2 ] \cdots \Pr[ B_n ] \end{aligned} \]

  2. If \(E_k \in \mathcal{F}, k \in \mathbb{N}^+\) are pairwise disjoint, and \(E = \cup_{k \ge 1} E_k\), then \(E \in \mathcal{F}\): \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ E \cap B_2 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] &= \sum_k \, \Pr[ E_k \cap B_2 \cap \ldots \cap B_n ] \\ &= \sum_k \, \Pr[E_k] \cdot \Pr[ B_2 ] \cdots \Pr[ B_n ] \\ &= \Pr[E] \cdot \Pr[ B_2 ] \cdots \Pr[ B_n ]. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Simple Random Variable

Definition. A random variable \(X : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is simple, if it has finite range (assumes finitely many values), and for each \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), \[ \{ \omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) = x \} \in \mathcal{F}. \]

In probability theory, \(\omega\) is often omitted, and \(\{X = x \}\) is short for \(\{ \omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) = x \}\). It it easy to see that a random variable can be represented by \[ X = \sum_{i \in [n]} x_i \mathbb{I}_{A_i}, \] where the \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}, i \in [n]\) is a finite partition of \(\Omega\).

Definition. For a finite or countably infinite sequence \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) of random variables, \(\sigma(X_1, X_2, \ldots)\) is the intersection of all \(\sigma\)-fields with respect to which each \(X_i\) is measurable.

Independence

Definition. A finite or countably infinite sequence \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) of simple random variables are independent, if \(\sigma(X_1), \sigma(X_2), \ldots\) are independent.

We study a several importance properties.

Property 1

Theorem. A finite sequence \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) of simple random variables are independent, if and only if for each \(x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \mathbb{R}\), \[ \Pr[ X_1 = x_1, \ldots, X_n = x_n ] = \Pr[ X_1 = x_1 ] \cdots \Pr[ X_n = x_n ]. \]

Proof. If \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) of random variables are independent, the equation clearly holds. It suffices to prove the other direction.

For each \(i \in [n]\), let \(X_i(\Omega)\) be the range of \(X_i\), which is finite. Define the class \[ \mathcal{A}_i \doteq \{ X_i^{-1} (x), x \in X_i(\Omega) \} \cup \emptyset, \] which is a \(\pi\)-system, since the \(X_i^{-1} (x), x \in X_i(\Omega)\) are pairwise disjoint. Then \(\mathcal{A}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{A}_n\) are independent. It follows that \(\sigma(X_1), \sigma(X_2), \ldots\) are independent.

\(\square\)

Property 2

Next, we prove an important result. Consider a finite sequence of simple random variables: \[ \begin{matrix} X_{1, 1}, &\ldots &X_{k_1}\\ X_{2, 1}, &\ldots &X_{k_2}\\ \ldots &\ldots &\ldots \\ X_{n, 1}, &\ldots &X_{k_n}. \end{matrix} \] For each \(i \in [n]\), \(f_i: \mathbb{R}^{k_i} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a function. Then

Claim. The \(f_i(X_{i, 1}, \ldots X_{k_i})\) are independent.

The following theorem states that functions of finite simple random variables are again simple random variables.

Theorem. For a finite sequence \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) of simple random variables,

  1. The \(\sigma\)-field \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\) consists of the sets \[ \{ \omega \in \Omega : \left( X_1(\omega), \ldots, X_n(\omega) \right) \in H \}, \] where \(H \in \mathcal{R}^n\); \(H\) in this representation can be taken finite.

  2. A simple random variable \(Y\) is measurable \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\) if and only if \[ Y = f(X_1, \ldots, X_n), \] for some \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\).

Proof of Claim One. Define the function \(\pmb{X}: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) by \[ \pmb{X} (\omega) \doteq \left( X_1(\omega), \ldots, X_n(\omega) \right). \] For each \(H \in \mathcal{R}^n\), define \(\pmb{X}^{-1} (H) \doteq \{ \omega \in \Omega : \pmb{X} (\omega) \in H \}\), and define and the class of sets \[ \pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n) \doteq \left\{ \pmb{X}^{-1} (H) : H \in \mathcal{R}^n \right\}. \]

Step One: \(\pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n) \subseteq \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\).

Observe that for each \(\pmb{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \[ \begin{aligned} \pmb{X}^{-1} (\pmb{x}) &= \{ \omega \in \Omega : \pmb{X} (\omega) = \pmb{x} \} \\ &= \cap_{i \in [n]} \{ \omega \in \Omega : X_i(\omega) = x_i \}. \end{aligned} \] Since \(\{ \omega \in \Omega : X_i(\omega) = x_i \} \in \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\) for each \(i \in [n]\), we have \[ \cap_{i \in [n]} \{ \omega \in \Omega : X_i(\omega) = x_i \} \in \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n). \] Since \(\pmb{X}\), as a function, has finite range, each set $ ^{-1} (H) $ is a finite union of sets of the form \(\{ \omega \in \Omega : \pmb{X} (\omega) = \pmb{x} \}\), and therefore belongs to \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\).

Step One: \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \subseteq \pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n)\).

It suffices to prove that \(\pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n)\) a \(\sigma\)-field. At a high level, this follows from that \(\mathcal{R}^n\) is a \(\sigma\)-field.

  1. Since if \(H \in \mathcal{R}^n\), then \(\bar H \in \mathcal{R}^n\), it holds that \[ \overline{\pmb{X}^{-1} (H)} = \pmb{X}^{-1} (\bar H) \in \pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n). \]

  2. Since if \(H_k \in \mathcal{R}^n, k \in \mathbb{N}^+\), then \(\cup_{k \ge 1} H_k \in \mathcal{R}^n\), it holds that \[ \cup_{k \ge 1} \pmb{X}^{-1} (H_k) = \pmb{X}^{-1} (\cup_{k \ge 1} H_k) \in \pmb{X}^{-1} (\mathcal{R}^n). \]

\(\square\)

Proof of Claim Two. Suppose that \[ Y = f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) = f \circ \pmb{X} \] for some \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\). Since \(\pmb{X}\) has finite range, so does \(Y\). For each possible output \(y \in \mathbb{R}\), \(Y^{-1} (y)\) is a finite union of \(\pmb{X}^{-1}(\pmb{x})\) over those \(\pmb x\) for which \(f(\pmb{x}) = y\). It follows that \(Y\) is measurable \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\).

To prove the other direction, suppose that \(Y\) is measurable \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\). Since it is a simple random variable, it assumes finite values \(y_1, \ldots, y_k\).

  • The \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\)-sets \(Y^{-1} (y_i), i \in [k]\) forms a partition of \(\Omega\).

  • Since \(Y^{-1} (y_i) \in \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)\), via previous claim, there exists for each \(i \in [n]\), a set \(H_i \in \mathcal{R}^n\), such that \(\pmb{X}^{-1}(H_i) = Y^{-1} (y_i)\) and \(H_i\) can be taken finite.

  • For each \(H_i\), we construct a subset \(H_i' \subseteq H_i\), by removing from \(H_i\) the \(\pmb{x}\) for which \(\pmb{X}^{-1} (\pmb{x}) = \emptyset\). The \(H_i', i \in [n]\) are pairwise disjoint.

  • Finally, for each \(i \in [n]\) and each \(\pmb{x} \in H_i'\), define \(f(\pmb{x}) = y_i\). For all other \(\pmb{x} \notin \cup_{i \in [n]} H_i'\), define \(f(\pmb{x}) = 0\).

\(\square\)

We finish the proof of the claim with another result.

Theorem. Let \[ \begin{matrix} X_{1, 1}, &X_{1, 2}, &\ldots \\ X_{2, 1}, &X_{2, 2}, &\ldots \\ \ldots &\ldots &\ldots \end{matrix} \] be a (possibly countably infinite) sequence of independent simple random variables. For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), suppose the random variable \(Y_n\) is measurable \(\mathcal{F}_n \doteq \sigma(X_{n, 1}, X_{n, 2}, \ldots )\). Then \(Y_1, Y_2, \ldots\) are independent.

Proof. For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), define the class \[ \mathcal{A}_n \doteq \left\{ X_{n, i_1}^{-1} (x_{i_1}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n, i_k}^{-1} (x_{i_k}) : \begin{matrix} 1 \le i_1 < i_2 < \ldots < i_k \\ x_{i_1}, \ldots, x_{i_k} \in \mathbb{R} \end{matrix} \right\}, \] which is the finite intersections of the sets \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (x), k \in \mathbb{N}^+, x \in \mathbb{R}\). It is easy to see that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{A}_n\), and \(\mathcal{A}_n\) is a \(\pi\)-system. Further,

  • \(\mathcal{A}_n\) generates \(\mathcal{F}_n\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) = \mathcal{F}_n\):

    • \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \subseteq \mathcal{F}_n\): via definition of \(\mathcal{F}_n\), \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (x) \in \mathcal{F}_n\), for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+, x \in \mathbb{R}\). Hence, each element in \(\mathcal{A}_n\) belongs to \(\mathcal{F}_n\), and \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \subseteq \mathcal{F}_n\).

    • \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \supseteq \mathcal{F}_n\): via definition of \(\mathcal{A}_n\), \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (x) \in \mathcal{A}_n\), for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+, x \in \mathbb{R}\). Hence, each \(X_{n, k}\) is measurable \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n)\). Via definition of \(\mathcal{F}_n\), \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \supseteq \mathcal{F}_n\).

  • The \(\mathcal{A}_n\) are independent: let \(n_1, \ldots, n_m\) be a finite sequence of indexes in \(\mathbb{N}^+\). Suppose that for each \(j \in [m]\), \[ B_{n_j} \doteq X_{n_j, i_1(j)}^{-1} (x_{i_1(j)}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n_j, i_k(j)}^{-1} (x_{i_{k}(j)}). \] Then \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ B_{n_1} \cap \ldots \cap B_{n_m} ] &= \Pr\left[ \cap_{j \in [m]} \left( X_{n_j, i_1(j)}^{-1} (x_{i_1(j)}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n_j, i_k(j)}^{-1} (x_{i_{k}(j)}) \right) \right] \\ &= \prod_{j \in [m]} \Pr[ X_{n_j, i_1(j)} = x_{i_1(j)}] \cdots \Pr[X_{n_j, i_k(j)} = x_{i_{k}(j)} ] \\ &= \prod_{j \in [m]} \Pr[B_{n_j}]. \end{aligned} \]

It follows that the \(\mathcal{F}_n\) are independent.

\(\square\)

Remark. The second claim would be much easier to prove, if \(f\) is measurable \(\mathcal{R}^n / \mathcal{R}\).

Random Variables and Vectors

Definition. A function \(X : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a random variable, if it is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{R}\) (or measurable \(\mathcal{F}\) for short), i.e., \[ [X \in H] = X^{-1}(H) \in \mathcal{F}, \forall H \in \mathcal{R}. \]

Definition. A function \(X : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^k\) is a random vector, if it is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{R}^k\) (or measurable \(\mathcal{F}\) for short), i.e., \[ [X \in H] = X^{-1}(H) \in \mathcal{F}, \forall H \in \mathcal{R}^k. \]

Consider a function \(X : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^k\). For each \(\omega \in \Omega\), we write the \(i\)-th coordinate of \(X(\omega)\) as \(X_i(\omega)\). Hence, \(X(\omega) = (X_1(\omega), \ldots, X_k(\omega))\), and each \(X_i\) is itself a function from \(\Omega\) to \(\mathbb{R}\).

Theorem. \(X\) is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{R}^k\) (a random vector), if and only if each \(X_i\) is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{R}\) ( a random variable).

The proof relies on the following lemma.

Lemma. \(\mathcal{L} \doteq \{ H \in \mathcal{R}^k : [X \in H] \in \mathcal{F} \} \subseteq \mathcal{R}^k\) is a \(\sigma\)-field.

Proof of the Lemma. Observe that \(\mathcal{L}\) is closed under complement and countable union: \[ X^{-1}(\bar H) = \Omega - X^{-1}(H) \in \mathcal{F}, \text{ and }\, X^{-1}( \cup_{n \ge 1} H_n ) = \cup_{n \ge 1} X^{-1}(H_n) \in \mathcal{F}. \] \(\square\)

Proof of the Theorem.

Define \[ \mathcal{S} \doteq \{ (-\infty, x_1] \times \dotsm \times (-\infty, x_k] : x_1, \dots, x_k \in \mathbb{R} \}. \] for each \(H \in \mathcal{S}\), \[ \begin{align} [X \in H] &= \{ \omega : X_1(\omega) \le x_1, \dots, X_k(\omega) \le x_k \} \\ &= \cap_{i \in [k]} \{ \omega : X_i(\omega) \le x_i \} \\ &= \cap_{i \in [k]} [X_i \le x_i]. \end{align} \]

\(\impliedby:\) Suppose that each \(X_i\) is measurable, then \([X_i \le x_i] \in \mathcal{F}, i \in [k]\). It follows that \([X \in H] \in \mathcal{F}\), and \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{L}\). Since \(\mathcal{S}\) generates \(\mathcal{R}^k\), we have \(\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{R}^k\).

\(\implies:\) Suppose that \(X\) is measurable. Fix an \(i \in [k]\). By setting \(x_j = \infty\) for each \(j \neq i\) we have \[ [X \in H] = [X_i \le x_i] \in \mathcal{F}. \] It concludes that \(X_i\) is measurable.

\(\square\)

Theorem. The \(\sigma\)-field \(\sigma(X) = \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_k)\) consists exactly of the sets \([X \in H]\) for \(H \in \mathcal{R}^k\).

Proof. Let \(\mathcal{G} \doteq \{ [X \in H] : H \in \mathcal{R}^k \} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\). Then \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under complement and countable union: \[ \begin{align} \Omega - [X \in H] &= [ X \in (\mathbb{R}^k - H) ] \\ \cup_{n \ge 1} [ X \in H_n ] &= [X \in \cup_{n \ge 1} H_n]. \end{align} \] Hence \(\mathcal{G}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field, and \(\sigma(X) \subseteq \mathcal{G}\). But by the definition of \(\sigma(X)\) and \(\mathcal{G}\), we also have \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \sigma(X)\).

\(\square\)

Theorem. In order that a random variable \(Y\) be measurable \(\sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_k)\), it is necessary and sufficient that there exists a measurable map \(f: \mathbb{R}^k \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) s.t., \(Y(\omega) = f(X_1(\omega), \ldots, X_k(\omega))\) for all \(\omega\).

Proof.

\(\impliedby:\) this direction is obvious.

\(\implies:\) first, assume that \(Y\) is a simple random variable which assumes value \(y_1, \ldots, y_n\). Then the \(\mathcal{F}\)-sets \([Y = y_i], i \in [n]\) constitute a partition of \(\Omega\). Via the previous theorem, there exists \(H_1, \ldots, H_n \in \mathcal{R}^k\), such that \([Y = y_i] = [X \in H_i]\).

Note that the \(H_i\) are not necessarily disjoint. But we have the following observation: fix an \([Y = y_i]\). Then \[ \begin{align*} X( \{ \omega \in \Omega: Y(\omega) = y_i \} ) &\subseteq H_i, \\ X( \{ \omega \in \Omega: Y(\omega) = y_i \} ) \cap H_j &= \emptyset, \forall j \neq i. \end{align*} \] The second observation is true, as otherwise, \(X^{-1}(H_j) = [Y = y_j]\) has non-empty intersection with \([Y = y_i]\), a contradiction. At this point, we can set \[ \bar H_i = H_i - \cup_{j \in [n], j \neq i} H_j, \] and set \(\bar H_{n + 1} = \mathcal{R}^k - \cup_{i \in [n]} \bar H_i\). Then it still holds that \([X \in \bar H_i] = [Y = y_i], \forall i \in [n]\). Define the function \(f: \mathbb{R}^k \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) by
\[ f = \sum_{i \in [n]} y_i \cdot \mathbb{I}_{\bar H_i} + 0 \cdot \mathbb{I}_{\bar H_{n + 1}}. \] Clearly it holds that \(Y = f \circ X\).

Next, we treat the general case. There exists a sequence of simple random variables \(Y_n \rightarrow Y\). Let \(f_n\) be the corresponding simple functions for which \(Y_n = f_n \circ X\). Since the \(f_n\) are measurable, so are the functions \(\sup_n f_n\) and \(\inf_n f_n\). It follows that the set \(C \doteq \{ t \in \mathbb{R}^k : \sup_n f_n(t) = \inf_n f_n(t) \}\) for which the \(f_n\) converges lies in \(\mathcal{R}^k\). Define the function \(f\) by \[ f(t) = \begin{cases} \lim_n f_n(t), & \text{ if } t \in C, \\ 0 & \text{ if } t \in \mathbb{R}^k - C. \end{cases} \] It is easy to check that \(f\) is measurable. For each \(\omega \in \Omega\), we must have \(Y(\omega) = \lim_n Y_n (\omega) = \lim_n f_n(X(\omega))\); hence \(X(\omega) \in C\), and \(\lim_n f_n(X(\omega)) = f(X(\omega))\). Then it holds that \(Y = f \circ X\).

\(\square\)

Remark. We don't know whether \(X([Y = y_i]) \in \mathcal{R}^k\) or not. Otherwise, we can set \(\bar H_i = X([Y = y_i])\) so that \(X^{-1}(\bar H_i) = [Y = y_i]\) and the \(\bar H_i, i \in [n]\) are disjoint automatically.

Independence

Definition. A finite or countably infinite sequence \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) of random variables are independent, if \(\sigma(X_1), \sigma(X_2), \ldots\) are independent.

Theorem. The random variables \(X_1, \dots, X_n\) are independent if and only if for all \(x_i \in \mathbb{R}, i \in [n]\), \[ \Pr[ X_1 \le x_1, \ldots, X_n \le x_n ] = \Pr[ X_1 \le x_1 ] \cdots \Pr[ X_n \le x_n ]. \]

Proof. Since the \(\sigma\)-field \(\sigma(X_i)\) consists exactly of the sets \([X_i \in H]\) for \(H \in \mathcal{R}\), \(X_1, \dots, X_n\) are independent if and only if for all \(H_i \in \mathcal{R}, i \in [n]\), \[ \Pr[ X_1 \in H_1, \ldots, X_n \in H_n ] = \Pr[ X_1 \in H_1 ] \cdots \Pr[ X_n \in H_n ]. \] Since the intervals \((-\infty, x_i]\) forms a \(\pi\)-system generating \(\mathcal{R}\), \([X_i \le x_i]\) forms a \(\pi\)-system generating \(\sigma(X_i)\), which proves the theorem.

\(\square\)

Corollary. If the \(X_i\) are simple random variables, then they are independent if and only if for all \(x_i \in \mathbb{R}, i \in [n]\), \[ \Pr[ X_1 = x_1, \ldots, X_n = x_n ] = \Pr[ X_1 = x_1 ] \cdots \Pr[ X_n = x_n ]. \]

Let \(X = (X_1, \ldots, X_k)\) have distribution \(\mu\) and distribution function \(F\). Let \(f_i : \mathbb{R}^k \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be the projection defined by \(f_i(x_1,\ldots, x_k) = x_i\), \(i \in [n]\). It is measurable, and therefore \(X_i = f_i \circ X\) is measurable. Let the \(X_i\) have distributions \(\mu_i\) and distribution functions \(F_i\) (the marginals). Consider the product measurable \(\mu_1 \times \dots \times \mu_k\).

Theorem. The following conditions are equivalent 1. \(X_i\) are independent if and only if 2. \[ \mu = \mu_1 \times \dots \times \mu_k. \] 3. \[ F(x_1, \ldots, x_k) = F(x_1) \dotsb F(x_k). \]

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mu (H) &= \left( \mu_1 \times \dots \times \mu_k \right) (H), &\forall H \in \mathcal{R}^k \\ \Longleftrightarrow \mu (H_1 \times \cdots \times H_k) &= \mu_1 (H_1) \cdots \mu_k (H_k), &\forall H_1, \ldots, H_k \in \mathcal{R}. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, conditions (1) and (2) are equivalent.

Since the intervals \((-\infty, x_i]\) forms a \(\pi\)-system generating \(\mathcal{R}\), \([X_i \le x_i]\) forms a \(\pi\)-system generating \(\sigma(X_i)\), conditions (2) and (3) are equivalent.

\(\square\)

Theorem. Suppose that \(\mu_i\) has density \(f_i\). If the \(X_i\) are independent, then \(\mu\) has density \[ f(x) = f_1(x_1) \dotsb f_k(x_k). \]

Proof. Since the density function are non-negative, via Fubini's theorem, for each \(H_1, \ldots, H_k \in \mathcal{R}\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[X_1 \in H_1] \cdots \Pr[X_k \in H_k] &= \int_{H_1} f_1 \, d\mu_1 \cdots \int_{H_k} f_k \, d\mu_k \\ &= \int_{H_1 \times \dots \times H_k} f_1 \cdots f_k \, d\mu \\ &= \Pr[ X \in H_1 \times \dots \times H_k ]. \end{aligned} \] It follows by \(\pi-\lambda\) theorem, for each \(H \in \mathcal{R}^k\), we have \[ \int_{H} f_1 \cdots f_k \, d\mu = \Pr[ X \in H ]. \] \(\square\)

Theorem. Let \[ \begin{matrix} X_{1, 1}, &X_{1, 2}, &\ldots \\ X_{2, 1}, &X_{2, 2}, &\ldots \\ \ldots &\ldots &\ldots \end{matrix} \] be a (possibly countably infinite) sequence of independent random variables. The \(\sigma\)-fields \(\mathcal{F}_n \doteq \sigma(X_{n, 1}, X_{n, 2}, \ldots )\), \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\) are independent.

Proof. For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), define the class \[ \mathcal{A}_n \doteq \left\{ X_{n, i_1}^{-1} (H_{i_1}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n, i_k}^{-1} (H_{i_k}) : \begin{matrix} 1 \le i_1 < i_2 < \ldots < i_k \\ H_{i_1}, \ldots, H_{i_k} \in \mathcal{R} \end{matrix} \right\}, \] which is the finite intersections of the sets \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (H), k \in \mathbb{N}^+, H \in \mathcal{R}\). It is easy to see that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{A}_n\), and \(\mathcal{A}_n\) is a \(\pi\)-system. Further,

  • \(\mathcal{A}_n\) generates \(\mathcal{F}_n\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) = \mathcal{F}_n\):

    • \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \subseteq \mathcal{F}_n\): via definition of \(\mathcal{F}_n\), \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (H) \in \mathcal{F}_n\), for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+, H \in \mathcal{R}\). Hence, each element in \(\mathcal{A}_n\) belongs to \(\mathcal{F}_n\), and \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \subseteq \mathcal{F}_n\).

    • \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \supseteq \mathcal{F}_n\): via definition of \(\mathcal{A}_n\), \(X_{n, k}^{-1} (H) \in \mathcal{A}_n\), for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+, H \in \mathcal{R}\). Hence, each \(X_{n, k}\) is measurable \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n)\). Via definition of \(\mathcal{F}_n\), \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}_n) \supseteq \mathcal{F}_n\).

  • The \(\mathcal{A}_n\) are independent: let \(n_1, \ldots, n_m\) be a finite sequence of indexes in \(\mathbb{N}^+\). Suppose that for each \(j \in [m]\), \[ B_{n_j} \doteq X_{n_j, i_1(j)}^{-1} (H_{i_1(j)}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n_j, i_k(j)}^{-1} (H_{i_{k}(j)}). \] Then \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ B_{n_1} \cap \ldots \cap B_{n_m} ] &= \Pr\left[ \cap_{j \in [m]} \left( X_{n_j, i_1(j)}^{-1} (H_{i_1(j)}) \cap \ldots \cap X_{n_j, i_k(j)}^{-1} (H_{i_{k}(j)}) \right) \right] \\ &= \prod_{j \in [m]} \Pr[ X_{n_j, i_1(j)} \in H_{i_1(j)}] \cdots \Pr[X_{n_j, i_k(j)} \in H_{i_{k}(j)} ] \\ &= \prod_{j \in [m]} \Pr[B_{n_j}]. \end{aligned} \]

It follows that the \(\mathcal{F}_n\) are independent.

\(\square\)

Expectation

Theorem. If \(X\) ane \(Y\) are independent and integrable, then \[ \mathbb{E}[ X Y ] = \mathbb{E}[ X ] \mathbb{E}[ Y ]. \]

Proof. The claim holds trivially if \(X\) and \(Y\) are simple random variables.

We treat the general case. There exists simple random variables \(X_n\) and \(Y_n\) such that

  1. \(X_n \rightarrow X, |X_n| \uparrow |X|\) and \(Y_n \rightarrow Y, |Y_n| \uparrow |Y|\);

  2. The \(X_n\) are measurable \(\sigma(X)\) and the \(Y_n\) are measurable \(\sigma(Y)\).

It follows that the \(X_n\) and \(Y_n\) are independent for each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\). Now \(\mathbb{E}[ |X_n| \cdot |Y_n| ] = \mathbb{E}[ |X_n| ] \mathbb{E}[ |Y_n| ]\). Via monotone convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[ |X| \cdot |Y| ] &= \lim_n \mathbb{E}[ |X_n| \cdot |Y_n| ] \\ &= \lim_n \mathbb{E}[ |X_n| ] \mathbb{E}[ |Y_n| ] \\ &= \mathbb{E}[ |X| ] \mathbb{E}[ |Y| ] \\ &\le \infty. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \(\mathbb{E}[ |XY| ] = \mathbb{E}[ |X| \cdot |Y| ]\) is integrable. Via monotone convergence theorem again, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_n \mathbb{E} \left[ \inf_{k \ge n} \left( 2 |X Y| - |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right)\right] &= \mathbb{E} \left[ \lim_n \inf_{k \ge n} \left( 2 |X Y| - |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right)\right]. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \[ 2 \mathbb{E}[ |X Y| ] - \lim_n \mathbb{E} \left[ \sup_{k \ge n} |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right] = 2 \mathbb{E}[ |X Y| ] - \mathbb{E} \left[ \lim_n \inf_{k \ge n} |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right] \] Noting that \(|XY|\) is integrable and that \(X_k Y_k \rightarrow X Y\), we have \[ \lim_n \mathbb{E} \left[ \sup_{k \ge n} |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right] = 0, \] and \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_n |\mathbb{E}[ X_n Y_n] - \mathbb{E}[X Y]| &\le \lim_n \mathbb{E} \left[ |X_n Y_n - X Y| \right] \\ &\le \lim_n \mathbb{E} \left[ \sup_{k \ge n} |X_k Y_k - X Y| \right] \\ &= 0, \end{aligned} \] which proves that \(\mathbb{E}[X Y] = \mathbb{E}[ X_n Y_n] = \mathbb{E}[X_n] \mathbb{E}[ Y_n ]\).

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.

Advanced Joint Convexity

Posted on 2022-04-30

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space.

\(\alpha\)-Divergence

Definition. Given \(\alpha \ge 1\), measures \(\mu, \nu\) on \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\), define the \(\alpha\)-divergence from \(\mu\) to \(\nu\) by \[ D_\alpha (\mu \Vert \nu) \doteq \sup_{S \in \mathcal{F}} \Big( \mu(S) - \alpha \cdot \nu(S) \Big). \]

When \(\alpha = 1\), \(D_\alpha (\mu \Vert \nu)\) is the total variational distance between \(\mu\) and \(\nu\).

Properties

Theorem. \(D_\alpha (\mu \Vert \nu)\) is non-negative.

Proof. Since \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{F}\), \[ D_\alpha (\mu \Vert \nu) \ge \mu(\emptyset) - \alpha \cdot \nu(\emptyset) = 0. \] \(\square\)

Theorem (Joint Convexity). Let \(\mu, \nu, \mu', \nu'\) be measures on \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\). For each \(\alpha \ge 1\), and \(t \in [0, 1]\), it holds that \[ D_\alpha \left( (1 - t) \cdot \mu + t \cdot \nu \Big\Vert t \cdot \mu' + (1 - t) \cdot \nu' \right) \le (1 - t) \cdot D_\alpha \big( \mu \Vert \mu' \big) + t \cdot D_\alpha \big( \nu \Vert \nu' \big). \]

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} &D_\alpha \left( (1 - t) \cdot \mu + t \cdot \nu \Big\Vert t \cdot \mu' + (1 - t) \cdot \nu' \right) \\ = &\sup_{S \in \mathcal{F}} \Big( (1 - t) \cdot \mu(S) + t \cdot \nu(S) - \alpha \Big( (1 - t) \cdot \mu'(S) + t \cdot \nu'(S) \Big) \Big) \\ = &\sup_{S \in \mathcal{F}} \Big( (1 - t) \cdot \Big( \mu(S) - \alpha \mu'(S) \Big) + t \cdot \Big( \nu(S) - \alpha \nu'(S) \Big) \Big) \\ \le &\sup_{S \in \mathcal{F}} \Big( (1 - t) \cdot \Big( \mu(S) - \alpha \mu'(S) \Big) \Big) + \sup_{S \in \mathcal{F}} \Big( t \cdot \Big( \nu(S) - \alpha \nu'(S) \Big) \Big) \\ = & (1 - t) \cdot D_\alpha ( \mu \Vert \mu' ) + t \cdot D_\alpha ( \nu \Vert \nu' ). \end{aligned} \]
\(\square\)

Theorem (Advanced Joint Convexity [1]). Let \(\mu, \mu'\) be probability measures satisfying \[ \mu = (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 + t \cdot \mu_1, \quad \mu' = (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 + t \cdot \mu_1', \] for some \(t \in [0, 1]\) and probability measures \(\mu_0, \mu_1, \mu_1'\). Given \(\alpha \ge 1\), let \(\alpha' = 1 + t \cdot (\alpha - 1)\) and \(r = \alpha' / \alpha\). Then the following holds: \[ D_{\alpha'} ( \mu \Vert \mu' ) = t \cdot D_\alpha ( \mu_1 \Vert (1 − r) \mu_0 + r \mu_{1}') \]

Proof. For each \(S \in \mathcal{F}\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \mu(S) - \alpha' \mu'(S) &= (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 (S) + t \cdot \mu_1 (S) - \alpha' (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 (S) - \alpha' t \cdot \mu_1'(S) \\ &= t \cdot \left( \mu_1 (S) - \alpha' \mu_1'(S) \right) - (1 - t) \cdot \left( \alpha' - 1 \right) \mu_0 (S) \\ &= t \cdot \left( \mu_1 (S) - \alpha \left( \frac{\alpha'}{\alpha} \mu_1'(S) + \frac{(1 - t) \cdot (\alpha' - 1)}{t\alpha} \mu_0 (S) \right) \right) \end{aligned} \]

The following conditions are equivalent \[ \begin{array}{lrl} &1 - \alpha' / \alpha &= (1 - t) \cdot (\alpha' - 1) / (t \cdot \alpha), \\ \Longleftrightarrow\, &t \cdot \alpha - t \cdot \alpha' &= \alpha' - t \cdot \alpha' - 1 + t, \\ \Longleftrightarrow\, &1 + t \cdot (\alpha - 1) &= \alpha'. \end{array} \]

Therefore, assuming that \(1 + t(\alpha - 1) = \alpha'\) holds, and replacing \(\alpha' / \alpha\) with \(r\), we have \[ \mu(S) - \alpha' \mu'(S) = t \cdot \left( \mu_1 (S) - \alpha \left( r \mu_1'(S) + \big( 1 - r \big) \mu_0 (S) \right) \right), \quad \forall S \in \mathcal{F}. \]

Tanking supremum over both hand sides finishes the proof.

\(\square\)

Application

Definition. Let \(\mu, \nu\) be measures on \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\). Given \(\epsilon, \delta \ge 0\), \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are \((\epsilon , \delta)\)-close, if \[ D_{e^\epsilon} (\mu \Vert \nu) \le \delta, \qquad D_{e^\epsilon} (\nu \Vert \mu) \le \delta. \]

Based on advanced joint convexity, we have the following result.

Corollary. Let \(\mu, \mu'\) be probability measures satisfying \[ \mu = (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 + t \cdot \mu_1, \quad \mu' = (1 - t) \cdot \mu_0 + t \cdot \mu_1', \] for some \(t \in [0, 1]\) and probability measures \(\mu_0, \mu_1, \mu_1'\). Given \(\epsilon \ge 0\), let \(\epsilon' = \ln \left(1 + t \cdot (e^\epsilon - 1) \right)\) and \(r = e^{\epsilon'} / e^{\epsilon}\). Combined with joint convexity, the following holds: \[ \begin{aligned} D_{e^{\epsilon'}} ( \mu \Vert \mu' ) &= t \cdot D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert (1 − r) \mu_0 + r \mu_{1}') \\ &\le t \cdot \Big( (1 − r) \cdot D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert \mu_0 ) + r \cdot D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert \mu_{1}') \Big) \\ &\le t \cdot \max \Big( D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert \mu_0 ), \, D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert \mu_{1}') \Big). \end{aligned} \]

Based on this corollary, suppose we know that \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu_1\) are \((\epsilon, \delta)\)-close, and that \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_1'\) are \((\epsilon, \delta)\)-close, then \(\mu\) and \(\mu'\) are \((\epsilon', t \delta)\) close. If \(\epsilon \le 1\), then \[ \begin{array}{l} &e^{\epsilon'} = 1 + t \cdot (e^\epsilon - 1) \\ \Longleftrightarrow &e^{\epsilon'} - 1 = t \cdot (e^\epsilon - 1) \\ \Longleftrightarrow &\epsilon' \approx t \cdot \epsilon. \end{array} \]

In comparison, if we apply the joint convexity directly, we only get \[ D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu \Vert \mu' ) = t \cdot D_{e^{\epsilon}} ( \mu_1 \Vert \mu_1' ), \] and conclude that \(\mu\) and \(\mu'\) are \((\epsilon, t \delta)\)-close. There is no amplification of \(\epsilon\).

Reference

[1] B. Balle, G. Barthe, and M. Gaboardi, “Privacy Amplification by Subsampling: Tight Analyses via Couplings and Divergences,” arXiv:1807.01647 [cs, stat], Nov. 2018, Accessed: Apr. 29, 2022. [Online]. Available: http://arxiv.org/abs/1807.01647

k-Center

Posted on 2022-04-28

Problem

Let \(G = \langle V, E \rangle\) be an undirected and complete graph, with a distance \(d(v, u) \in \mathbb{R}^+\) between each pair of \(v, u \in V\). Assume that

  • \(d(v, v) = 0\) for each \(v \in V\);
  • \(d(v, u) = d(u, v)\) for each \(v, u \in V\);
  • The distances satisfy triangle inequality: \[ d(v, w) \le d(v, u) + d(u, w), \qquad \forall v, u, w \in V. \]

The goal is to find a set \(S \subset V\) with size \(|S| = k\), for given input parameter \(k \in \mathbb{N}\), to minimize \[ \max_{v \in V} d(v, S), \] where \(d(v, S) \doteq \min_{j \in S} d(v, u)\) is the distance between \(i\) and its nearest point in \(S\).

Approximation Algorithm

Algorithm \(\mathcal{A}\)
1. Pick arbitrary point \(v_1\) from \(V\)
2. \(S \leftarrow \{ v_1 \}\)
2. For \(t \leftarrow 2\) to \(k\) do
3. \(\qquad\) let \(v_t \doteq \underset{v \in V}{\arg \max} \, d(v, S)\)
4. \(\qquad\) \(S \leftarrow S \cup \{ v_t \}\)

Denote the optimal solution as \(S^* \doteq \{ v_1^*, \ldots, v_k^* \}\): \[ S^* \doteq \underset{S \subseteq V, |S| = k}{\arg \min} \, \, \left( \max_{v \in V} d(v, S) \right). \] We claim that the algorithm outputs a \(2\)-approximate solution to \(S^*\).

Theorem. Algorithm \(\mathcal{A}\) outputs a \(2\)-approximate solution: \[ \max_{v \in V} d(v, S) \le 2 \cdot \max_{v \in V} d(v, S^*). \]

Proof. We can partition the vertices in \(V\) into \(k\) subsets \(V_1^*, \ldots V_k^*\), such that \[ V_i^* \doteq \left\{ v \in V: \underset{v^* \in S^*}{\arg \min} \, d(v, v^*) = v_i^* \right\}, \qquad \forall i \in [k]. \]

Lemma. If \(u, w\) belongs to the same subset \(V_i^*\), then \[ d(u, w) \le d(u, v_i^*) + d(v_i^*, w) \le 2 \cdot \max_{v \in V} d(v, S^*). \]

We consider the following two cases:

Case One. For each \(i \in [k]\), \(|V_i^* \cap S| = 1\). Fix an \(i \in [k]\), and let \(v_t \in V_i^* \cap S\). Then for each \(u \in V_i^*\), via the lemma, \[ d(u, S) \le d(u, v_t) \le 2 \cdot \max_{v \in V} d(v, S^*). \] Since \(V_i^*, i \in [k]\) is a partition of \(V\), this holds for all \(u \in V\).

Case Two. There exists some \(V_i^*\) contains more than one points in \(S\). This implies that at some iteration, the algorithm \(\mathcal{A}\) selects a point \(v_t \in V_i^*\), even though it has selected a point \(v_t' \in V_i^*\) in an earlier iteration.

When \(v_t\) is selected, via the lemma, it holds that \[ d(v_t, S) \le d(v_t, v_t') \le 2 \cdot \max_{v \in V} d(v, S^*). \]

Since \(v_t\) is the farthest point from \(S\) when it is selected, all points are within a distance of \(2 \cdot \max_{v \in V} d(v, S^*)\) from \(S\). This remains true as the algorithm \(\mathcal{A}\) adds more points.

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] D. P. Williamson and D. B. Shmoys, The Design of Approximation Algorithms. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2011.
