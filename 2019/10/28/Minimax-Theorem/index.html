Minimax Theorem

Posted on 2019-10-28

A zero-sum game is a game in which a player’s payoff implies the loss of another player. It is specified by a matrix A∈Rn×mA \in R^{n \times m}A∈Rn×m. The row player can choose from rows {1,2,...,n}\{1, 2, ..., n\}{1,2,...,n} and the column player chooses from columns {1,2,...,m}\{1, 2, ..., m\}{1,2,...,m}. The entry aija_{ij}aij​ of AAA is the payoff when the row player chooses iii and the column player chooses jjj. In such case the column player loses aija_{ij}aij​.

If one player makes a choice first and the other play follows, then the second player can always respond according the first player choice, to maximize its payoff or minimize its loss.

To make the game more fair, we require the row player to act with strategy x∈Rnx \in R^nx∈Rn, i.e., a distribution on the the rows, and the column player to act with strategy y∈Rmy \in R^my∈Rm. At each round, the row player and column player act independently. Then the expected payoff is

xTAyx^TAy xTAy

But what is some player knows the other’s strategy? In this scenario, the row player choose its strategy xxx first and the column player responds by a strategy yyy, or vise versa. Note that at each round they still act independently according to their strategies. The question is, whether the order of choosing a strategy implies a difference in the expected payoff.

First we write down what the expected payoff is. If the row player commit to a strategy xxx first, the column player will choose a distribution yyy minimize it expected loss:

min⁡yxTAy\min_y x^T A y ymin​xTAy

As xxx is known, this is equivalent to choosing the column with minimum value:

min⁡j∈[m]xTAej\min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j j∈[m]min​xTAej​

Understanding the expected behavior of the column player, the row player should choose a strategy xxx, such that in the worst case, its expected payoff is maximized:

max⁡x(min⁡yxTAy)=max⁡x(min⁡j∈[m]xTAej)\max_{x} (\min_y x^T A y) = \max_{x} (\min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j) xmax​(ymin​xTAy)=xmax​(j∈[m]min​xTAej​)

Similarly, if the column player play first, the expected payoff is

min⁡y(max⁡xxTAy)=min⁡y(max⁡i∈[n]eiTAy) \min_y (\max_{x} x^T A y) = \min_{y} (\max_{i \in [n]} e_i^T A y) ymin​(xmax​xTAy)=ymin​(i∈[n]max​eiT​Ay)

The minimax-theorem states that

max⁡x(min⁡yxTAy)=max⁡x(min⁡j∈[m]xTAej)=min⁡y(max⁡xxTAy)=min⁡y(max⁡i∈[n]eiTAy)\begin{aligned} &\max_{x} (\min_y x^T A y) &= \max_{x} (\min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j) \\ = &\min_y (\max_{x} x^T A y) &= \min_{y} (\max_{i \in [n]} e_i^T A y) \end{aligned} =​xmax​(ymin​xTAy)ymin​(xmax​xTAy)​=xmax​(j∈[m]min​xTAej​)=ymin​(i∈[n]max​eiT​Ay)​
