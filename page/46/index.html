Polynomials

Posted on 2019-10-31 Edited on 2024-11-04

Introduction

Polynomials constitute of a rich class of functions. A polynomial in a single variable defined on a field \(\mathbb{F}\) is a function of the form: \[ p(x) = c_d x^d + c_{d - 1} x^{d - 1} + ... + c_1 x + c_0 \] where the variable \(x\) and coefficients \(c_i \in \mathbb{F}, \forall i \in [d]\). The integer \(d\) is called the degree of \(p(x)\).

Properties

Polynomials have two fundamental properties:

  1. A non-zero polynomial of degree \(d\) has at most \(d\) distinct roots.
  2. Given \(d + 1\) pairs \(\{ (a_i, b_i) \in \mathbb{F}^2 : i \in [d + 1] \}\), such that \(a_i \neq a_j\) for \(i \neq j\), there is a unique polynomial \(p(x)\) with degree at most \(d\) that goes through all these points.

Number of Distinct Roots

We prove claim 1 by induction.

  1. The base case is \(d = 0\). As the polynomial is non-zero, it does not have any root.

  2. Suppose the claim holds for non-negative integers \(\le d\) and \(p(x)\) is a polynomial of degree \(d + 1\). If \(p(x)\) has a root \(z\), by polynomial division, we can rewrite \(p(x)\) as \[ p(x) = (x - a)^k q(x) \] where \(k \ge 1\) is the largest integer such that \((x - a )^k | p(x)\). Hence \(q(x)\) has degree \(d + 1 - k\). For any \(z \neq a\), if \(p(z) = (z - a)^k q( z ) = 0\), then \(q(z) = 0\), which implies that \(z\) is a root of \(q(x)\). By the inductive hypothesis, it has at most \(d + 1 - k\) distinct roots.

Remark: We don't claim that \(p(x)\) must have a root, but it has at most \(d\) distinct roots, if it has degree \(d\).

Unique Interpolation

We first prove the existence of the interpolation, then prove its uniqueness.

Existence of Interpolation

The method is called Lagrange Interpolation. Our basic building blocks are a collection of special polynomials \(\{ \delta_i( x ) : i \in [d + 1] \}\), which we call signal functions, such that \(\forall i \in [d + 1]\),

  1. The polynomial \(\delta_i( x )\) has degree \(d\).

  2. The polynomial takes value 1 at \(a_i\), i.e., \(\delta_i(a_i) = 1\).

  3. The polynomial takes value 0 at \(a_j\) for \(j \neq i\), i.e., \(\delta_i(a_j) = 0\).

First, define \(\delta_i'(x) = \prod_{j \neq i} (x - a_j)\). It equals to \(0\) on \(a_j, \forall j \neq i\). We normalize its value at \(a_i\), to obtain

\[ \delta_i(x) = \frac{\delta_i'(x)}{\delta_i'(a_i)} = \frac{\prod_{j \neq i} (x - a_j) }{ \prod_{j \neq i} (a_i - a_j) }. \]

With proper scaling and summation, \(p(x)\) is given by \[ p(x) = \sum_{i = 1}^{d + 1} b_i \cdot \delta_i(x) \]

Uniqueness of Interpolation

Suppose for contradiction that there is another polynomial \(q(x)\), s.t., \(p(a_i) = q(a_i), \forall i \in [d + 1]\). Let \(r(x) = p(x) - q(x)\). If \(r(x) \equiv 0\), then the claim holds trivially. Otherwise, \(r(x)\) is a non-zero polynomial with degree at most \(d\). Claim 1 asserts that \(r(x)\) has at most \(d\) distinct roots, contradicting that \(r(a_i) = 0\) for \(i \in [d + 1]\).

Remark: uniqueness can also be proven by showing the following equation has unique solution \([ c_d, c_{d - 1}, ..., c_0]\): \[ \begin{bmatrix} a_1^d & a_1^{d - 1} & ... & a_1 & 1\\ a_2^d & a_2^{d - 1} & ... & a_2 & 1\\ ...\\ a_{d + 1}^d & a_{d + 1}^{d - 1} & ... & a_{d + 1} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_d \\ c_{d - 1} \\ ... \\ c_0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} b_1 \\ b_{1} \\ ...\\ b_{d + 1} \end{bmatrix} \\ \]

The left matrix is called Vandermonde Matrix, whose determinant is \[ \begin{aligned} &\det \begin{bmatrix} a_1^d & a_1^{d - 1} & ... & a_1 & 1\\ a_2^d & a_2^{d - 1} & ... & a_2 & 1\\ ...\\ a_{d + 1}^d & a_{d + 1}^{d - 1} & ... & a_{d + 1} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ =& \det \begin{bmatrix} 0& 0 & ... & 0 & 1\\ a_2^d - a_1 a_2^{d - 1} & a_2^{d - 1} - a_1 a_2^{d - 2}& ... & a_2 - a_1 & 1\\ ...\\ a_{d + 1}^d - a_1 a_{d + 1}^{d - 1} & a_{d + 1}^{d - 1} - a_1 a_{d + 1}^{d - 2}& ... & a_{d + 1} - a_ 1& 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ =&\prod_{i = 1}^{d + 1} (a_i - a_1) \det \begin{bmatrix} a_2^d & a_2^{d - 1} & ... & a_2 & 1\\ ...\\ a_{d + 1}^d & a_{d + 1}^{d - 1} & ... & a_{d + 1} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ ...\\ =& \prod_{i < j} (a_j - a_i) \end{aligned} \] which is non-zero when \(a_i \neq a_j\).

Applications

Pairwise Independent Hash Function.

Assume that we want to map a set of elements \(\mathcal{U} \doteq \{0, 1, ..., u - 1\}\) into some set \(\mathbb{F}\), by picking a function \(h\) uniformly at random from a family of functions \(\mathcal{H} \doteq \{ h : \mathcal{U} \rightarrow \mathbb{F} \}\). For an \(i \in \mathcal{U}\), the value \(Y_i \doteq h(i)\) is a random variable. The \(Y_i\)'s are called pairwise independent, if for \(i, j \in \mathcal{U}, i \neq j\) and \(b_i, b_j \in \mathbb{F}\), \[ \Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} } [Y_i = b_i, Y_j = b_j] = \Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} } [Y_i = b_i] \Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} } [Y_j = b_j]. \]

Similarly we can define \(k\)-wise independence. For any \(0 \le i_1 < i_2 <... < i_k < u\), and \(b_{i_1} , b_{i_2}, ..., b_{i_k} \in \mathbb{F}\), we have \[ \Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} } [ Y_{i_1} = b_{i_1}, ... Y_{i_k} = b_{i_k} ] = \prod_{j} \Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} } [ Y_{i_j} = b_{i_j}]. \]

Finite Field \(\mathbb{F}_p\)

Now we restrict our discussion to the finite field \(\mathbb{F}_p = \{0, 1, ..., p - 1\}\), where \(p\) is a prime larger than \(u\).

Theorem. The set of all possible functions from \(\mathcal{U}\) to \(\mathbb{F}_p\) is pairwise independent.

Proof: \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[Y_i = a, Y_j = b] = \frac{p^{u - 2}}{p^u} = \frac{1}{p^2} = \Pr[Y_i = a] \cdot \Pr[Y_j = b]. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

However, each of the functions takes \(u \log p\) bits to represent. To reduce the space, we restrict to the family: \[ \mathcal{H_1} = \{ p(x) = c_1 x + c_0 \mid c_1, c_0 \in \mathbb{F}_p \} \]

which contains all polynomials with degree at most 1 in the field \(\mathbb{F}_p\), i.e., all lines in \(\mathbb{F}_p\). There are \(p^2\) such functions.

We know that, for given \(x_1, x_2 \in \mathcal{U}, x_1 \neq x_2\), then \(\forall y_1, y_2 \in \mathbb{F}_p\), there is a unique line that goes through \((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)\). If we pick a line uniformly at random from \(\mathcal{H}_1\), then the probability that it goes through this pair of points is \({1} / {p^2}\).

This already implies pairwise independence, as \[ \Pr[h(x_1) = y_1 ] = \sum_{y_2 = 0}^{p - 1} \Pr[h(x_1) = y_1, h(x_2) = y_2] = \frac{1}{p} \]

In a similar manner, we can construct a \(k\)-wise independent family: \[ \mathcal{H_{k - 1}} = \{ p(x) = c_{k - 1} x^{k - 1} + c_{k - 2} x^{k - 2} + ... + c_1 x_1 + c_0 \mid c_{k - 1}, ..., c_0 \in \mathbb{F}_p \}. \]

Note that \(|\mathcal{H}| = p^{k}\). For \(0 \le x_1 < x_2 < ... < x_k < u\), and \(y_1, y_2, ..., y_k \in \mathbb{F}_p\), there is a unique polynomial that goes through the points \((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), ..., (x_k, y_k)\).

Key-Sharing

Suppose that we have \(n\)-candidates who knows some information about the key and we require that only when no less than \(k\) of them aggragate their information can they figure the actual key. No group of less than \(k\) candidates can achieve this. The way we do it is as follows:

  1. Select a large prime number \(p\).
  2. Select \(k+1\) random numbers \(y_0, y_1, ..., y_k\) from \([p-1]\).
  3. Generate the unique polynomial \(p(x)\) with degree \(k\) that goes through \((0, y_0), (1, y_1), ..., (k, y_k)\).
  4. Inform the value of \(p(1)\) to the first person, \(p(2)\) to the second one, ... and \(p(n)\) to the \(n\)-th person.

If \(k - 1\) people pool their keys together, then there are \(p\) possible polynomials that goes through all their keys, each has different value at point 0. The probability of guessing the correct key is given by \(\frac{1}{p}\).

Minimax Theorem

Posted on 2019-10-28 Edited on 2021-07-14

A zero-sum game is a game in which a player's payoff implies the loss of another player. There is a payoff a matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}\). One player chooses from rows \(\{1, 2, ..., n\}\) and the other chooses from columns \(\{1, 2, ..., m\}\). The former is called the row player and the later is called the column player. The entry \(a_{ij}\) of \(A\) is the payoff when the row player chooses \(i\) and the column player chooses \(j\). In such case the column player loses \(a_{ij}\).

If one player makes a choice first and the other play follows, then the second player can always respond according the first player choice, to maximize its payoff or minimize its loss.

To make the game more fair, suppose that there are two huge black boxes. We require the row player to go into one black box, and write down her choice \(i\) on a note, and then leave the black box. We require the column player to do the same in another black box. Suppose the row player chooses his row index according to some distribution \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and the column player chooses according to distribution \(y \in \mathbb{R}^m\). The \(x\) and \(y\) are called strategies and the expected payoff is (for the row player) is \[ x^T A y. \]

What is one player knows the other's strategy? In this scenario, the row player choose its strategy \(x\) first and the column player responds by a strategy \(y\), or vise versa. Obvious, the strategy of the player who plays first can not be deterministic, i.e., have probability \(1\) on one index and \(0\) on others.

The question is, whether the order of choosing a strategy results in different expected payoff.

First we write down what the expected payoff is. If the row player commit to a strategy \(x\) first, the column player will choose a distribution \(y\) minimize it expected loss: \[ \min_y x^T A y. \]

As \(x\) is known, this is equivalent to choosing the column with minimum value: \[ \min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j. \]

Understanding the expected behavior of the column player, the row player should choose a strategy \(x\), such that in the worst case, its expected payoff is maximized: \[ \max_{x} (\min_y x^T A y) = \max_{x} (\min_{j \in [m]} x^T A e_j). \]

Similarly, if the column player play first, the expected payoff is \[ \min_y (\max_{x} x^T A y) = \min_{y} (\max_{i \in [n]} e_i^T A y). \]

The minimax-theorem states that \[ \begin{aligned} \max_{x} (\min_y x^T A y) = &\min_y (\max_{x} x^T A y). \end{aligned} \]

The proof is not difficult -- by formulating the two optimizing problems as linear programming, and observing that they are dual to each other. If the row player goes first, \[ \begin{array}{lr} \max v \\ v - \sum_{i = 1}^n a_{ij} x_i \le 0 \,, & \forall j \in [m] \\ \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i = 1 \\ x_i \ge 0 \,, & \forall i \in [n] \end{array} \]

The first constraint just says that \(v\) should be smaller than each column of \(x^T A\).

To upper bound \(v\), we introduce a variable \(u \in \mathbb{R}\) (that is associated with constraint \(\sum_{i = 1}^n x_i = 1\)). Then \[ \begin{array}{ll} u &= u \left( \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i \right) \\ &\ge u \left( \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i \right) + \sum_{j = 1}^m y_j \left( v - \sum_{i = 1}^n a_{ij} x_i \right) \\ &= v \left( \sum_{j = 1}^m y_j \right) + \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i \left( u - \sum_{j = 1}^m a_{ij} y_j \right) \\ &= v + \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i \left( u - \sum_{j = 1}^m a_{ij} y_j \right) \\ \end{array} \]

The first equality follows from that \(\sum_{i = 1}^n x_i = 1\). The second inequality follows from that \(y_j \ge 0\) and \(v - \sum_{i = 1}^n a_{ij} x_i \le 0\). The last equality follows from that \(\sum_{j = 1}^m y_j = 1\).

If it holds that \(u - \sum_{j = 1}^m a_{ij} y_j \ge 0\) for all \(i \in [n]\), as \(x_i \ge 0\), then \(u\) is an upper bound of \(v\): \[ \begin{array}{ll} u \ge v + \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i \left( u - \sum_{j = 1}^m a_{ij} y_j \right) \ge v. \end{array} \]

Minimizing \(u\) gives the following linear program: \[ \begin{array}{lr} \min u \\ u - \sum_{j = 1}^m a_{ij} y_j \ge 0 \,, & \forall i \in [n] \\ \sum_{i = 1}^m y_j = 1 \\ y_j \ge 0 \,, & \forall j \in [m] \end{array} \]

Observe this is exactly \(\min_y \max_x x^T A y\). Further, by strong duality, we know that the optimal values of the two program are equal.

Balanced Binary Search Tree

Posted on 2019-10-08 Edited on 2019-10-10

It is the depth of the binary search that affects the performance of it. We say that a binary search tree with \(n\) nodes is balanced if its depth is of order \(O(\log n)\) (hence we can perform find, insert and delete operations in time \(O(\log n)\)).

If a tree is unbalanced, we can maintain the balance by rotation. Further, we need some criteria to guide the rotation. In what's follows we discuss some of the popular design of the criteria.

By the number of elements of the subtree.

Denote a note \(v\) and its left and right child \(v.left\) and \(v.right\) respectively. Define the size of \(v\) (\(s(v)\)) the number of elements contained in the subtree rooted at \(v\).

A critical observation is that, if for each node \(v\) of the tree, the size of each of it subtrees is at most a constant fraction \(\big(\) denoted as \(\epsilon \in (\frac{1}{2}, 1)\); Exercise: why can't we pick \(\epsilon \in [0, \frac{1}{2}] \big)\) of its size, i.e., \[ s(v.left) \le \epsilon s(v) \\ s(v.right) \le \epsilon s(v) \] then the depth of the tree is in the order of \(O(\log n)\). The reason is obvious: let \(r = v_0, v_1, v_2, ..., v_k\) be any root-to-leaf path of the tree, then \[ s(v_1) \le \epsilon s(v_0) \\ s(v_2) \le \epsilon s(v_1) \\ ...\\ s(v_k) \le \epsilon s(v_{k - 1}) \] it follows that \(1 = s(v_k) \le \epsilon^k s(v_0) = \epsilon^k n\) and \(k \le \log_\epsilon \frac{1}{n} = O(\log n)\).

Therefore, we can pick arbitrary \(\epsilon \in (\frac{1}{2}, 1)\) and maintain this criteria explicitly and recursively.

WLOG, suppose a new element is inserted into the left subtree of a vertex \(v\). Denote the new size function \(s\) as \(s'\) after insertion. If after insertion, \(s'(v.left) \le \epsilon s'(v)\), we do nothing and check the balance of \(v\)'s parent (if there is any). Otherwise, we may try a right rotation on \(v\): \[ \begin{aligned} &\qquad \qquad v \\ &/ &\setminus \\ &v.left &&v.right \\ / &\setminus \\ v.left.left & \quad v.left.right \end{aligned} \] the subtree transform into: \[ \begin{aligned} &\qquad \qquad v.left \\ &/ &\setminus \\ v.left.left && v\\ &&/ &\setminus \\ && v.left.right & \quad v.right \end{aligned} \] Since before insertion, \(s(v.left) \le \epsilon s(v)\), it holds that \(s'(v.left.left) \le \epsilon s'(v.left) = \epsilon (s(v) + 1)\). Therefore, the left subtree no longer violate the balance constraint. However, the constraint could be violated by the right subtree. Since it is possible that \[ s'(v.left.right) + s'(v.right) + 1 > \epsilon s'(v.left) = \epsilon (s(v) + 1) \] For example, suppose that \(\epsilon = 0.8\) and \(s(v.left.right) = 0.8 s(v.left) = 0.64 s(v)\) and \(s(v.right) = 0.2 s(v) - 1\), then \[ s(v.left.right) + s(v.right) + 1 = 0.84 s(v) \] The reason is that there is so much weight that \(v.left.right\) has. Therefore, we don't want to rotate it to the right subtree. An alternative is to perform a left rotate on the left subtree first \[ \begin{aligned} &\qquad \qquad v \\ &/ &\setminus \\ &v.left.right &&v.right \\ / &\setminus \\ v.left & \quad v.left.right.right \\ / \setminus & \\ v.left.left & \quad v.left.right.left \end{aligned} \] Followed by a right rotation on \(v\): \[ \begin{aligned} &\qquad \qquad v.left.right \\ &/ &\setminus \\ &v.left &&v \\ / &\setminus &&/ \setminus\\ v.left.left &\quad v.left.right.left& \quad v.left.right.right &\quad v.right\\ \end{aligned} \]

To satisfy the balance requirement, we require that

  1. \(s''(v.left.right.left) = s'(v.left.right.left) \le \epsilon s''(v.left) = \epsilon (s'(v.left) - s'(v.left.right.right) -1 )\).
  2. \(s'(v.left.left) \le s'(v.left)\)
  3. \(s'(v.left.right.right) + s'(v.right) + 1 \le \epsilon s'(v.left.right)\)

Condition 1 implies that \(s'(v.left.right.right)\) can take up too much weight of \(s'(v.left)\), which imposes an upper bound on \(\epsilon\) by \[ (1 - \epsilon) \epsilon s'(v.left) - 1 \le \epsilon (s'(v.left) - \epsilon^2s'(v.left) - 1) \]
