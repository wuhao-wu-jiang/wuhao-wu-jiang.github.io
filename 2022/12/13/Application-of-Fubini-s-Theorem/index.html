  2. In the above, we operate on the product space $(\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B}, \mu \times \nu) = (\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)$, instead of $(\Omega \times \Omega, \mathcal{F} \times \mathcal{F}, \lambda \times \lambda) = (\Omega^2, \mathcal{F}^2, \lambda^2)$.

  3. There are various interpretations of $\mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ]$:

  • $f(X, Y)$ can be viewed as a function from $\Omega$ to $\mathbb{R}$, given by $\omega \rightarrow f( X(\omega), Y(\omega) )$.
    It is easy to see that it is measurable $\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{B}$, and therefore is a random variable.

  • Alternative, we can view $f(x, y)$ as a measurable function from the probability space $(\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)$, to $\mathbb{R}$.
    It is a random variable, and

  • It can be proven that the two integrals are equal:

    • If $f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in A \times B]$, for some $A, B \in \mathcal{B}$, then by independence of $X$ and $Y$, both integrals equal $\mu(A) \cdot \nu(B)$.

    • Since $\mathcal{S} \doteq { A \times B: A, B \in \mathcal{B} }$ generates $\mathcal{B}^2$, i.e., $\sigma(\mathcal{S}) = \mathcal{B}^2$, applying $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem, if $f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M]$ for some $M \in \mathcal{B}^2$, the two integral are equal.

    • By linearity of integration, if $f(x, y)$ is $\mathcal{B}^2$ simple function, the two integral are equal.

    • By monotone convergence theorem, if $f(x, y)$ is a non-negative measurable function, the two integral are equal.

    • By decomposing $f$ into non-negative and negative parts, if $f(x, y)$ is an integrable function, the two integral are equal.
