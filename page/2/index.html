Replicated Secret Sharing

Posted on 2025-01-05 Edited on 2025-01-09

Secret Sharing

Let \(s\) be a secret data from some finite filed \(\mathbb{F}\), which are shared among \(n\) parties \(P = \{ P_1, \ldots , P_n \}\), such that party \(P_i\) holds some processed data \(s_i\), called shadows, for each \(i \in [n]\).

We require that only qualified subsets of parties can reconstruct the original secret \(s\), with their process data. The collection of qualified subsets, denoted by \(\mathcal{A} \subseteq 2^P\), is called the access structure.

It is easy to see that if \(A\) is a qualified subset and \(A \subseteq A'\), then so is \(A'\).

Property (A). If \(A \subseteq \mathcal{A}\) and \(A \subseteq A'\), then \(A' \subseteq \mathcal{A}\).

The family of maximal sets in \(\mathcal{A}\) is denoted by \[ \partial^+ A \doteq \{ A \in \mathcal{A}: \nexists A' \in \mathcal{A}, \text{ s.t. } A \subseteq A' \} . \]

Multiple Assignment Scheme

  1. Choose two integers \(k, m\) and a prime number \(q\) such that \(k < m < q\).

  2. Choose \(a_1, \ldots, a_{k - 2} \in \mathbb{F}_q\) and \(a_{k - 1} \in \mathbb{F}_q^+\) randomly.

  3. Let \(f(x) = s + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_{k - 1} x^{k - 1}\).

  4. Choose distinct elements \(x_1, \ldots, x_m \in \mathbb{F}_q^+\), let \(y_i = f(x_j)\) for \(j \in [m]\), and let \(S = \{ (x_1, y_1), \ldots , (x_m, y_m) \}\).

  5. For each \(i \in [n]\), choose \(s_i \subseteq S\), and assign \(s_i\) to \(P_i\).

The multiple assignment scheme has the following access structure \[ \mathcal{A} = \{ A \subseteq [n] : | \cup_{i \in A} s_i | \ge k \}. \]

Theorem. For each \(\mathcal{A} \subseteq 2^P\) with Property \((A)\), there exists a set \(S\), a function \(g: P \rightarrow 2^S\) and a non-negative integer \(k\) such that \(\mathcal{A}(g, k) = \mathcal{A}\).

Proof. Let \(B = 2^P - \mathcal{A}\). By (A), \(B\) satisfies the following property:

  1. \(B \in \mathcal{B}\) and \(B' \subseteq B\) imply \(B' \in \mathcal{B}\).

We now construct a set \(S\) so that each element of \(S\) corresponds one-to-one to a maximal set of \(B\). That is, we define \[ S = \{ d_s : B \in \partial^+ \mathcal{B} \}, \] where \(d_s \neq d_s'\) if \(B\) and \(B' \in \partial^+ \mathcal{B}\) are distinct. Define \(g: P \rightarrow 2^S\) by \[ g(p) = \{ d_s: B \in \partial^+ \mathcal{B}, p \notin B \}. \] We claim \(A(g, k) = \mathcal{A}\) if \(k = |\partial^+ \mathcal{B}| = |S|\).

We first show \(A \subseteq A(g, k)\). Assume to the contrary that there exists \(Q \in A\) such that \(Q \nsubseteq A(g, k)\). Then \(\bigcup_{p \in Q} g(p) \neq S\) since \(k = |S|\). Thus \(d_B \in S - \bigcup_{p \in Q} g(p)\) for some \(B \in \partial^+ \mathcal{B}\). Therefore for every \(p \in Q, d_B \notin g(p)\) and so \(p \in B\). Hence \(Q \supset B\). By the property (b) \(Q \in \mathcal{B}\). Thus \(Q \in \mathcal{A} \cap \mathcal{B}\), contradicting the definition of \(B\).

Next, we show that \(A(g, k) \subseteq \mathcal{A}\). Assume to the contrary that there exists \(Q \in A(g, k)\) such that \(Q \nsubseteq A\). Since \(Q (/. A\), \(Q EB\) and hence \(Q \subseteq B\) for some \(B \in \partial^+ B\).

By the definition of \(g\), \(d_s (/. g(p)\) for all \(p \in Q\). Therefore \(d_B \notin \bigcup_{p \in Q} g(p)\), and hence \(Q \nsubseteq A(g, k)\), a contradiction. \(\square\)

Let \(\Gamma \subseteq 2^{[n]}\) be a (monotone) access structure, and let \(\mathcal{T}\) include all maximal unqualified sets of \(\Gamma\). The replicated scheme for \(\Gamma\), denoted \(\mathcal{R}_\Gamma\),proceeds as follows. To share a secret \(s \in K\), the dealer first additively share \(s\) into \(|\mathcal{T}|\) shares, each labelled by a different set from \(\mathcal{T}\); that is, it lets \(s = \sum_{T \in \mathcal{T}} r_T\) where the shares \(r_T\) are otherwise random field elements. Then, the dealer distributes to each player \(P_j\) all shares \(r_T\) such that \(j \notin T\); that is, \(P_j\)’s share vector is \((r_T)_{T \notni j}\). Privacy follows from the fact that members of every maximal unqualified set \(T \in \mathcal{T}\) jointly miss exactly one additive share, namely the share \(r_T\) (hence members of any unqualified set miss at least one share).

On the other hand, since \(\Gamma\) is monotone, a qualified set \(Q \in \Gamma\) cannot be contained in any unqualified set; hence, members of \(Q\) jointly view all shares \(r_T\) and can thus reconstruct \(s\).

\(t\)-private Replicated Secret Sharing Scheme

\[ s = \sum_{A \subseteq [n]: |A| = t} r_A \]

In this case, we have

  • \(\Gamma = \{ B \subseteq [n] : |B| > t\}\)
  • \(\mathcal{T} = \{ A \subseteq [n]: |A| = t \}\)

To locally convert these shares into shares of \(s\) according to the \(t\)-private Shamir scheme, we assign to player \(P_i\) the point \(i\) in the field. Now, for each set \(A \subseteq [n]\) of cardinality \(n - t\), let \(f_A\) be the (unique) degree-t polynomial such that:

  1. \(f_A(0) = 1\) and
  2. \(f_A(i) = 0\) for all \(i \in [n] \setminus A\).

Each player \(P_j\) can compute a share \(s_j\) as follows: \[ s_j = \sum_{ A \subseteq [n] : |A|=n - t, j \in A} r_A \cdot f_A(j). \] We claim that this results in a set of shares from Shamir’s scheme, consistent with the original secret \(s\). To see this, define a polynomial \[ f = \sum_{A \subseteq [n] : |A|=n - t} r_A \cdot f_A. \] Clearly, \(f\) has degree (at most) \(t\), and it is straightforward to verify that condition 1) above on the \(f_A\)’s implies \(f(0) = s\) and condition 2) implies \(f(j) = s_j\).

Applications

A secret \(s\) has been shared according to the \(t\)-private replicated scheme, namely \(s = A \subseteq [n] : |A| = n - t\) \(r_A\) where \(r_A\) has been given to all players in \(A\). To locally convert these shares into Shamir shares, each player \(P_j\) computes its share as \(s_j = |A| = n - t,j \in A\) \(r_A \cdot f_A(j)\), where \(f_A\) is a degree-\(t\) polynomial determined by \(A\). The main observation is that when the secret \(s\) is random, all replicated shares \(r_A\) will be random and independent. Hence we may use the initially distributed \(r_A\) as keys to a PRF \(\psi (\cdot)\), and as long as players agree on a common input a to the function, all players in A can compute \(\psi r_A (a)\) and use it in the above construction in place of \(r_A\). Concretely, we get the following.

Protocol Pseudorandom Secret-Sharing (PRSS) Common inputs: a value a and independent keys \(\{ r_A \}\) that have been predistributed as above. Each player \(P_j\) computes his share \(s_j\) as: \[ s_j = A \subseteq[n] : |A| = n - t, j \in A \psi r_A (a) \cdot f_A(j) \]

The technique for pseudo-random secret-sharing can be generalized to create sharings of a particular value, such as zero. We explain how this is done for the same threshold \(t\) access structure as before, but for polynomials of degree \(2t\), since this is what we need in the following. Generalizations to other degrees follow easily. Consider a set \(A\) of size \(n - t\) and consider the set of polynomials \[ F_A = \{ f | deg(f) \le 2t, f(0) = 0, j \in A \Rightarrow f(j) = 0 \}. \] If we think of the set of all degree-\(2t\) polynomials as a vector space over \(K\), it is easy to see that \(F_A\) is a subspace of dimension \(2t + 1 - t - 1 = t\). So we choose for each \(A\), once and for all, a basis for \(F_A\) consisting of \(t\) polynomials \[ f_A 1 , ..., f_A t \]

Finally, we distribute initially \(t\) keys \(r_A 1 , ..., r_A t\) to every player in \(A\). This leads to the following protocol:

Protocol. Pseudorandom Zero-Sharing (PRZS) Common input: a value \(a\), keys \(\{ r_A i |i = 1..t, |A| = n - 1 \}\) that have been predistributed as above. Each player \(P_j\) computes his share \(s_j\) as: \[ s_j = A \subseteq[n] : |A|=n - t, j \in A t i=1 \psi r_A i (a) \cdot f_A i (j) \] It is straightforward to verify that this results in shares consistent with the polynomial \(f_0 = A, |A| = n - t, P_j \in A t i = 1 \psi si A (a) \cdot f_A i\), that \(deg(f0) \le 2t\) and that \(f_0(0) = 0\).

Let \(p\) be some prime number, \(\mathbb{F}_p\) be a finite field of order \(p\), and \(\vec{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{F}_p^n\). Let \(\ell \doteq \lceil -1 + \log_2 (p + 1) \rceil.\) For each \(x_i\), denote its binary representation as \(\mathbb{B}in(x_i) = (b_{i, \ell}, b_{i, \ell - 1}, \ldots, b_{i, 0})\), s.t., \[ x_i \doteq \sum_{j \in 0}^\ell b_{i, j} \cdot 2^j. \]

Permutation. A permutation \(\pi : [n] \leftrightarrow [n]\) is a bijection between \([n]\) and \([n]\).

We can apply a permutation \(\pi\) to a vector \(\vec{x}\), to obtain a new vector \(\pi(\vec{x}) \doteq (x_{\pi(1)}, \ldots, x_{\pi(n)}).\)

We present an alternative view of the radix sort.

Algorithm (Radix Sort)
1. For \(j \leftarrow 0\) to \(\ell\) do
2. \(\quad\) Obtain a permutation \(\pi\), s.t., \(\pi(\vec{x})\) is a stable sort of \(\vec{x}\) according to the \(j^{(th)}\) bit. 3. \(\quad\) Apply the permutation \(\vec{x} \leftarrow \pi(\vec{x})\)

It remains to convert this into a secure sorting protocol, for three parties. We need to show that at each iteration,

  1. A permutation \(\pi\) can be generated in a secure manner, and
  2. The permutation \(\pi\) can be applied in a secure manner.

Generating Secure Permutation for Stable Sort

In this section, we discuss the protocol for stably sorting \(\vec{x}\) according to the \(j^{(\text{th})}\) bits. This reduces to designing a stable sorting protocol for an array \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\) of binary bits.

The key observation is that, for each \(a_i\), we can explicitly compute its position, i.e., \(\pi(i)\). In particular, for each \(i\), define \[ \begin{aligned} s_0(i) &\doteq \{ j \in [i - 1] : a_j = 0 \}, \\ s_1(i) &\doteq \{ j \in [i - 1] : a_j = 1 \}. \end{aligned} \] i.e., \(s_0(i)\) and \(s_1(i)\) are the number of zeros and ones before \(a_i\), respectively. If \(a_i = 0\), after sorting, it should be place in the \(s_i(0)\)-th position, and if \(a_i = 1\), it should be placed in the \(\big( s_i(0) + s_i(1) \big)\)-th position.

The above description actually describe the inverse of the permutation \(\pi\), which stably sorts the input array \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\), which is given by the following formula: \[ \pi^{-1} (i) = s_0(i) + s_1(i) \cdot a_i, \quad \forall i \in [n]. \]

Algorithm.
1. \(\llbracket s_0 \rrbracket \leftarrow 0\)
2. \(\llbracket s_1 \rrbracket \leftarrow 0\)
3. For \(i \in [n]\) do
4. \(\quad\) \(\llbracket s_0 \rrbracket \leftarrow \llbracket s_0 \rrbracket + (1 - \llbracket a_i \rrbracket)\)
5. \(\quad\) \(\llbracket s_1 \rrbracket \leftarrow \llbracket s_1 \rrbracket + \llbracket a_i \rrbracket\)
6. \(\quad\) \(\llbracket \pi^{-1}(i) \rrbracket \leftarrow \llbracket s_0 \rrbracket + \llbracket s_1 \rrbracket \cdot \llbracket a_i \rrbracket\)
7. return \(\llbracket \pi^{-1} \rrbracket \leftarrow \big( \llbracket \pi^{-1} (1) \rrbracket, \ldots, \llbracket \pi^{-1} (n) \rrbracket \big)\).

The above protocol involves only addition and multiplication of secret shares.

Applying a Permutation

The above protocol compute \(\pi^{-1}\) as secret shares. The following question is, how can we apply it to \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\), to obtain a sorted sequence. We don't want to recover \(\pi^{-1}\) from its secret shares, as \(\pi^{-1}\) itself contains information about \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\).

We can apply a special trick to get around this. Assume that \(\sigma\) is a random permutation. Consider applying it to both \(\pi^{-1}\), and \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\): \[ \begin{array}{ccc} \sigma(\pi^{-1}) &= \quad (& \pi^{-1}(\sigma(1)), &\ldots, &\, \pi^{-1}(\sigma(n)) &), \\ \sigma(a_1, \ldots, a_n) &= \quad (& a_{\sigma(1)}, &\ldots, &\, a_{\sigma_n} &). \end{array} \] Observe that, since \(\pi^{-1}(i)\) specifies the position of \(a_i\), and \(\sigma\) is a bijection, \(\pi^{-1}(\sigma(i))\) specifies exactly the position of \(a_{\sigma(i)}\) for each \(i \in [n]\).

BGW Protocol

Posted on 2024-11-09 Edited on 2025-08-27

The BGW is a secure multiparty computation protocol for polynomials over a finite field.

Model

Let \(p\) be a prime number, and let \(\mathbb{F}_p\) be a finite field of order \(p\).

In the setting, there are \(n\) parties, where the party \(i\) holds data \(b_{i, 0} \in \mathbb{F}_p\). The objective is to compute a polynomial \(f (x_1, \ldots, x_n)\) over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) in a privacy-preserving manner.

Privacy Guarantee. We assume all parties are curious but honest. A protocol is considered \(t\)-private, if any set of up to \(t\) players cannot learn more from protocol's outcome than they could jointly infer from their own private inputs and outputs.

Protocol

We need the following protocol as a primitive for sharing information between the parties.

Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme \(\mathcal{S}\)
Party \(i \in [n] :\)
1. Sample independent random primes \(b_{i, 1}, \ldots, b_{i, t} \in \mathbb{F}_p\).
2. Let \(\vec{b}_i \doteq (b_{i, 0}, b_{i, 1}, \ldots, b_{i, t}).\)
3. Let \(g_{\vec{b}_i} (x) \doteq b_{i, 0} + b_{i, 1} \cdot x + \ldots + b_{i, t} \cdot x^t\).
4. For each user \(j \in [n]\):
5. \(\quad\) Send \(g_{\vec{b}_i} (j)\) to user \(j\)

We call \(g_{\vec{b}_i} (j)\) a share of the secret \(b_{i, 0}\).

Privacy Guarantee. It is easy to verify that \(\mathcal{S}\) is \(t\)-private.

The key result we would like to establish is that a polynomial \(f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)\) can also be computed in a \(t\)-private manner.

Since a polynomial can be constructed through additions, scalar multiplication, and the multiplication of (smaller) polynomials, it is sufficient to demonstrate how these operations can be performed in a \(t\)-private manner. We begin by discussing the addition protocol. Protocols for subtraction and scalar multiplication can be derived using similar techniques. Finally, we present the multiplication protocol.

Addition

We start with a simple protocol, which tries to add up \(b_{k, 0}\) and \(b_{\ell, 0}\), for some parties \(k, \ell \in [n]\).

Adding \(b_{k, 0}\) and \(b_{\ell, 0}\):

Party \(k\):

  • Invoke \(\mathcal{S}\) to send \(g_{\vec{b}_k}(j)\) to each \(j \in [n]\).

Party \(\ell\):

  • Invoke \(\mathcal{S}\) to send \(g_{\vec{b}_\ell} (j)\) to each \(j \in [n]\).

Party \(i \in [n]\):

  • On receiving \(g_{\vec{b}_k}(i)\) and \(g_{\vec{b}_\ell}(i)\), compute \(g_{\vec{b}_k}(i) + g_{\vec{b}_\ell}(i)\).

The key observation is that the following relation holds for polynomials: \[ \begin{aligned} g_{\vec{b}_k}(x) + g_{\vec{b}_\ell}(x) &= g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(x) \\ &= (b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0}) + (b_{k, 1} + b_{\ell, 1}) \cdot x + \ldots + (b_{k, t} + b_{\ell, t}) \cdot x^t. \end{aligned} \]

Composition in the View of a Virtual Party

Thus, the addition operation can be viewed as a virtual party \(v\) holding data \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0}\), which is then shared with all parties \(i \in [n]\) via the Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme. In this scheme, the polynomial coefficients are \(\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell\). Consequently, each party \(i\) receives a share of the secret, denoted as \(g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(i)\).

Virtual Party \(v\): * Invoke \(\mathcal{S}\) to send \(g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(j)\) to each \(j \in [n]\).

This alternative view facilitates a clearer understanding of composition.

For example, suppose we want to compute \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0} + b_{m, 0}\) for some parties \(k, \ell, m \in [n]\). First, we can apply the protocol described above to compute \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0}\).

Then, we consider two parties: a virtual party \(v\) holding data \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0}\), and party \(m\) with data \(b_{m, 0}\). We can then invoke the addition protocol again to compute \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0} + b_{m, 0}\). Importantly, communication with the virtual party is not actually required, as after the first protocol invocation, each party \(i\) already possesses the secret share \(g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(j)\).

Privacy Guarantee. After the addition protocol, each party \(i\) receives a share of the secret \(b_{k, 0} + b_{\ell, 0}\), represented by \(g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(i)\). Since \(g_{\vec{b}_k + \vec{b}_\ell}(x)\) is a random polynomial of degree \(t\), the output of the addition protocol remains \(t\)-private.

Multiplication

The multiplication is more intricate. Note that: \[ \begin{aligned} g_{\vec{b}_k}(x) \cdot g_{\vec{b}_\ell}(x) &= g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(x) \\ &= \left( b_{k, 0} \cdot b_{\ell, 0} \right) + \left(\sum_{i, j \ge 0, \, i + j = 1} b_{k, i} \cdot b_{\ell, j}\right) \cdot x + \cdots + \left(\sum_{i, j \ge 0, \, i + j = 2 t} b_{k, i} \cdot b_{\ell, j}\right) \cdot x^{2 t}. \end{aligned} \]

There are two problems with this polynomial:

  1. The coefficients of this polynomial are not independent.
  2. The polynomial has degree \(2t\), instead of \(t\).

Hence the previous security analysis of the Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme does not hold.

Rerandomization

To fix the first problem, each party \(i\) can generate a random polynomial \[ g_i (x) = 0 + c_{i, 1} \cdot x + c_{i, 2} \cdot x^2 + \cdots + c_{i, 2t} \cdot x^{2t}, \] then they sends the shares \(g_i(1), \ldots, g_i(n)\) to other parties.

After sending and receiving new shares, each party \(i\) then computes \(g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(i) + \sum_{j \in [n]} g_j(i)\). The polynomial of interest is given by

\[ \begin{aligned} g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(x) + \sum_{i \in [n]} g_i (x) &= \left( b_{k, 0} \cdot b_{\ell, 0} \right) + \left(\sum_{i, j \ge 0, \, i + j = 1} b_{k, i} \cdot b_{\ell, j} + \sum_{i \in [n]} c_{i, 1} \right) \cdot x + \cdots + \left(\sum_{i, j \ge 0, \, i + j = 2 t} b_{k, i} \cdot b_{\ell, j} + \sum_{i \in [n]} c_{i, 2t} \right) \cdot x^{2 t}. \end{aligned} \]

Note this is a random polynomial, in the sense that the coefficients of this polynomial are independent. Therefore, the outputs \(g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(i) + \sum_{j \in [n]} g_j(i), i \in [n]\) are \(2t\)-private.

Remark. It appears sufficient for one party \(i\) (if trusted) to generate a random polynomial \(g_i\) and share \(g_i(1), \ldots, g_i(n)\) with other parties. Essentially, this adds a random coefficient to each coefficient of \(g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(x)\).

Truncation

To simplify notation, let \[ h(x) \doteq c_0 + c_1 \cdot x + \ldots + c_{2t} \cdot x^{2t} \] be a shorthand for the polynomial \(g_{\vec{b}_k \times \vec{b}_\ell}(x) + \sum_{i \in [n]} g_i (x)\). We have shown that \(c_1, \ldots, c_n\) are random integers from \(\mathbb{F}_p\).

Our goal is to truncate its degree from \(2t\) to \(t\): \[ \bar{h}(x) \doteq c_0 + c_1 \cdot x + \ldots + c_{t} \cdot x^{t}, \] and for each party \(i \in [n]\), replaces its share \(h(i)\) by \(\bar{h}(i)\).

A naive approach is to reveal the secret shares of a set of \(2t\) clients, for example \(h(1), \ldots, h(2 t)\), so that we can recover the entire polynomial \(h(x)\) (and therefore the secret value \(h(0) = c_0\)).

Can we achieve this in a \(t\)-private manner? The answer is "Yes"! We are going to show that, there is a matrix \(T\), s.t., \[ \bar{h}_{1 : n} \doteq \begin{bmatrix} \bar{h}(1) \\ \bar{h}(2) \\ \vdots \\ \bar{h}(n) \end{bmatrix}, \quad h_{1 : n} = \begin{bmatrix} h(1) \\ h(2) \\ \vdots \\ h(n) \end{bmatrix}, \quad \bar{h}_{1 : n} = T \times h_{1 : n}. \]

Observe that, multiplying \(h_{1 : n}\) by \(T\) only involves addition and scalar multiplications of secret shares, which we have already shown can be performed in a \(t\)-private manner. It remain to explain what \(T\) is.

Define \[ V = \begin{bmatrix} 1, &1, &\cdots, &1 \\ 1, &2, &\cdots, &2^{n - 1} \\ &&\cdots\, \\ 1, &i, &\cdots, &i^{n - 1} \\ &&\cdots\, \\ 1, &n, &\cdots, &n^{n - 1} \end{bmatrix}, \quad \vec{c} = \begin{bmatrix} c_0 \\ c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_{2t} \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \bar{c} = \begin{bmatrix} c_0 \\ c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_{t} \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Then \(h_{1: n} = V \times \vec{c}\), and \(\bar{h}_{1: n} = V \times \bar{c}\). We also know that there exists a projection matrix \(P\), s.t., \(\bar{c} = P \vec{c}\). It follows that \[ \bar{h}_{1 : n} = V \times \bar{c} = V \times P \times \vec{c} = V \times P \times V^{-1} \times h_{1 : n}. \] Taking \(T = V \times P \times V^{-1}\) computes the proof.

Remark. If we directly compute \(V^{-1} \times h_{1 : n}\), we recover all coefficients of \(h(x)\). The key trick is to compute \((V \times P \times V^{-1}) \times h_{1 : n}\) instead, bypassing the explicit computation of \(V^{-1} \times h_{1 : n}\).
