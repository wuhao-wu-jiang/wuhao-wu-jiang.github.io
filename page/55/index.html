RSA

Posted on 2018-11-21 Edited on 2025-08-07

We discuss the problem of sending a message secretly over the internet. Suppose the original message is a sequence \(x\), the encrypted message is \(y\).

XOR Encryption

One solution is to via the XOR operation. Suppose there is a key \(k\) that is known both to the sender the receiver. Given an integer \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), denote \((n)_2\) its binary representation.

Sender Encryption. $ (y)_2 = (x)_2 (k)_2. $

Receiver Decryption. $ (x)_2 = (y)_2 (k)_2. $

Analysis

Given \(x\) with length \(n\), if \(k\) is sampled uniformly at random from \(\{0, 1\}^n\), then \((y)_2\) is a sequence uniformly at random from \(\{0, 1\}^n\). If the message is sent only one time, the eavesdropper can't get more information about \(x\) after seeing \(y\).

Two drawbacks exists.

  1. How can the sender exchange the key \(k\) with the receiver without meeting each other?

  2. If the many messages are sent, there are frequency pattern associated with \(y\), making it breakable.

RSA Encryption

RSA encryption algorithm overcomes the first issue by generating two keys for the receiver: the secrete key and the public key. The public key is distributed across the internet. The sender encrypts the message \(x\) with the public key. Only the receiver who has the secret key can decrypt the message. The mathematics behind is explained as follows.

Question to ponder: can we attack RSA by the frequency pattern?

Generation

First generate two prime number \(p, q\) and let \(N = pq\). Further, we find a integer \(e\), such that

  1. \(e\) is coprime to \((p - 1)(q - 1)\), i.e., \(\exists d\), such that \[ ed \equiv 1 \pmod{(p - 1)(q - 1)}. \]
  2. \(e \neq p\), \(e \neq q\).

Question to ponder: does such \(e\) exists?

Answer: YES. There are \(\Omega \left( \frac{(p - 1)(q - 1) }{ \log (p - 1)(q - 1) } - \frac{ \sqrt{ (p - 1)(q - 1) } }{ \log \sqrt { (p - 1)(q - 1) } } \right)\) prime numbers in \(\left( \sqrt{(p - 1)(q - 1)}, (p - 1)(q - 1) \right)\). Find three primes within this range, denoted as \(p_1, p_2, p_3\). Then \(p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot p_3 > (p - 1) (q - 1)\), so at least one of them is not a prime factors of \((q - 1)(p - 1)\).

Encryption and Decryption

Release the pair as public key: \[ (N, e). \]

And keep in secrete the triple as private key: \[ (p, q, d). \]

The algorithm for encryption and decryption is as follows:

Sender Encryption. $ y x^e N. $

Receiver Decryption. $ x y^d N. $

To prove the correctness of the algorithm, we have

Theorem $ x^{e d} x N. $

Proof One. Since \(ed \equiv 1 \pmod{(p - 1)(q - 1)}\), there exists some \(k \in \mathbb{N}\), s.t., \(e d = k (p - 1)(q - 1) + 1\). Hence, \[ m \stackrel{\cdot}{\equiv} x^{ed} \pmod{N} \equiv x^{k \cdot (p - 1) \cdot (q - 1) + 1} \pmod{N}. \] By Fermat's Little Theorem, \[ m \equiv x \pmod p,\, \text{ and }\, m \equiv x \pmod q. \]

If follows that \(N | (m - x)\), since \(p\) and \(q\) are primes and \(N\) is their least common multiple. Hence \(m \equiv x \pmod N\).

\(\square\)

Proof Two. \[ y^d \equiv x^{ed} \equiv x^{k(p - 1)(q - 1) + 1} \pmod N. \]

Case 1: this is the easy case in which \(x\) is coprime to \(N\). Then by Fermat-Euler theorem, \(x^{\varphi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N\). It follows immediately that \(x^{ed} \equiv x \pmod N\).

Case 2: otherwise, \(x \equiv \ell p\) for some \(1 \le \ell \le q\) or \(x \equiv \ell q\) for some \(1 \le \ell \le p\). WLOG, we just consider the former case. Now \(y^d \equiv (\ell p)^{k(p - 1)(q - 1) + 1} \pmod N\). On the other hand, as \((\ell p)^{k(p - 1)}\) is relative prime to \(q\), we conclude that \(((\ell p)^{k(p-1)})^{q- 1} \equiv mq + 1\) for some integer \(m\), by Fermat's little theorem. Therefore, \(y^d \equiv (mq + 1) x \equiv (mq + 1) (\ell p) \equiv m \ell p q + \ell p \equiv \ell p \equiv x \pmod N\) , which finishes our proof.

\(\square\)

Fermat's Little Theorem

Posted on 2018-11-20 Edited on 2023-07-14

Fermat's Little Theorem

It is a fundamental result in number theory.

Theorem. Given a prime \(p\), for every positive integer \(a \in \mathbb{N}^+\), if \(a \neq 0 \pmod p\), then \[ a^{p - 1} = 1 \quad \pmod p. \]

It suffices to prove the theorem for numbers in \(\mathbb{F}_p^+ \doteq \{ 1, 2, ..., p - 1\}\).

Proof One

Define the function \(f : \mathbb{F}_p^+ \rightarrow \mathbb{F}_p^+\) to be \[ f(x) = ax \pmod p. \]

For every \(x \neq x' \in \mathbb{F}_p^+\), \(ax \neq ax' \mod p\), otherwise \((x - x') \mid p\). Hence \(f\) is a bijection and \[ \{f(1), f(2), ..., f(p - 1)\} = \mathbb{F}_p^+. \]

Now we take product over all number in these two sets to obtain \[ (p - 1)! = \prod_{i = 1}^{p - 1} (ai) = a^{p - 1} (p - 1)! \pmod p \]

As \(p\) is a prime, every non-zero integer has a multiplicative inverse mod \(p\). Therefore we can cancel out \((p - 1)!\) from both side and get \[ 1 = a^{p - 1} \pmod p. \]

\(\square\)

Remark: Fermat-Euler theorem relaxes the condition of \(p\) being prime. It states that \(\forall p, a \in \mathbb{N}^+\) with \(\gcd(a, p) = 1\) (i.e., \(a\) and \(p\) are coprime), we have \[ a^{\varphi(p)} = 1 \pmod p, \]

where \(\varphi(p)\) is the number of elements that are coprime to \(p\) in \(\{1, 2, .., p - 1\}\). When \(p\) is prime, \(\varphi(p) = p - 1\).

The proof for the theorem is similar to the previous one. Let \[ S \subseteq \{1, 2, .., p - 1\} \]

be the set of numbers coprime to \(p\) and define \(f: S \rightarrow S\) to be \[ f(x) = ax \pmod p \]

which is a bijection. Therefore, \[ \prod_{x \in S} x = a^{\varphi(p)} \prod_{x \in S} x \pmod p \] Cancelling out the factor \(\prod_{x \in S} x\) from both side gives the desired result.

Proof Two

This proof uses the binomial expansion as follows: \[ \begin{aligned} (a + 1)^p &= a^p + \binom{p}{1} a^{p - 1} + ... + \binom{p}{p - 1} a + 1 &\pmod p \\ &= a^{p} + 1 &\pmod p \\ &= (a - 1)^{p} + 1 + 1 &\pmod p \\ &= ... \\ &= \begin{matrix} \underbrace{1 + 1 + ... + 1} \\ a + 1 \end{matrix} &\pmod p \\ &= a + 1 &\pmod p \end{aligned} \] The second equality holds since for \(1 \le i \le p - 1\), the coefficient \(\binom{p}{i} = \frac{p (p - 1) ... (p - i + 1)}{i !}\) divides \(p\), as \(p\) is relative prime to all integers \(1, 2, ..., i\). The third equality follows from respectively applying the previous expansion. Finally, we get Fermat's Little Theorem by multiplying both sides of \((a + 1)^p = (a + 1) \pmod p\) by the inverse of \(a + 1\) module \(p\).

Proof Three

Let \(a \in \mathbb{F}_p^+\). Consider the following numbers in \(\mathbb{F}_p^+\): \[ a, a^2, a^3, a^4, a^5, ... \]

As there are only \(p - 1\) distinct numbers in \(\mathbb{F}_p^+\), it holds that there exits a smallest \(h \in \mathbb{N}_+\), such that \[ a^h = 1 \pmod p. \]

Now consider the following procedure:

  1. \(\mathcal{U} \leftarrow \mathbb{F}_p^+;\)
  2. \(\mathcal{C} \leftarrow \{ \}, i \leftarrow 0\);
  3. while \(\mathcal{U} \neq \emptyset\) do:
  4. \(\qquad\) \(i \leftarrow i + 1\);
  5. \(\qquad\) let \(b\) be an element in \(\mathcal{U}\);
  6. \(\qquad\) let \(S_i \leftarrow \{ b, ab, a^2b, ..., a^{h - 1} b\}\);
  7. \(\qquad\) \(\mathcal{U} \leftarrow \mathcal{U} \setminus S_i\);
  8. \(\qquad\) \(\mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{C} \cup \{ S_i \}\);

Suppose that \(\mathcal{C} = \{ S_1, ..., S_m \}\). We claim that

  • The elements in \(S_i\) are distinct. Otherwise, \[ a^k b = a^\ell b \pmod p \Longrightarrow a^{k - \ell} b= 0 \pmod p, \] a contradiction. If follows that \(|S_i| = h\).

  • For \(i \neq j\), \(S_i \cap S_j = \emptyset\). Otherwise, let \(b \in S_i \cap S_j\). Then every element in \(S_i \cup S_j\) can be expressed as \(a^k b\) for some integer \(k\).

It follows that \(\mathcal{C}\) is a partition of \(\mathbb{F}_p^+\). As each set in \(\mathcal{C}\) has the same size, we have \(h \mid (p - 1)\) and \[ a^{p - 1} = 1 \pmod p. \]

\(\square\)

Euclidean Algorithm

Posted on 2018-11-08 Edited on 2021-03-18

We discuss the Euclidean algorithm and use it to compute the multiplicative inverse.

Multiplicative Inverse. Given \(x, y \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(z\) is called the multiplicative inverse of \(x\) (module \(y\)) if \[ xz \equiv 1 \pmod y. \]

Theorem 1

\(x\) has a multiplicative inverse module \(y \iff \gcd(x, y) = 1\).

Proof.
Define the set \(S = \{ ax + by \mid a, b \in Z\}\). Let \(d = a^* x + b^* y\) be the smallest positive integer in \(S\). Our proof relies on the following lemma.

Lemma: \(\gcd(x,y) = d\).

First, as \(d\) is a linear combination of \(x\) and \(y\), \(\gcd(x, y) | d\) and \(\gcd(x, y) \le d\).

Second, we claim that \(d | x\) and \(d | y\). By symmetry, we prove only the former. If not, suppose that \(d \nmid x\). Then \(\exists k \in \mathbb{Z}, \ell \in [1, d - 1]\), s.t.,
\[ x = k d + \ell. \]

Since \(\ell = x - k d\) is a linear combination of \(x\) and \(y\), it holds that \(\ell \in S\), contradicting \(d\) being the smallest positive element in \(S\).

It concludes that \(d\) equals to \(\gcd(x, y)\).

\(\square\)

The proof of the above theorem immediately follows from the lemma.

  1. \(\gcd(x, y) = 1 \rightarrow x\) has a multiplicative inverse.
    In this case, \(d = a^* x + b^* y = 1\). \(a^*\) is th multiplicative inverse of \(x\) module \(y\).

  2. \(x\) has a multiplicative inverse \(\rightarrow \gcd(x, y) = 1\).
    If \(x\) has a multiplicative inverse, there exists some \(z\) and \(l\), such that \(zx = ly + 1\).

\(\blacksquare\)

Theorem 2

If \(x\) has a multiplicative inverse module \(y\), it is unique.

Proof.
Let \(a\) and \(b\) both be the multiplicative inverse of \(x\) module \(y\), then \[ a \equiv a (xb) \equiv (ax) b \equiv b \pmod y \]

\(\blacksquare\)

Basic Euclidean Algorithm

Euclidean algorithm computes \(\gcd(x, y)\) as follows:

Correctness

If \(y =0\), whether \(x = 0\) or not, we have \(\gcd(x, y) = x\).

If \(x < y\), \(x \mod y = x\). The algorithm exchanges \(x\) and \(y\) at the first recursion. It suffices to consider only \(x \ge y > 0\).

If \(x \ge y > 0\), then \(\gcd(x, y) = \gcd(y, x \mod y)\). Let \(d = \gcd(x, y)\). Clearly \(d | x\) and \(d | y\). We claim that \[ d | (x \mod y) \]
Suppose \(x = k d\) and \(y = \ell d\), then \(x \mod y = d \cdot (k \mod \ell)\), which finishes proof.

Time

Let \(n\) be the the total number of bits to represent \(x\) and \(y\). Then the algorithm stops after \(O(n)\) recursions.

Proof.

We can assume \(x \ge y\). Otherwise, they are swapped by a recursive call.

The key observation is that, after every two recursive calls, the first argument \(x\) shrinks at least by a factor of two.

  1. If \(y \le x / 2\), the observation holds after the first call.
  2. If \(y > x / 2\), then after two recursive call, the first argument becomes \(x \mod y\), which is smaller than \(x / 2\).

\(\square\)

Extended Euclidean Algorithm

We extend the basic version to return a pair \(a^*, b^* \in \mathbb{N}\) s.t. \(\gcd(x, y) = d = a^* x + b^* y\).

Correctness

Suppose \(s = a' y + b' \cdot (x \mod y)\). Then \[ s = a' y + b'(x - \lfloor x / y \rfloor \cdot y) =b' x + (a' - b' \cdot \lfloor x / y \rfloor) \cdot y \] This is why the algorithm work.

\(\square\)

Example

\[ \begin{aligned} & \gcd(9, 6) = (3, 1, -1) \quad [-1 = 0 - 1 * 9 / 6] \\ & \rightarrow \gcd(6, 3) = (3, 0, 1) \quad [1 = 1 - 0 * 6 / 3]\\ & \quad \rightarrow \gcd(3, 0) = (3, 1, 0) \\ \end{aligned} \]
