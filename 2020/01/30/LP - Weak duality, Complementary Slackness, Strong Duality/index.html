Dual Program and Weak Duality

Linear programming studies the optimization problems in which both its objective function and feasible region are represented by linear relationship.

Every linear program can be converted into Canonical form

max⁡cTxs.t.,Ax≤bx≥0\begin{aligned} & \max & c^T x \\ & s.t., & Ax \le b \\ && x \ge 0 \end{aligned} ​maxs.t.,​cTxAx≤bx≥0​

where c,x∈Rnc, x \in \mathcal{R}^nc,x∈Rn, A∈Rm×nA \in \mathcal{R}^{m \times n}A∈Rm×n and b∈Rmb \in \mathcal{R}^mb∈Rm.

Suppose we have a conic combination (y∈Rmy \in \mathcal{R}^my∈Rm and y≥0y \ge 0y≥0) of the linear constraints:

yT(Ax)≤yTby^T (Ax) \le y^T b yT(Ax)≤yTb

Such that yTA≥cTy^T A \ge c^TyTA≥cT, then yTby^T byTb gives an upper bound of the original program. We want to find a yyy, such that the bound is as tight as possible. This gives rise to another linear program:

min⁡yTbs.t.,yTA≥cTy≥0\begin{aligned} & \min & y^T b \\ & s.t., & y^T A \ge c^T \\ && y \ge 0 \end{aligned} ​mins.t.,​yTbyTA≥cTy≥0​

We call the original program the primal and the derived program its dual.

The philosophy of dual program is to use the combination of primal constraints to construct a bound of its objective function. More formally, the goal is that the weak duality holds:

cTx≤(yTA)x=yTAx=yT(Ax)≤yTbc^T x \le (y^T A) x = y^T A x = y^T (Ax) \le y^T b cTx≤(yTA)x=yTAx=yT(Ax)≤yTb

Note that cTx≤(ATy)Txc^T x \le (A^T y)^T xcTx≤(ATy)Tx is true because yTA≥cy^T A \ge cyTA≥c and x≥0x \ge 0x≥0. And yT(Ax)≤yTby^T (Ax) \le y^T byT(Ax)≤yTb follows from Ax≤bAx \le bAx≤b and yT≥0y^T \ge 0yT≥0.

Corollary 1: If the primal is unbounded, then the dual is infeasible. If the dual is unbounded, then the primal is infeasible.

Complementary Slackness

The strong duality states that, if both primal and dual are feasible, then the objective function values of their optimal solutions equal.

If we expand the weak duality expression for the standard form, we get

Strong Duality

Question to ponder: what is the geometric interpretation of complementary slackness?

Special Case

To illustrate its underlying meaning, first consider the special case

min⁡yTbs.t.,yTA=cT\begin{aligned} & \min & y^T b \\ & s.t., & y^T A = c^T \end{aligned} ​mins.t.,​yTbyTA=cT​

whose dual form is given by

max⁡cTxs.t.,Ax=b\begin{aligned} & \max & c^T x \\ & s.t., & Ax = b \end{aligned} ​maxs.t.,​cTxAx=b​

First note that yTA=cTy^T A = c^TyTA=cT means that cTc^TcT is a linear combination of rows of AAA. The primal is feasible if ccc is in the row space of AAA.

In this special case, if both primal and dual are feasible, then for any feasible solutions xxx and yyy, strong duality holds, since cTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTbc^T x = (y^T A) x = y^T (Ax) = y^T bcTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTb.

The dual search for some xxx such that

Ax=bAx = b Ax=b

If we can find such xxx, it implies that bbb lies in the column space of AAA, denoted as C(A)C(A)C(A) (the subspace in Rn\mathcal{R}^nRn spanned by the row vectors of AAA). What if not? We can decompose bbb into two part: b=b1+b2b = b_1 + b_2b=b1​+b2​, such that b1∈C(A)b_1 \in C(A)b1​∈C(A) and 0≠b2∈N(AT)0 \neq b_2 \in N(A^T)0​=b2​∈N(AT) (the subspace orthogonal to C(A)C(A)C(A)). Here is the problem: if yyy is a feasible solution for the primal, then we can decrease yyy by arbitrary amount along the direction orthogonal to C(A)C(A)C(A), while maintaining its feasibility. In particular, if we add k⋅b2k \cdot b_2k⋅b2​ to yyy (k>0k > 0k>0), then (y−kb2)(y - k b_2)(y−kb2​) is still feasible as (y−kb2)TA=yTA−kb2TA=cT(y - k b_2)^T A = y^T A - k b_2^T A = c^T(y−kb2​)TA=yTA−kb2T​A=cT. But now (y−k⋅b2)Tb=yTb−kb2Tb2<yTb(y - k \cdot b_2)^T b = y^T b - k b_2^T b_2 < y^T b(y−k⋅b2​)Tb=yTb−kb2T​b2​<yTb, which implies that the primal is unbounded.

General Case

Now we return our discussion to the more general form:

min⁡yTbs.t.,yTA≥cT\begin{aligned} & \min & y^T b \\ & s.t., & y^T A \ge c^T \end{aligned} ​mins.t.,​yTbyTA≥cT​

Its dual is given by

max⁡cTxs.t.,Ax=bx≥0\begin{aligned} & \max & c^T x \\ & s.t., & Ax = b \\ && x \ge 0 \end{aligned} ​maxs.t.,​cTxAx=bx≥0​

Every LP can be converted into standard form.

The weak duality states that

cTx≤yTAx=yTbc^T x \le y^T A x = y^T b cTx≤yTAx=yTb

If both primal and dual are feasible, then strong duality reduce to show that the first inequality holds with equality, that is, ∃\exists∃ feasible x,yx, yx,y, s.t., cTx=yTAxc^T x = y^T A xcTx=yTAx. In particular, complementary slackness, this is equivalent to proving that

(yTA−cT)i=0∨xi=0∀ i\begin{aligned} &(y^T A - c^T)_i = 0 &&\vee &&x_i = 0 &&\forall \ i \\ \end{aligned} ​(yTA−cT)i​=0​​∨​​xi​=0​​∀ i​

We begin by assuming that yyy is the optimal solution for the dual. We claim that yyy can not be an interior point of the feasible region. Otherwise, yyy can move along arbitrary direction by a little while maintaining feasibility. If it moves along the direction pointed by −b-b−b, then the objective function value decreases, contradicting yyy being optimal.

Therefore, yyy must be on the boundary of the feasible region.

Question to ponder: prove the existence of optimal solution of the primal, assuming feasibility of the dual.

The boundary yyy touches are specified by tight constraints. The intuition behind is that, bbb must be the positive combination of the normal vector of the tight constraints. Otherwise, we can find some direction that decreases objective function value while maintaining feasibility.

Denote S⊂[m]S \subset [m]S⊂[m] the set of indexes, such that ∀i∈S\forall i \in S∀i∈S, yTAi=ciy^T A_i = c_iyTAi​=ci​, where AiA_iAi​ is the iii-th column of matrix AAA and cic_ici​ is the iii-th coordinator of vector ccc. In other words, SSS is the set of indexes for which the inequality is tight.
