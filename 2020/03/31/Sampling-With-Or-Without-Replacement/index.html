Sampling With Or Without Replacement

Posted on 2020-03-31

Suppose that there are aaa blue balls and bbb red balls in a box and we would like to sample kkk balls from them. We can sample the balls with replacement such that after picking each ball we put it back into the box, or we can do it without replacement.

For convenience of discussion, denote n=a+bn = a + bn=a+b, p=anp = \frac{a}{n}p=na​ and q=bnq = \frac{b}{n}q=nb​. Define XiX_iXi​ (i∈[k]i \in [k]i∈[k]) the indicator random variable of whether the iii-th selected ball is blue. Let

X=∑XiX = \sum X_i X=∑Xi​

Then we claim that

E[X]=∑i∈[k]E[Xi]=kpE[X] = \sum_{i \in [k]} E[X_i] = kp E[X]=i∈[k]∑​E[Xi​]=kp

The first equality holds by linearity of expectation. The second equality states that in expectation, ppp fraction of the selected balls are blue.

The claim is trivial when we sample with replacement, as Pr⁡[Xi=1]=p\Pr[X_i = 1] = pPr[Xi​=1]=p. To prove that the probability applies for the case of sampling without replacement requires a little more effort, as shown by the following

  1. There are in total (nk)\begin{pmatrix} n \\ k\end{pmatrix}(nk​) possible combinations of choosing kkk balls out of nnn.
  2. Among them there are (a1)⋅(n−1k−1)\begin{pmatrix} a \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} n - 1 \\ k - 1 \end{pmatrix}(a1​)⋅(n−1k−1​) combinations such that the iii-th ball is blue.
  3. Therefore, Pr⁡[Xi=1]=(a1)⋅(n−1k−1)(nk)=an=p\Pr[X_i = 1] = \frac{ \begin{pmatrix} a \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} n - 1 \\ k - 1 \end{pmatrix} }{ \begin{pmatrix} n \\ k\end{pmatrix} } = \frac{a}{n} = pPr[Xi​=1]=(nk​)(a1​)⋅(n−1k−1​)​=na​=p.
