    Integrating sin(x)/x from zero to infinity

    Posted on 2022-11-19 Edited on 2023-03-08

    We want to prove the following improper integral

    Theorem. \[ \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

    Existence of Limit

    Since \(\int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \sin x \, dx = (-1)^{n} \cdot 2\), \[ \int_{n \pi}^{(n + 1) \pi} \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx \in \Theta \left( \frac{ (-1)^{n} }{n} \right). \]

    Hence \(\lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} \, dx\) exists. On the other hand, \(\frac{\sin x}{x}\) is not Lebesgue integrable over \([0, \infty)\).

    Proving the Limit

    Observation. \[ \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, dy = \frac{\sin x}{x}. \]

    We need two technical lemmas to finish the proof.

    Lemma 1. \(e^{-xy} \sin x\) is integrable on \([0, a] \times [0, \infty)\).

    Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= \frac{ - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a }{1 + y^2} \\ &= \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2}. \end{aligned} \]

    We defer the proof of the lemmas to the end of the article. Denote \(\mathcal{F}\) the collection of Lebesgue measurable sets on \(\mathbb{R}\), and \(\lambda\) the Lebesgue measure defined on \(\mathcal{F}\). Let \(\mu \doteq \lambda \times \lambda\) be the product measure on \((\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{F})\). Now, via Fubini's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a \frac{\sin x}{x} dx &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \sin x \, d y dx \\ &=\int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} e^{-xy} \sin x \, d \mu \\ &= \int_0^\infty \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx d y \\ &=\int_0^\infty \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \arctan y \mid_0^\infty - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2} - \int_0^\infty \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \, d y. \end{aligned} \]

    The right hand side converges to \(0\) as \(a\) approaches infinity: \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty \left| \frac{ e^{- ay} \cos a + y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2} \right| \, d y &\le \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} \int_0^\infty (1 + y) e^{-ay} \, d y \\ &= \lim_{a \rightarrow \infty} -\frac{1}{a} \left( (1 + y) e^{-ay} + \frac{1}{a} e^{-ay} \right) \Bigg|_0^\infty = 0. \end{aligned} \]

    Proof of Lemma 1. Via Tonelli's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{[0, a] \times [0, \infty)} | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d \mu &= \int_0^a \int_0^\infty | e^{-xy} \sin x | \, d y dx \\ &\le \int_0^a \int_0^\infty e^{-xy} \, d y dx \\ &= \int_0^a \frac{1}{x} \, d y dx \\ &= \ln a \\ &\le \infty. \end{aligned} \]

    \(\square\)

    Proof of Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \cos x \, dx \\ &= - e^{-xy} \cos x \mid_0^a - y \left( e^{-xy} \sin x \mid_0^a + y \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx \right). \end{aligned} \]

    Another proof of Lemma 2. \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^a e^{-xy} e^{ix} \, dx &= \int_0^a e^{-x (y - i)} \, dx \\ &= \frac{-1}{y - i} \cdot e^{-x (y - i)} \mid_0^a \\ &= \frac{1}{y - i} \cdot \left( 1 - e^{- a(y - i)} \right) \\ &= \frac{y + i}{1 + y^2} \cdot \left( 1 - e^{-ay} (\cos a + i \sin a ) \right). \end{aligned} \]

    Hence, \[ \int_0^a e^{-xy} \sin x \, dx = \Im \left( \int_0^a e^{-xy} e^{ix} \, dx \right) = \frac{ 1 - e^{- ay} \cos a - y e^{-ay} \sin a }{1 + y^2}. \]

    \(\square\)

    Reference
