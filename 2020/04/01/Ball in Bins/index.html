Ball in Bins

Posted on 2020-04-01 Edited on 2021-10-27

Suppose we would like to throw \(n\) balls into \(n\) bins uniformly at random. We claim that the bin with maximum load has \(\Theta( \frac{\ln n}{\ln \ln n})\) ball with high probability.

Upper Bound

The probability that a particular bin receives at least \(k\) balls is given by \[ \Pr[ \exists k \text{ balls in the bin} ] \le \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \le \frac{1}{k!} \le \frac{ e^k }{ k^k}, \]

where the first inequality follows from union bound, and the last inequality comes from \(e^{ k } \ge \frac{k^k}{k!}\), as for \(\forall x \ge 0\), \[ e^x = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + ...\ge \frac{x^k}{ k! }. \]

We want to find a \(k\), such that \[ \left( \frac{e}{ k} \right)^k = \frac{1}{n^2}. \]

Taking \(\ln\) of both sides, \[ \begin{aligned} &k \ln k - k = 2 \ln n \\ \implies &k \ln k = \Theta( \ln n) \\ \implies &\ln k = \Theta( \ln \ln n). \end{aligned} \]

(By using asymptotically notations, the relationship only holds for reasonably large enough \(k\) and \(n\).)

Substituting \(\ln k = \Theta( \ln \ln n)\) into \(k \ln k = \Theta( \ln n)\), we get \[ k = \Theta\left( \frac{\ln n}{\ln \ln n} \right). \]

Finally, by union bound over the number of possible bins, the probability that there is a bin which receives \(\ge k\) ball is bounded by \(1 / n\).

Lower Bound

The probability that a bin gets exactly \(k\) balls is given by (for \(k \ge 1\))

\[ \begin{aligned} \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} &\ge \frac{n^k}{k^k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} \cdot \left( \frac{ n - 1}{n} \right)^{n - 1} \\ &\ge \frac{n^k}{k^k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} \cdot \frac{1}{ \left( 1 + \frac{1}{n - 1} \right)^{n - 1} } \\ &\ge \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e}. \end{aligned} \]

The first inequality follows from that \[ \frac{n (n - 1) \ldots (n - k + 1) }{k (k - 1) \ldots 1} \ge \frac{n^k}{k^k}, \]

and the last inequality from that \(1 + x \le e^x\) for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\).

If we set \(k = \frac{1}{3} \frac{ \ln n }{ \ln \ln n }\), then \[ \begin{aligned} & k^k \le ( \ln n)^k = n^{1/3} \\ \implies & \frac{1}{k^k} \frac{1}{e} \ge e^{ - 1} n^{-1 / 3}. \end{aligned} \]

This probability is rather smaller. At this point it seems that a single bin is unlikely to success. But by linearity of expectation, the expected number of bins with \(k\) balls is given by \(e^{ - 1} n^{ 2/ 3}\). Define the indicator variables \(X_i\) for the event that the \(i\)-th bin receives exactly \(k\) balls, and let \(X = \sum X_i\). Then

  1. \(\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \Pr[X_i = 1] \ge e^{-1} n^{ - 1 / 3}\).
  2. \(\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum \mathbb{E}[X_i] \ge e^{ - 1} n^{ 2/ 3}\).

As in expectation there are \(e^{-1} n^{2/ 3}\) such bins, it seems that we are likely to find one. To formalize the intuition, we need to make sure that \(X\) does not deviate from its expectation much. To see why this might cause a problem, consider the random variable \(Y\) such that \[ \begin{aligned} &\Pr[Y = e^{-1} n^{ 5 / 3} ] = \frac{1}{n}, \\ &\Pr[Y = 0 ] = 1 - \frac{1}{n}. \\ \end{aligned} \]

\(Y\) has expectation \(\mathbb{E}[Y] = e^{ - 1} n^{ 2 / 3}\), but is very likely to be \(0\). The problem with \(Y\) is that it has large variance.

We investigate the variance of \(X\): \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{Var}[X] &= \sum_i \mathbb{Var}[X_i] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) \\ &\le \sum_i \mathbb{Var}[X_i] \le \sum_i \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] = \sum_i \mathbb{E}[X_i] \\ &\le n. \end{aligned} \]

The first inequality follows from that \[ \mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) < 0,\, \forall i \neq j. \]

Intuitively, that bin \(i\) obtains ball makes it more difficult for bin \(j\) to obtain ball.

Finally, by Chebyshev Inequality, we have \[ \Pr[ |X - \mathbb{E}[X]| \ge \mathbb{E}[X] ] \le \frac{ \mathbb{Var}[X] }{ \mathbb{E}[X]^2} \le \frac{n}{ e^{-2} n^{4 / 3}} = \frac{e^2}{n^ {1 / 3} }. \]

Fact. For \(i \neq j\), \(\mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) < 0\)

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{Cor}(X_i, X_j) &= \mathbb{E} [ X_i X_j ] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \Pr[X_i = 1, X_j = 1] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \binom{n}{k} \binom{n - k}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} \frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{n - 2k} - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &< \binom{n}{k} \binom{n}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} \frac{1}{n^k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n - k} - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] - \mathbb{E} [X_i] \cdot \mathbb{E} [X_j] \\ &= 0. \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)
