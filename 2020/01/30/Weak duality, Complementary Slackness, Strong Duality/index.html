Every linear program can be converted into Canonical form

max⁡cTxs.t. Ax≤bx≥0 \begin{array}{lr} \max & c^T x \\ \text{s.t. } & Ax \le b \\ &x \ge 0 \end{array} maxs.t. ​cTxAx≤bx≥0​

for some c,x∈Rnc, x \in \mathbb{R}^nc,x∈Rn, A∈Rm×nA \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}A∈Rm×n and b∈Rmb \in \mathbb{R}^mb∈Rm.

Dual Program and Weak Duality

Next, we try to construct an upper bound for the optimal solution of this optimization problem, based on its linear constraints.

If y∈Rmy \in \mathbb{R}^my∈Rm and y≥0y \ge 0y≥0, then yT(Ax)y^T (Ax)yT(Ax) is called a conic combination of the constraints.
It holds that

yT(Ax)≤yTb. y^T (Ax) \le y^T b. yT(Ax)≤yTb.

If yyy further satisfies the constraint

cT≤yTA, c^T \le y^T A, cT≤yTA,

combining with the fact that x≥0x \ge 0x≥0, we get

cTx≤(yTA)x=yT(Ax)≤yTb. c^T x \le (y^T A) x = y^T (Ax) \le y^T b. cTx≤(yTA)x=yT(Ax)≤yTb.

Hence yTby^T byTb is an upper bound of the optimal value of the original program.
We want the bound to be as tight as possible.
This motivates another linear program:

min⁡yTbs.t. yTA≥cTy≥0 \begin{array}{lr} \min & y^T b \\ \text{s.t. } & y^T A \ge c^T \\ & y \ge 0 \end{array} mins.t. ​yTbyTA≥cTy≥0​

We call the original program the primal and the derived program its dual.
The philosophy behind the dual construction is to combine the primal constraints to obtain a bound of its optimal value.

Corollary. If the primal is unbounded, then the dual is infeasible. If the dual is unbounded, then the primal is infeasible.

In general, the primal may not be given in Canonical form and can be formulated as

Complementary Slackness

The strong duality states that, if both primal and dual are feasible, then the objective function values of their optimal solutions are equal.
It follows that the inequalities in weak duality are saturated

cTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTb. c^T x = (y^T A) x = y^T (Ax) = y^T b. cTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTb.

Therefore,

(yTA−cT)x=0, yT(b−Ax)=0. (y^T A - c^T) x = 0,\, \\ y^T (b - Ax) = 0. (yTA−cT)x=0,yT(b−Ax)=0.

If the primal is in canonical form, then the fact that yTA≥cy^T A \ge cyTA≥c, x≥0x \ge 0x≥0, y≥0y \ge 0y≥0, and b−Ax≥0b - Ax \ge 0b−Ax≥0 implies that

where (yTA−cT)i(y^T A - c^T)_i(yTA−cT)i​ is its iii-th coordinate of the vector yTA−cT∈R1×ny^T A - c^T \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times n}yTA−cT∈R1×n, and

Strong Duality

Theorem. Exactly one of the following four cases holds:

  1. Neither the primal nor the dual is feasible.
  2. If both the primal and dual are feasible, then their optimal values are equal.
  3. If the primal is feasible and unbounded, and dual is infeasible.
  4. If the dual is feasible and unbounded, and primal is infeasible.

Special Case

We first consider the special case such that not every linear program can be converted to this case.
We consider the general form in the next section.

The special case is given by

min⁡yTb,s.t. yTA=cT. \begin{array}{lr} \min & y^T b, \\ \text{s.t. } & y^T A = c^T. \end{array} mins.t. ​yTb,yTA=cT.​

The dual form is given by

max⁡cTx,s.t. Ax=b. \begin{array}{lr} \max & c^T x, \\ \text{s.t. } & Ax = b. \end{array} maxs.t. ​cTx,Ax=b.​

The primal is feasible if ccc is a linear combination of rows of AAA, i.e., in the row space of AAA.
The dual is feasible if bbb is a linear combination of columns of AAA, i.e., in column space of AAA, denoted as C(A)C(A)C(A).

It is possible that neither the primal nor the dual is feasible.

Example.

A=[1, 00, 0], b=[11], c=[11]. A = \begin{bmatrix} 1,\, &0\\ 0,\, &0\\ \end{bmatrix},\, b= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{bmatrix},\, c= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}. A=[1,0,​00​],b=[11​],c=[11​].

Then neither Ax=bA x = bAx=b nor yTA=cTy^T A = c^TyTA=cT is feasible.

If both primal and dual are feasible, then for all feasible solutions xxx and yyy, it holds that

cTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTb. c^T x = (y^T A) x = y^T (Ax) = y^T b. cTx=(yTA)x=yT(Ax)=yTb.

Next, we show that

Theorem.

  1. If the primal is feasible and dual is infeasible, then the primal is unbounded.
  2. If the dual is feasible and primal is infeasible, then the dual is unbounded.

Proof. By symmetry, we prove just the former.

If Ax=bAx = bAx=b is not feasible, then bbb lies outside the column space of AAA, denoted as C(A)C(A)C(A) (the subspace in Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm spanned by the column vectors of AAA).
Let N(AT)N(A^T)N(AT) be the subspace orthogonal to C(A)C(A)C(A).
We can decompose bbb into two part:

Then for all k∈Rk \in \mathbb{R}k∈R, y+k⋅b2y + k \cdot b_2y+k⋅b2​ is still a feasible solution, since

General Case

We discuss the more general form:

min⁡yTb,s.t. yTA≥cT. \begin{array}{lr} \min & y^T b, \\ \text{s.t. } & y^T A \ge c^T. \end{array} mins.t. ​yTb,yTA≥cT.​

Every linear program can be converted into this general form.

Its dual is given by

max⁡cTx,s.t. Ax=b,x≥0. \begin{array}{lr} \max & c^T x, \\ \text{s.t. } & Ax = b, \\ & x \ge 0. \end{array} maxs.t. ​cTx,Ax=b,x≥0.​
