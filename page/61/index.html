Karger Algorithm I

Posted on 2018-07-21 Edited on 2022-02-17

Algorithm

Let \(G = (V, E)\) be an undirected connected graph with \(n\) vertices, \(m\) edges, and min-cut size \(c^*\). David R. Karger [1] proposed a simple randomized algorithm for estimating the value of \(c^*\), as described thus.

Karger Algorithm. 1. repeat until two nodes are left in the graph:
2. \(\qquad\) Select an edge \(e = (u,v)\) from the graph uniformly at random.
3. \(\qquad\) Merge its endpoints \(u\) and \(v\) into new vertex \(w\).
4. \(\qquad\) Remove self loops created by the merge.
4. \(\qquad\) Connect the edges incident to \(u\) or \(v\) to new vertex \(w\).
5. Return the number of edges, denoted as \(c\), between the remaining two vertices.

Each iteration in the algorithm is called a contraction.

Analysis

Theorem. It holds that \[ \Pr[ c = c^* ] = \frac{2}{n(n - 1)}. \]

We can boost the successful probability via repetitions.

Corollary. If we repeat the algorithm \(0.5 \cdot n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot \ln n\) times, the probability we don't find \(c^*\) is at most \(1 / n\).

Proof of the Corollary. \[ \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n (n - 1)} \right)^{0.5 \cdot n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot \ln n} \le e^{\ln n} = \frac{1}{n} \] \(\square\)

Proof of the Theorem.

Consider the first iteration of the algorithm. The key observation is that the degree of each vertex is at least \(c^*\). Therefore, \[ m \ge c^* \cdot n / 2. \]

Fix a min-cut in \(G\), the probability that any of its edges is selected at most \[ \frac{c^*}{m} \le \frac{2} { n }. \]

Therefore, with probability at least \(1 - 2 / n\), none of the edges in the chosen min-cut is contracted in the first iteration. Moreover, we have the following observation.

After contracting an edge, each cut in the new graph corresponds to a cut in the original graph.

Therefore, if none of the edges of the chosen min-cut is contracted, it remains to be the min-cut in the new graph. Conditioned on this, via similar argument, we can prove that in the second iteration, none of the edges of the chosen min-cut is contracted with probability at least \(1 - 2 / (n - 1)\).

By induction, it holds that, none of the edges of the chosen min-cut is contracted over all iterations is given by \[ \left(1 - \frac{2}{n} \right) \left(1 - \frac{2}{n - 1} \right) \ldots \left(1 - \frac{2}{3} \right) = \binom{n}{2}^{-1}. \]

\(\square\)

Number of Min Cuts

Corollary. There are \(n (n - 1) / 2\) min-cuts in the graph.

Proof.
The events of outputting different min-cuts are disjoint.
\(\square\)

By slightly modifying the algorithm, we can obtain another useful fact.

Theorem. (Approximate Min-Cut). Given \(t \in [1, n / 2]\), an \(t\)-minimal cut is a cut with value \(\le t c^*\). It follows that the number of \(t\)-minimal cut is bounded by \[ O \left( 2^{2t} \cdot \sqrt{ \frac{n}{(n - 2t) \cdot 2t} } \left( \frac{n}{n - 2t} \right)^{n - 2t} \left( \frac{n}{2t} \right)^{2t}\right) \]

Remark. The bound can be further upper bounded by \[ \begin{aligned} &O \left( \sqrt{ \frac{n}{(n - 2t) \cdot 2t} } \cdot \exp \left( \frac{2t}{n - 2t} \cdot (n - 2t) \right) \cdot \left( \frac{n}{t} \right)^{2t} \right) \subseteq O \left( \left( \frac{e \cdot n}{t} \right)^{2t} \right). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Suppose we run the contraction algorithm, until \(k = \lceil 2t \rceil\) are left. Then we output one of the \(2^k\) cuts uniformly at random. The probability \(p\) that a particular \(t\)-minimal cut is outputted is given by
\[ \begin{aligned} p &\doteq \frac{1}{2^k} \cdot \left( 1 - \frac{2t}{n} \right) \cdot \left( 1 - \frac{2t}{n - 1} \right) \cdot \left( 1 - \frac{2t}{k + 1} \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{2^k} \cdot\frac{ (n - 2t) \cdot (n - 1 - 2t) \cdot \ldots (k + 1 - 2t)}{ n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot \ldots \cdot (k + 1)}. \end{aligned} \]

Denote \(a = k - 2t \in [0, 1)\), \(b = n - 2t\), and \[ f = \prod_{ i \in [n - 2k] } (a + i). \]

We rely on the following lemmas and facts.

Lemma. \[ f \in \Theta \left( \sqrt{n - 2t} \left( \frac{n - 2t}{e} \right)^{n - 2t} \right). \]

Fact (Stirling's Approximation). \[ n! \in \Theta \left( \sqrt{n} \left( \frac{n}{e} \right)^{n}\right), \quad k! \in \Theta \left( \sqrt{k} \left( \frac{k}{e} \right)^{k}\right). \]

It follows that \[ p = \frac{1}{2^k} \cdot \frac{f \cdot k!}{n!} \in \Theta \left( \frac{1}{2^k} \cdot \frac{ \sqrt{(n - 2t) \cdot k}}{n} \cdot \frac{\left( n - 2t \right)^{n - 2t} k^k }{ n^n } \right). \]

\(\square\)

It remains to prove the lemma.

Proof of the Lemma. Note \[ \begin{aligned} \ln f &= \sum_{ i \in [n - 2k] } \ln (a + i). \end{aligned} \]

For each \(r \ge 1\), define \[ U_r \doteq \int_{r}^{r + 1} \ln x \, dx = \left( x \ln x - x \right) \mid_r^{r + 1} = \ln \frac{(r + 1)^{r + 1}}{r^r} - 1, \] and \[ L_r \doteq \frac{1}{2} \big( \ln r + \ln (r + 1) \big) = \ln \sqrt{ r (r + 1) }. \]

Denote the gap between them as \(\epsilon_r \doteq U_r - L_r\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \ln f = \sum_{ i \in [n - 2k] } \ln (a + i) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \ln (a + 1) + \ln b \right) + \sum_{i \in [n - 2k - 1] } L_{a + i}. \end{aligned} \]

Moreover, via that \(a + 1 \in [1, 2)\), \[ \begin{aligned} U &\doteq \sum_{i \in [n - 2k - 1] } U_{a + i} = \int_{a + 1}^{b} \ln x \, dx \\ &= \big( b \ln b - (a + 1) \ln (a + 1) - (b - a - 1) \big) \in \Theta (b \ln b - b). \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \[ \big( \ln f \big) - U \in \Theta \left( \frac{1}{2} \ln b + b \ln b - b - \sum_{i \in [n - 2k - 1] } \epsilon_{a + i} \right). \]

Finally,
\[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon_r &\doteq U_r - L_r = \ln \frac{(r + 1)^{r + 0.5}}{r^{r + 0.5}} - 1 = (r + 0.5) \ln \frac{r + 1}{r} - 1. \end{aligned} \]

We will prove that \(\epsilon_r \in \Theta(1 / r^2)\). Let \(1 / z \doteq 2 \cdot (r + 0.5) = 2r + 1\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon_r &= \frac{1}{2 z} \cdot \ln \frac{1 / z + 1}{1 / z - 1} - 1 = \frac{1}{2 z} \cdot \ln \frac{1 + z}{1 - z} - 1\\ &= \frac{1}{2 z} \cdot \left( -\sum_{ i \in \mathbb{N}^+ } \frac{(-z)^i}{i} + \sum_{ i \in \mathbb{N}^+ } \frac{z^i}{i} \right) - 1 \\ &= \frac{1}{z} \cdot \left( z + \frac{z^3}{3} + \frac{z^5}{5} + \ldots \right) - 1 \\ &= \frac{z^2}{3} + \frac{z^4}{5} + \frac{z^6}{7} + \ldots. \end{aligned} \]

It follows that \[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon_r &\in \Theta \left( \frac{z^2}{3} \left( 1 + z^2 + z^4 + \ldots \right) \right) \\ &\subset \Theta \left( \frac{z^2}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - z^2} \right) \\ &\subset \Theta \left( \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{ (2 r + 1)^2 - 1} \right)\\ &\subset \Theta \left( \frac{1}{3} \frac{1}{(2r + 2) \cdot 2r} \right) \\ &\subset \Theta \left( \frac{1}{12} \cdot \left( \frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r + 1}\right) \right), \end{aligned} \]

and \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{i \in [n - 2k - 1] } \epsilon_{a + i} \in \Theta \left( \frac{1}{12} \cdot \sum_{i \in [n - 2k - 1] } \left( \frac{1}{a + i} - \frac{1}{a + i + 1}\right) \right) \subset \Theta \left( 1 \right). \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Reference

[1] Karger, David R. "Global Min-cuts in RNC, and Other Ramifications of a Simple Min-Cut Algorithm." Soda. Vol. 93. 1993.

[2] D. R. Karger and C. Stein, “A new approach to the minimum cut problem,” J. ACM, 1996.

Random Points on Shpere

Posted on 2018-07-20 Edited on 2018-09-16

To generate a random unit vector in \(R^n\), the most trivial approach is to use spherical coordinates and generate the angles uniformly at random. Here we introduce another way : sample \(n\) independent identical random variables from Gaussian distribution \(N(0, 1)\), denoted as \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\). Then a unit vector \(v\) is obtained by

\[ v = \frac{1}{\sum_{i = 1}^n x_i^2 }(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n) \]

The key observation is that all points at a fixed distance from the origin is equally generated. To see this, the joints distribution of \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\) is given by

\[ p = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{n / 2} } \exp \big( - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 }{ 2 } \big) \]

\(p\) is fixed when \(\sum_{i = 1}^n x_i^2\) is fixed.

Pairwise Independent Hashing

Posted on 2018-07-18 Edited on 2023-07-16

Let \(U \in \N^+\), and \(S = \{ x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m \} \subseteq \mathbb{Z}_U\) be a set of distinct element.

Consider a function \(h: \mathbb{Z}_U \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}_n\), where \(n \le U\). We call \(h\) a hash function. In the case where \(n < U\), there must be some \(i \in \mathbb{Z}_n\), s.t., \(|h^{-1}(i)| > 1\). We are interested in the number of total duplicate value in \[ h(S) \doteq \{ h(x_1), h(x_2), \ldots, h(x_m) \}. \]

If both \(S\) and \(h\) are fixed, there is nothing uncertain. There are two ways to add some randomness to our analysis

  1. \(h\) is fixed, and \(S\) follows some distribution on \(\mathbb{Z}_U\). E.g., let \(U = 2^{20}\) and \(h(x) = x \ \& \ 1023\) that returns the last \(10\) bits of \(x\) (in binary form). If \(S\) is sampled uniformly at random from \(\mathbb{Z}_U\), then on average for each \(x_i \in S\), there are \(\frac{k - 1}{1024}\) elements that have the same value as \(x_i\) under \(h\).

  2. The assumption that \(S\) follows some distribution is too strong. Instead we assume that given \(S\), we select a function \(h\) uniformly, from a set of functions \(\mathcal{H}\). Now we are interested in the probability \[ \Pr[ h(x_i) = h(x_j) ], \quad i \neq j. \]

The finite field \(\mathbb{F}_p\)

Let \(p > U\) be a prime number and \[ \mathcal{H} \doteq \left\{ h(x) \doteq \left( ax + b \mod p \right) \mod n \, \middle| \, a, b \in \mathbb{F}_p \right\}. \]

Theorem. If \(h\) is sampled uniformly at random from \(\mathcal{H}\), then \[ \Pr[ h(x_i) = h(x_j) ] \le \frac{1}{n}, \quad x_i \neq x_j. \]

Lemma 1. Given \(x_i, x_j, s, t \in \mathbb{F}_p\), s.t., \(x_i \neq x_j\), there is a unique pair \((a,b) \in \mathbb{F}_p \times \mathbb{F}_p\) \[ a x_i + b = s, \\ a x_j + b = t, \] where the equations hold in the field \(\mathbb{F}_p\).

Proof. Since \(x_i \neq x_j\), the following matrix is invertible \[ \begin{bmatrix} x_i , 1 \\ x_j , 1 \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{F}_p^{2 \times 2}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_i, 1 \\ x_j, 1 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} s \\ t \end{bmatrix} \]

\(\square\)

Proof of the Theorem.

For \(x_i \neq x_j\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[h(x_i) = h(x_j)] &= \sum_{s, t \in \mathbb{F}_p, s \equiv t \pmod n} \Pr[ h(x_i) = s \wedge h(x_j) = t] \\ &= \sum_{s, t \in \mathbb{F}_p, s \equiv t \pmod n} \frac{1}{ p^2 } \\ &= \sum_{s \in \mathbb{F}_p} \left( \sum_{t \in \mathbb{F}_p, t \equiv s \pmod n} \frac{1}{ p^2 } \right) \\ &\le \sum_{s \in \mathbb{F}_p} \frac{p}{ n } \cdot \frac{1}{ p^2 } \\ &= \frac{1}{ n }. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Lemma 3. The expected number of pair \(x_i, x_j\), s.t., \(x_i \neq x_j \wedge h(x_i) = h(x_j)\) is given by \[ \mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{x_i \neq x_j} \mathbb{I}_{h(x_i) = h(x_j)} \right] = \sum_{x_i \neq x_j} \Pr[h(x_i = x_j)] = \binom{m}{2} \frac{1}{n} = \frac{m (k - 1)}{ 2n } \]

When \(m^2 \le n\), then \(\mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{x_i \neq x_j} \mathbb{I}_{h(x_i) = h(x_j)} \right] \le \frac{1}{2}\). By Markov inequality, \[ \Pr[\exists \, \, \text{collision}] \le \frac{1}{ 2 }. \]

Lemma 4. With probability at most \(1/2\), some bin contains more than \(\frac{m}{n} + \sqrt{2m}\) balls.

For a fixed bin, let \(Y\) denote the number of ball in this bin. Further, define \[ Y_i = \begin{cases} 1, \qquad \text{if the } i \text{-th ball falls into the bin} \\ 0, \qquad \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \] Then \(Y = \sum_i Y_i\), and \[ \mathbb{E} [Y] = \frac{m}{n} \\ \] By pair-wise independence, we have \[ Var[Y] = \sum_i Var[Y_i] = m \frac{1}{n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n} \right) \] Therefore, by Chebyshev inequality, we have \[ \Pr \left[ |Y - \mathbb{E}[Y]| \ge \sqrt{2n} \sqrt{ m \frac{1}{n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n} \right)} \right] = \Pr[|Y - \mathbb{E}[Y]| \ge \sqrt{2n} \cdot \sigma] \le \frac{Var[Y]}{2n\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{2n}. \] Finally, applying union bound over \(n\) bins, we get \[ \Pr[\text{some bin gets more than } \sqrt{2m} \text{ balls} ] \le \frac{1}{2}. \]

The finite field \(\mathbb{F}_{2^k}\)

We consider an extension such that each element \(x_i \in S\) is now a vector \(\in \mathbb{F}_2^k\).

We would like to have some operations \(\otimes\) and \(\oplus\), such that if we sample a hash function \(h : \mathbb{F}_2^k \rightarrow \mathbb{F}_2^k\) uniformly at random from the following family \[ \mathcal{H} = \left\{ h(x) \doteq a \otimes x \oplus b \, \middle| \, a, b \in \mathbb{F}_2^k \right\}, \]

then \(h(x_i)\) and \(h(x_j)\) are pairwise independent for \(x_i \neq x_j\).

In particular, when \(x_i \neq x_j\), it holds that \[ \begin{align*} \Pr[ h(x_i) = s \, \wedge \, h(x_j) = t] &= \Pr[ h(x_i) = s ] \cdot \Pr[ h(x_j) = t ] \\ &= \left( \frac{1}{2^k} \right)^2, &\quad \forall s, t \in \mathbb{F}_2^k. \end{align*} \]

We would like the \(a, b \in \mathbb{F}_2^k\) to be determined by \[ \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_i, 1 \\ x_j, 1 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} s \\ t \end{bmatrix}. \]

In order that this holds, the set of vectors \(\mathbb{F}_2^m\), when augmented with operations \(\otimes\) and \(\oplus\), should constitute a field.

Fact. For each \(k \in \mathbb{N}^+\), there is a polynomial \(q_k\) with coefficients in \(\mathbb{F}_2\) and of degree \(k\) that is irreducible (cannot be factor).

We now view \(x_i = (x_i[0], \ldots, x_i[k - 1])\) as a polynomial \[ p_{x_i}(z) \doteq \left( x_i[0] + x_i[1] \cdot z + \cdots + x_i[k - 1] \cdot z^{k - 1} \right) \mod q_k. \]

The \(\oplus\) is defined to be the addition of polynomials. Hence, \[ x_i \oplus x_j = (x_i[0] + x_j[0], \ldots, x_i[k - 1] + x_j[k - 1]), \] since \[ \begin{aligned} &p_{x_i}(z) + p_{x_j}(z) \\ &= \left( ( x_i[0] + x_j[0] ) + ( x_i[1] + x_j[1] ) \cdot z + \cdots + ( x_i[k - 1] + x_j[k - 1] ) \cdot z^{k - 1} \right) \mod q_k. \end{aligned} \]

Moreover, we can consider the \(\otimes\) as polynomial multiplications \(\pmod q_k\), which results in a polynomial of degree at most \(k\).

Finally, since \(q_k\) is irreducible, if \(x_i \neq 0\), there exists polynomial \(c\) and \(d\), such that \(c \pmod q_k \equiv c\) and \[ 1 = c \cdot x_i + d \cdot q_k. \]

Hence, \(c\) can be viewed as the inverse of \(x_i\) and division is well-defined. It concludes that \((\mathbb{F}_2^k, \otimes, \oplus)\) constitute a field.
