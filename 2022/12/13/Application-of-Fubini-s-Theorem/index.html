Application of Fubini's Theorem

Posted on 2022-12-13

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \lambda)\) be a probability space, and \(X, Y : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be independent random variables.

Define the distributions of \(X\) and \(Y\) by \[ \begin{aligned} \mu(A) \doteq \Pr[ X \in A ] \doteq \lambda \Big(X^{-1}(A) \Big), \quad \forall A \in \mathcal{B}, \\ \nu(B) \doteq \Pr[ Y \in B ] \doteq \lambda \Big(Y^{-1}(B) \Big), \quad \forall B \in \mathcal{B}. \end{aligned} \]

Hence, both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are probability measures on \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B})\). Denote \(\mu \times \nu\) the product measure of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) on \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2)\).

By independence of \(X\) and \(Y\), \(\forall A, B \in \mathcal{B}\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ X \in A, Y \in B ] &= \Pr[ X \in A ] \cdot \Pr[ Y \in B ] \\ &= \mu(A) \cdot \nu(B) \\ &= (\mu \times \nu) (A \times B). \end{aligned} \]

Since \(\mathcal{S} \doteq \{ A \times B: A, B \in \mathcal{B} \}\) generates \(\mathcal{B}^2\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{S}) = \mathcal{B}^2\), applying \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, we get \[ \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] = (\mu \times \nu) (M), \quad \forall M \in \mathcal{B}^2. \]

It follows that \(\mu \times \nu\) is the distribution of \((X, Y)\).

We list some interesting corollaries based on Fubini's theorem.

Corollary One. For each \(M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), it holds that \[ \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \mu( dx ). \] Similarly, for each \(A \in \mathcal{B}, M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), \[ \Pr[ X \in A, (X, Y) \in M ] = \int_{A} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \mu( dx ). \]

Proof. Via Fubini's theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[ (X, Y) \in M ] &= \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ] \, \, (\mu \times \nu)( d (x, y) ). \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ] \, \, \nu( dy ) \, \, \mu( dx ) \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \nu( \{ y : (x, y) \in M \} ) \, \, \mu( dx ) \\ &= \int_{\mathbb{R}} \Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ] \, \, \mu( dx ), \end{aligned} \] where \(\Pr[ (x, Y) \in M ]\) is a shorthand for \[ Y \in M_x \doteq \{ y : (x, y) \in M \}. \]

Substituting the function \(\mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ]\) with \[ \mathbb{I}[ x \in A ] \cdot \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M ]. \] proves the second claim.

\(\square\)

Another corollary is a direct application of the Fubini's theorem.

Corollary Two. Assume that \(f: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a function measurable \(\mathcal{B}^2 / \mathcal{B}\). Define \[ g(x) \doteq \mathbb{E}[ f(x, Y) ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x, y) \,\, \nu (dy), \quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R}. \] Then we have: \[ \mathbb{E}[ g(X) ] = \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ], \] i.e., \[ \int_{\mathbb{R}} \left( \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x, y) \,\, \nu (dy) \right) \, \, \mu (dx) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x, y) \, \, (\mu \times \nu)(d (x,y)) \]

Remarks:

  1. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are not independent, then the above claim may not hold, since the product measure \(\mu \times \nu\) may not be the joint distribution of \((X, Y)\).

  2. In the above, we operate on the product space \((\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B}, \mu \times \nu) = (\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)\), instead of \((\Omega \times \Omega, \mathcal{F} \times \mathcal{F}, \lambda \times \lambda) = (\Omega^2, \mathcal{F}^2, \lambda^2)\).

  3. There are various interpretations of \(\mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ]\):

  • \(f(X, Y)\) can be viewed as a function from \(\Omega\) to \(\mathbb{R}\), given by \(\omega \rightarrow f( X(\omega), Y(\omega) )\). It is easy to see that it is measurable \(\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{B}\), and therefore is a random variable. Then \[ \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ] = \int_\Omega f( X(\omega), Y(\omega) ) \, \, \lambda (d \omega). \]

  • Alternative, we can view \(f(x, y)\) as a measurable function from the probability space \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{B}^2, \mu \times \nu)\), to \(\mathbb{R}\). It is a random variable, and \[ \mathbb{E} [ f(X, Y) ] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x, y) \, \, (\mu \times \nu)(d (x,y)). \]

  • It can be proven that the two integrals are equal:

    • If \(f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in A \times B]\), for some \(A, B \in \mathcal{B}\), then by independence of \(X\) and \(Y\), both integrals equal \(\mu(A) \cdot \nu(B)\).

    • Since \(\mathcal{S} \doteq \{ A \times B: A, B \in \mathcal{B} \}\) generates \(\mathcal{B}^2\), i.e., \(\sigma(\mathcal{S}) = \mathcal{B}^2\), applying \(\pi\)-\(\lambda\) theorem, if \(f(x, y) = \mathbb{I}[ (x, y) \in M]\) for some \(M \in \mathcal{B}^2\), the two integral are equal.

    • By linearity of integration, if \(f(x, y)\) is \(\mathcal{B}^2\) simple function, the two integral are equal.

    • By monotone convergence theorem, if \(f(x, y)\) is a non-negative measurable function, the two integral are equal.

    • By decomposing \(f\) into non-negative and negative parts, if \(f(x, y)\) is an integrable function, the two integral are equal.

Reference

[1] P. Billingsley, Probability and measure, 3rd ed. New York: Wiley, 1995.
