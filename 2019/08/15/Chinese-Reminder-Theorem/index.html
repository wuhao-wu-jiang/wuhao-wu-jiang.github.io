Chinese-Reminder-Theorem

Posted on 2019-08-15 Edited on 2022-10-11

Chinese-Reminder-Theorem

If \(n_1, n_2,\dots,n_k \in \mathbb{N}\) are relatively prime, then for \(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_k \in \mathbb{N}\), there exists \(x \in \mathbb{N}\) such that \[ \begin{aligned} x \equiv a_1 \pmod {n_1} \\ x \equiv a_2 \pmod {n_2} \\ \dots \\ x \equiv a_k \pmod {n_k} \end{aligned} \]

Proof One

Define \(N_i = (\prod_{j \in [k] } n_j) / {n_i}\) for every \(i \in [k]\). Clearly \(n_j | N_i\) for every \(j \in [k] \setminus \{ i \}\). Moreover \(N_i\) is coprime to \(n_i\). Therefore, \(\exists M_i \in [1, N_i - 1]\), s.t., \[ M_i \equiv N_i^{-1} \pmod {n_i} \] It follows that \(a_i M_i N_i \equiv a_i \pmod {n_i}\).

Now \[ \sum_{i = 1}^k a_i M_i N_i \] is a solution to the problem. Indeed, for every \(\ell \in \mathbb{Z}\), \[ \sum_{i = 1}^k a_i M_i N_i + \ell \cdot \prod_{i = 1}^k n_i \] is also a solution.

\(\square\)

Proof Two

There is a probably more intuitive proof. First we investigate a simple example of pair \(2\) and \(3\) and their modules \[ \begin{matrix} & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & \dots \\ \mod 2 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & \dots \\ \mod 3 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 & 2 & \dots \end{matrix} \] Note that there is one-to-one correspondence between numbers in \(\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}\) and pairs in \(\{0, 1\} \times \{0, 1, 2\}\). In general, if integers \(n_1, n_2,\dots,n_k\) that are relatively prime, and two numbers \(1 \le x \le y \le \prod_j n_j\), if \[ \begin{matrix} x \equiv y \pmod {n_1}, \\ x \equiv y \pmod {n_2}, \\ \dots \\ x \equiv y \pmod {n_k}, \end{matrix} \] then \(x = y\). To see this, we have for any \(1 \le j \le k\), \[ n_j | (y - x ) \] As the \(n_j\)'s are coprime, we have \(\prod_j n_j | (y - x)\), hence \(y - x = 0\) and \(y = x\). Then we can define a one-to-one correspondence between \([\prod_j n_j]\) and the \(k\) dimension vectors \([n_1] \times [n_2] \times \dots \times [n_k]\).

Corollary

There is a unique solution in \([0, \prod_{i = 1}^k n_i - 1]\).

Suppose that \(x\) and \(y\) are two solutions to the problem. Then for every \(i \in [1, k]\), \[ x - y \equiv 0 \pmod {n_k } \]

Hence, \(x - y = \ell \cdot \prod_{i = 1}^k n_i\) for some \(\ell\). Therefore, the gap between two solutions is at least \(\prod_{i = 1}^k n_i\).

\(\square\)

Applications

The theorem has many interesting applications. Here are some:

Theorem 1. Let \(\varphi(n)\) be the numbers in \([n]\) that are coprime with \(n\). If \(n = pq\) for primes \(p\) and \(q\), then \(\varphi(n) = (p - 1)(q - 1)\).

Proof. By Chinese Reminder Theorem, there is a one-to-one correspondence between \([n]\) and \([p] \times [q]\). If \(x \in [n]\) is not coprime with \(n\), either \(p \mid x\) or \(q \mid x\). Therefore, \(x\) corresponds to a pair \((p, a)\) or \((b, q)\) for some \(a \in [q]\) or \(b \in [p]\). The number of such pairs is given by \(p + q - 1\).

\(\square\)

Theorem 2. If \(n = pq\) for positive integers \(p, q\) and \(p\) and \(q\) are coprime, then \(\varphi(n) = \varphi(p) \varphi(q)\).

Proof. By Chinese Reminder Theorem, there is a one-to-one correspondence between \([n]\) and \([p] \times [q]\). If \(x \in [n]\) is coprime with \(n\), it holds that \(\gcd(x, p) = 1 \wedge \gcd(x, q) = 1\). Therefore, \(x\) corresponds to a pair for \((x \mod p, x \mod q)\) , such that \(x \mod p\) is coprime with \(p\) and \(x \mod q\) is coprime with \(q\). The number of possible values of \(x \mod p\) is \(\varphi(p)\), and the number of possible values of \(x \mod q\) is \(\varphi(q)\) . Hence \(\varphi(n) = \varphi(p) \varphi(q)\).

\(\square\)

Theorem 3. Let \(n = p_1^{k_1} p_2^{k_2} \dots p_m ^{k_m}\) be the unique prime factorization of \(n\), then \(\varphi(n) = n \prod_{i = 1}^m (1 - 1/p_i)\).

Proof. \[ \varphi(n) = \prod_{i = 1}^m \varphi(p_i^{k _i}) \] On the other hand, there are \(p_i^{k _i - 1}\) numbers in \([p_i^{k _1}]\) there are not coprime with \(p_i^{k_i}\), namely \[ 1\cdot p_i, 2 \cdot p_i, 3 \cdot p_i\dots, p_i^{k_i - 1} p_i \] Therefore, \(\varphi(p_i^{k_i}) = p_i^{k_i} - p_i^{k_i - 1} = p_i^{k_i}( 1 - 1/p_i)\) and \[ \varphi(n) = \prod_{i = 1}^m p_i^{k_i} (1 - 1/ p_i) = n \prod_{i = 1}^m (1 - 1/ p_i) \]

\(\square\)
