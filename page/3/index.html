Sub-Gaussian Random Variable

Posted on 2023-06-14 Edited on 2024-11-25

Definition (Sub-Gaussian Random Variable). A random variable \(X\) is called a sub-gaussian random variable, if it exhibits one of the following equivalent properties: let \(K_i > 0, i \in [5]\) be absolute constants.
1. The tails of \(X\) satisfy \[ \Pr[|X| \ge t] \le 2 \exp \left(− \frac{t^2}{K_1^2} \right), \quad \forall t \ge 0. \] 2. The moments of \(X\) satisfy \[ \Vert X \Vert_{L^p} = ( \mathbb{E}|X|^p )^{1/p} \le K_1 \sqrt[p]{p \cdot \Gamma(p / 2)} \le K_2 \sqrt{p}, \quad \forall p \ge 1. \] 3. The MGF of \(X^2\) satisfies \[ \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda^2 X^2) \le \exp(K_3^2 \lambda^2) \quad \forall \lambda \, \text{ s.t. }, \, |\lambda| \le 1 / K_3 . \] 4. The MGF of \(X^2\) is bounded at some point, namely \[ \mathbb{E} \exp(X^2 / K_4^2) \le 2. \]
5. Moreover, if \(\mathbb{E} [ X ] = 0\) then properties 1 - 4 are also equivalent to the following property. The MGF of \(X\) satisfies
\[ \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) \le \exp(K_5^2 \lambda^2) \quad \forall \lambda \in \R. \]

Proof.

\(1 \rightarrow 2:\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \big[ |X|^p \big] &= \int_0^\infty \Pr\big[ |X|^p \ge t \big] \, d t \\ &= \int_0^\infty \Pr\big[ |X| \ge t^{1 / p} \big] \, d t \\ &\le \int_0^\infty 2 \cdot \exp \left(− \frac{t^{2/ p}}{K_1^2} \right) \, d t & u = t^{2/p} / K_1^2 \rightarrow t = K_1^p u^{p / 2} \\ &= \int_0^\infty 2 \cdot K_1^p \cdot (p / 2) \cdot u^{p / 2 - 1} e^{-u} \, d u \\ &= K_1^p \cdot p \cdot \Gamma(p / 2) \end{aligned} \]

Applying the Stirling's approximation of \(\Gamma(x) \le x^x\), and taking the \(p\) root of both sides given \[ \mathbb{E} \big[ |X|^p \big] \le K_1 \cdot \sqrt[p]{p} \cdot \sqrt{p / 2}. \]

\(2 \rightarrow 3:\) By monotone convergence theorem, \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda^2 X^2) &= \mathbb{E} \left( 1 + \sum_{n \in \N^+} \frac{\lambda^{2n} X^{2n}}{ n! } \right) \\ &= 1 + \sum_{n \in \N^+} \mathbb{E} \left( \frac{\lambda^{2n} X^{2n}}{ n! } \right) \\ &\le 1 + \sum_{n \in \N^+} \frac{ ( \lambda^2 K_1^2 )^n \cdot 2n \cdot \Gamma(n) }{ n! } & \text{since } \mathbb{E} |X|^{2n} \le K_1^{2n} \cdot 2 n \cdot \Gamma(2n / 2) \\ &\le 1 + 2 \cdot \sum_{n \in \N^+} (\lambda^2 K_1^2 )^n \\ &= 1 + \frac{2 K_1^2 \lambda^2}{1 - K_1^2 \lambda^2} & \text{provided that } K_1^2 \lambda^2 < 1 \\ &= \frac{1 + K_1^2 \lambda^2}{1 - K_1^2 \lambda^2} \\ &= \exp(3 \cdot K_1^2 \lambda^2), & \frac{1 + x}{1 - x} \le e^{3x}, \, \forall x \in [0, 4 / 5]. \end{aligned} \]

\(3 \rightarrow 4:\) Taking \(\lambda = 1 / K_3\) in property 3 proves property 4.

\(4 \rightarrow 1:\) By Markov's inequality, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[|X| \ge t] &= \Pr[X^2 / K_4^2 \ge t^2 / K_4^2] \\ &= \Pr[\exp (X^2 / K_4^2 ) \ge \exp( t^2 / K_4^2 )] \\ &\le \frac{ \mathbb{E} \exp(X^2 / K_4^2) }{ \exp( t^2 / K_4^2 ) } \\ &\le 2 \exp( - t^2 / K_4^2 ). \end{aligned} \]

\(3 \rightarrow 5\) (when \(|\lambda| \cdot K_3 \le 1\)): Since \(e^x \le x + e^{x^2}, \forall x \in R\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) &\le \mathbb{E} \left( \lambda X + \exp(\lambda^2 X^2) \right) \\ &= \mathbb{E} \left( \exp(\lambda^2 X^2) \right) \\ &\le \exp(K_3^2 \lambda^2). \end{aligned} \]

\(4 \rightarrow 5\) (when \(|\lambda| \cdot K_3 \ge 1\)): Since \(\lambda X \le (X^2 / K_3^2 + \lambda^2 \cdot K_3^2) / 2\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) &\le \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \frac{X^2 / K_3^2 + \lambda^2 \cdot K_3^2}{2} \right) \\ &= \exp \left( \frac{\lambda^2 \cdot K_3^2}{2} \right) \cdot \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \frac{X^2 / K_3^2 }{2} \right) \\ &\le \exp \left( \frac{\lambda^2 \cdot K_3^2}{2} \right) \exp \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) \\ & \exp \left( \lambda^2 \cdot K_3^2 \right), \end{aligned} \] where the final inequality follows as \(\lambda^2 K_3^2 \ge 1\).

\(5 \rightarrow 1:\) When \(\lambda > 0\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[X \ge t] &= \Pr[ \exp(\lambda X) \ge \exp(\lambda t) ] \\ &\le \exp(- \lambda t) \cdot \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) \\ &\le \exp( - \lambda t + K_5^2 \lambda^2 ) \\ &= \exp \left( - \frac{t^2}{4 K_5^2} \right) & \text{ choosing } \lambda = 1 / (2 K_5^2). \end{aligned} \] When \(\lambda < 0\), \[ \begin{aligned} \Pr[X \le -t] &= \Pr[ \exp(\lambda X) \ge \exp(\lambda t) ] \\ &\le \exp(- \lambda t) \cdot \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) \\ &\le \exp( - \lambda t + K_5^2 \lambda^2 ) \\ &= \exp \left( - \frac{t^2}{4 K_5^2} \right) & \text{ choosing } \lambda = 1 / (2 K_5^2). \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Remark. If \[ \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) \le \exp(K_5^2 \lambda^2) \quad \forall \lambda \in \R, \] then it holds that \[ \mathbb{E} [ X ] = 0, \quad \text{ and } \quad \mathbb{E} [ |X|^k ] \in O(k!), \, \forall k \in \mathbb{N}^+. \]

Proof. Since \(\exp(|\lambda X|) \le \exp(-\lambda X) + \exp(\lambda X)\), it holds that \(\exp(|\lambda X|)\) is integrable and \[ \mathbb{E} \exp(|\lambda X|) \le 2 \cdot \exp(K_5^2 \lambda^2). \] It follows that \(\mathbb{E} \exp(|X|) \in O(1)\). Since \(|X|^k \le k! \cdot \exp(|X|)\), it is integrable and \[ \mathbb{E} |X|^k \in O(k!). \]

Further, as for each \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), it holds that \[ \sum_{k = 0}^n \frac{\lambda^k X^k}{k!} \le \sum_{k = 0}^n \frac{|\lambda|^k |X|^k}{k!} \le \exp(|\lambda X|), \] by dominated convergence theorem, we have \[ 1 + \lambda \mathbb{E}[X] + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} \mathbb{E}[X^2] + \sum_{k = 3}^\infty \frac{\lambda^k \mathbb{E}[X^k] }{k!} = \mathbb{E} \exp(\lambda X) \le \exp(K_5^2 \lambda^2) = 1 + K_5^2 \lambda^2 + \sum_{k = 2}^\infty \frac{ (K_5\lambda)^{2k} }{k!}. \] When \(|\lambda| < 1\) and \(\lambda \rightarrow 0\), clearly \[ \sum_{k = 2}^\infty \frac{ (K_5\lambda)^{2k} }{k!} \in o(\lambda^2). \]

Observe that \[ \frac{\lambda^k \mathbb{E}[X^k] }{k!} \le \frac{\lambda^k \mathbb{E}[|X|^k] }{k!} \in O(\lambda^k). \] Therefore, \[ \left| \sum_{k = 3}^\infty \frac{\lambda^k \mathbb{E}[X^k] }{k!} \right| \in O(\lambda^3) \in o(\lambda^2). \] Hence, \[ \lambda \mathbb{E}[X] + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} \mathbb{E}[X^2] + o(\lambda^2) \le K_5^2 \lambda^2 + o(\lambda^2). \]

Taking \(\lambda > 0\) and letting \(\lambda \rightarrow 0\) gives \(\mathbb{E}[X] \le 0\).

Taking \(\lambda < 0\) and letting \(\lambda \rightarrow 0\) gives \(\mathbb{E}[X] \ge 0\).

It concludes that \(\mathbb{E} [ X ] = 0\), and therefore \(\mathbb{E}[X^2] \le 2 K_5^2\).

\(\square\)

Definition. The sub-gaussian norm of \(X\), denoted \(\Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}\), is defined to be the smallest \(K_4\) in property 4, i.e., \[ \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} = \inf \{ t > 0: \mathbb{E} \exp( X^2 / t^2) \le 2 \}. \]

We first show that \(\mathbb{E} \exp( X^2 / \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}^2) \le 2\). Let \(t_1, t_2, t_3, \ldots\) be a decreasing sequence such that \(t_i - \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} \le 1 / k\). Then by monotone convergence theorem, we have \[ \mathbb{E} \exp( X^2 / \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}^2) = \mathbb{E} \lim_{i \rightarrow \infty} \exp( X^2 / t_i^2 ) = \lim_{i \rightarrow \infty} \mathbb{E} \exp( X^2 / t_i^2 ) \le 2. \]

We restate the previous theorem in terms of the sub-gaussian norm.

  1. \(\Pr (|X| \ge t) \le 2 \exp( −c t^2 / \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} ), \quad \forall t \ge 0\);
  2. \(\Vert X \Vert_{L_p} \le C \sqrt{p} \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}, \quad \forall p \ge 1\)
  3. \(\mathbb{E} \exp(X^2 / \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} ) \le 2\);
  4. if \(\mathbb{E} [ X ] = 0\) then \(\mathbb{E} \exp( \lambda X) \le \exp( C \lambda^2 \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} ) , \quad \forall \lambda \in \R\).

Here \(C, c > 0\) are absolute constants.

Theorem. \(\Vert \cdot \Vert_{\psi_2}\) is a norm on the space of sub-gaussian random variables.

Proof. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be sub-gaussian random variables, and \(a = \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}, b = \Vert Y \Vert_{\psi_2}\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \frac{X + Y}{a + b} \right)^2 &= \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \frac{X}{a} \cdot \frac{a}{a + b} + \frac{Y}{b} \cdot \frac{b}{a + b} \right)^2 \\ &\le \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{a}{a + b} \exp \left( \frac{X}{a} \right)^2 + \frac{b}{a + b} \cdot \exp \left( \frac{Y}{b} \right)^2 \right] \le 2. \end{aligned} \] Hence, \[ \Vert X + Y \Vert_{\psi_2} \le a + b = \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} + \Vert Y \Vert_{\psi_2}. \]

\(\square\)

Theorem (Sums of independent sub-gaussians) Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be independent mean-zero sub-gaussian random variables. Then \(\sum_{i \in [N]} X_i\) is also a sub-gaussian random variable, and \[ \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [N]} X_i \right\Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \le C \cdot \sum_{i \in [N]} \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \] where \(C\) is an absolute constant.

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \lambda \sum_{i \in [N]} X_i \right) &= \prod_{i \in [N]} \mathbb{E} \exp \left( \lambda X_i \right) \\ &\le \prod_{i \in [N]} \exp \left( C \lambda^2 \Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \right) = \exp \left( \sum_{i \in [N]} C \lambda^2 \Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \right). \end{aligned} \] Hence, \(\sum_{i \in [N]} X_i\) is sug-gaussian, and \(\Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \in O( \sum_{i \in [N]} \Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 )\).

\(\square\)

Theorem (General Hoeffding Inequality). Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be independent mean-zero sub-gaussian random variables. Then, \[ \Pr \left( \left| \sum_{i \in [N]} X_i \right| \ge t \right) \le 2 \exp \left( − \frac{c t^2}{\sum_{i \in [N]} \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}^2} \right), \quad t \ge 0. \]

Proof. This can be viewed as a corollary as the previous theorem.

\(\square\)

Theorem (Khintchine’s inequality) Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be independent sub-gaussian random variables with zero means and unit variances, and let \(a = (a_1,\ldots,a_N) \in \R^N\). Then for every \(p \in [2, \infty)\) we have \[ \Vert a \Vert_2 \le \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [N]} a_i X_i \right\Vert_{L_p} \le C K \sqrt{p} \Vert a \Vert_2 \] where \(K = \max_i \Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}\) and \(C\) is an absolute constant.

Proof. First, since \(p \ge 2\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [N]} a_i X_i \right\Vert_{L_p} &\ge \left\Vert \sum_{i \in [N]} a_i X_i \right\Vert_{L_2} \\ &= \left( \mathbb{E} \left[ \left( \sum_{i \in [N]} a_i X_i \right)^2 \right] \right)^{1 / 2} \\ &= \left( \sum_{i, j \in [N]} \mathbb{E} \left[ a_i a_j X_i X_j \right] \right)^{1 / 2} = \Vert a \Vert_2. \end{aligned} \]

Let \(X \doteq \sum_{i \in [N]} a_i X_i\). Then \[ \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \le \sum_{i \in [n]} \Vert a_i X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 = \sum_{i \in [n]} a_i^2 \cdot \Vert X_i \Vert_{\psi_2}^2 \le K^2 \cdot \Vert a \Vert_2^2. \] It follows that \[ \mathbb{E} [ |X|^p ] \in O( \sqrt{p} \cdot \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} ) \subseteq O( \sqrt{p} \cdot K \cdot \Vert a \Vert_2 ). \]

\(\square\)

Theorem (Centering). If \(X\) is a sub-gaussian random variable then \(X − \mathbb{E} [ X ]\) is sub-gaussian too and \[ \Vert X − \mathbb{E} [ X ] \Vert_{\psi_2} \le C \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2}, \] where \(C\) is an absolute constant.

Proof. Since \(\Vert \cdot \Vert_{\psi_2}\) is a norm, \[ \Vert X − \mathbb{E} [ X ] \Vert_{\psi_2} \le \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} + \Vert \mathbb{E} [ X ] \Vert_{\psi_2}. \] It remains to bound the second term. By definition, we have \[ \mathbb{E} \left[ \exp \left( \frac{(\mathbb{E} [ X ])^2}{ \big( (1 / \ln 2)^{1 / 2} \cdot\mathbb{E} [ X ] \big)^2} \right) \right] = 2, \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \Vert \mathbb{E} [ X ] \Vert_{\psi_2} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\ln 2}} \cdot \mathbb{E} [ X ] \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{\ln 2}} \cdot \mathbb{E} | X | \in O( \Vert X \Vert_{\psi_2} ). \end{aligned} \] \(\square\)

Reference

Leibniz’s Formula

Posted on 2023-06-07 Edited on 2024-08-27

We would like to prove that \[ \begin{align} \pi = \frac{4}{1} - \frac{4}{3} + \frac{4}{5} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{4}{9} \cdots \end{align} \]

This is a corollary of the Leibniz's Formula

Theorem. \[ \arctan(x) \doteq \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{x^{2n + 1}}{2n + 1}, \quad \forall x \in [0, 1]. \]

Substituting \(x = 1\) establishes formula \((1)\).

Proof. First, observe that \[ \arctan'(t) = \frac{1}{1 + t^2} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n t^{2n}, \quad \forall t \in [0, 1). \]

For each \(x \in [0, 1)\), Since the sequence \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n t^{2n}\) converges uniformly for \(t \in [0, x)\), \[ \begin{aligned} \arctan(x) &= \int_0^x \arctan'(t) \, dt \\ &= \int_0^x \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n t^{2n} \, dt \\ &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \int_0^x (-1)^n t^{2n} \, dt \\ &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{x^{2n + 1}}{2n + 1}. \end{aligned} \]

It is left to prove that the last equality also holds for \(x = 1\). This is a result of the following lemma, which is a special case of the Abel's test.

Lemma. Let \[ f(x) \doteq \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{x^{2n + 1}}{2n + 1}, \quad \forall x \in [0, 1]. \] Then \(f(1)\) is well-defined, and is left-continuous at \(x = 1\).

Proof 1 of Lemma. Note that \[ A \doteq f(1) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{1}{2n + 1}. \] It is easy to see that the sequence converges, and hence \(f(1)\) is well-defined.

Define \[ s_n = \sum_{\ell = 0}^n (-1)^\ell \frac{1}{2\ell + 1}, \quad \forall n \in \mathbb{N}. \]

Then for integers \(n, m \in \mathbb{N}^+\) s.t., \(n \ge m + 1\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} f_n(x) - f_{m + 1} (x) &= \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^n (s_{\ell} - s_{\ell - 1}) x^{2\ell + 1} \\ &= s_n x^{2n + 1} - s_{m} x^{2m + 3} + \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} s_{\ell} (x^{2\ell + 1} - x^{2\ell + 3}) \\ &= s_n x^{2n + 1} - s_{m} x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} s_{\ell} x^{2\ell + 1}. \end{aligned} \]

Given \(\epsilon > 0\), \(\exists N \in \mathbb{N}\), such that $ |s_- A| , N. $ Hence, \[ \begin{aligned} &s_n x^{2n + 1} - s_{m} x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} s_{\ell} x^{2\ell + 1} \\ \le &(A + \epsilon) x^{2n + 1} - (A - \epsilon) x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} (A + \epsilon) x^{2\ell + 1} \\ \le &(A + \epsilon) x^{2n + 1} - (A - \epsilon) x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \cdot (A + \epsilon) \cdot \frac{x^{2m + 3} - x^{2n + 1}}{1 - x^2} \\ = & 2 \cdot \epsilon \cdot x^{2m + 3}. \end{aligned} \]

On the other hand, \[ \begin{aligned} &s_n x^{2n + 1} - s_{m} x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} s_{\ell} x^{2\ell + 1} \\ \ge &(A - \epsilon) x^{2n + 1} - (A + \epsilon) x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \sum_{\ell = m + 1}^{n - 1} (A - \epsilon) x^{2\ell + 1} \\ \le &(A - \epsilon) x^{2n + 1} - (A + \epsilon) x^{2m + 3} + (1 - x^2) \cdot (A - \epsilon) \cdot \frac{x^{2m + 3} - x^{2n + 1}}{1 - x^2} \\ = & - 2 \cdot \epsilon \cdot x^{2m + 3}. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \[ |f_n(x) - f_{m + 1} (x)| \le 2 \cdot \epsilon. \]

\(\square\)

Proof 2 of Lemma. Note that \[ A \doteq f(1) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{1}{2n + 1}. \] It is easy to see that the sequence converges, and hence \(f(1)\) is well-defined.

Define \[ s_n = \sum_{\ell = 0}^n (-1)^\ell \frac{1}{2\ell + 1}, \quad \forall n \in \mathbb{N}. \] Since \(\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \sqrt[n]{|s_n|} = 1\), the following series converge \[ h(x) \doteq \sum_{n = 0}^\infty s_n x^{2n + 1}, \quad x \in [0, 1). \]

Hence, for each \(x \in [0, 1)\), \[ \begin{align*} h(x) - x^2 \cdot h(x) &= (1 - x^2) \cdot \sum_{n = 0}^\infty s_n x^{2n + 1} \\ &= s_0 + \sum_{n = 1}^\infty ( s_n - s_{n - 1} ) x^{2n + 1} = f(x). \end{align*} \]

Given \(\epsilon > 0\), \(\exists N \in \mathbb{N}\), such that \[ |s_n - A| \le \epsilon, \quad \forall n \ge N. \]

Then for each \(x \in [0, 1)\), \[ \begin{align*} \left| (1 - x^2) \cdot \sum_{n = N}^\infty s_n x^{2n + 1} - (1 - x^2) \cdot \sum_{n = N}^\infty A \cdot x^{2n + 1} \right| \le \epsilon \cdot (1 - x^2) \cdot \frac{1}{1 - x^2} \le \epsilon. \end{align*} \]

But \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{x \rightarrow 1^-} (1 - x^2) \sum_{n = N}^\infty A \cdot x^{2n + 1} &= \lim_{x \rightarrow 1^-} A \cdot x^{2N + 1} = A, \\ \lim_{x \rightarrow 1^-} (1 - x^2) \sum_{n = 0}^N s_n x^{2n + 1} &= 0. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Riemann Integral

Posted on 2023-05-22 Edited on 2024-08-27

Integration of Real-valued Functions

Motivation

Given an interval \([a, b]\) and some function \(f: [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), we want to create some rule, that computes the area under the curve \(y = f(x)\) between \(x = a\) and \(x = b\).

This rule, which assigns real numbers to functions, can be itself represented as a function \(\varphi_{[a, b]} : \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\). Here, \(\mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\), the domain of \(\varphi\), refers to a collection of functions defined on \([a, b]\) such that the area under the curve \(y = f(x)\) between \(x = a\) and \(x = b\) is well-defined according to the rule.

The integral of a function \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) is defined as the value obtained under the rule \(\varphi_{[a, b]}\). To represent this value, we conventionally use the notation: \[ \int_a^b f \, dx \doteq \varphi_{[a, b]} (f). \]

Similarly, when considering another interval \([s, t]\), we can establish a similar rule \(\varphi_{[s, t]}\) with domain \(\mathcal{F} \big( [s, t] \big)\). The integral of \(f\) over \([s, t]\) is denoted as: \[ \int_s^t f \, dx \doteq \varphi_{[s, t]} (f). \] In the subsequent discussion, we will employ the notation on the left-hand side exclusively.

The integral should adhere to our geometric understanding of areas and satisfy various properties. First and foremost, the integral should accurately compute the area of rectangles.

Property (Area of Rectangle). If \(\exist c \in \mathbb{R}\), s.t., \(f(x) = c\) for each \(x \in [a, b]\), then \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \(\int_a^b f \, dx = c \cdot (b - a)\). .

Secondly, the integrals should demonstrate linearity.

Property (Linearity). 1. If \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \(c \in \mathbb{R}\), then \(c \cdot f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \[ \int _{a}^{b} c \cdot f \, dx = c \cdot \int_{a}^{b} f \, dx. \]
2. If \(f_1, f_2 \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\), then \(f_1 + f_2 \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\), and
\[ \int _{a}^{b} f_1 + f_2 \, dx = \int_{a}^{b} f_1 \, dx + \int_{a}^{b} f_2 \, dx. \] 3. If \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \(c \in (a, b)\), then \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, c] \big)\) and \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [c, b] \big)\), and \[ \int _{a}^{b} f \, dx = \int _{a}^{c} f \, dx + \int _{c}^{b} f \, dx. \]

The integrals should allow for meaningful comparisons.

Property (Monotonicity). 1. If \(f_1, f_2 \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \(f_1 \le f_2\), then \[ \int_{a}^{b} f_1 \, dx \le \int_{a}^{b} f_2 \, dx. \]
2. If \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \(|f| \le M\), then \[ \left| \int_{a}^{b} f \, dx \right| \le M \cdot (b - a). \]

So far, it is straightforward to specify a family of functions, and a rule that assigning real numbers to them,satisfying the aforementioned properties. For instance, we can define \(\mathcal{F}\big( [a, b] \big)\) as the set of step functions on \([a, b]\). Each step function \(f\) can be expressed as: \[ f(x) = \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot \mathbb{I}_{E_i} (x), \] where \(E_i, i \in [n]\), are sub-intervals, forming a partition of \([a, b]\). Further, we can define the integral as \[ \int_a^b f \, dx \doteq \sum_{i \in [n]} c_i \cdot |E_i|, \] where \(|E_i|\) is the length of \(E_i\).

However, it is also desirable for the constructed integrals to satisfy convergence properties.

Property (Convergence). 1. If \(f_i \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) for each \(i \in \N^+\) and \(f_i \rightarrow f\) under some metric, then \(f \in \mathcal{F} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \int_{a}^{b} f_i \, dx = \int_{a}^{b} f \, dx. \]

Unfortunately, the previous construction does not satisfy convergence properties. Additional considerations and refinements are required in order to establish an integral that fulfills the desired convergence properties.

Construction

Bernhard Riemann presented the following construction of integral.

Let \([a, b]\) be a given interval. \(P = \{ x_0 , x_1 , \ldots, x_n \}\) is a partition of \([a, b]\), if \[ a = x_0 \le x_1 \cdots \le x_n = b. \] For each \(i \in [n]\), let \(\Delta x_i \doteq x_i, - x_{i - 1}\).

Assume \(f: [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a bounded function. Associated with each partition \(P\), define \[ M_i \doteq \sup_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} f(x), \qquad m_i \doteq \inf_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} f(x). \] Further, let \[ U(P, f) \doteq \sum_{i \in [n]} M_i \cdot \Delta x_i, \quad L(P, f) \doteq \sum_{i \in [n]} m_i \cdot \Delta x_i. \] Finally, consider \[ \overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx \doteq \inf U(P, f), \quad \underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx \doteq \sup L(P, f), \] where the inf and sup are taken over all partitions \(P\) of \([a, b]\).

Definition \(f: [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is considered Riemann integrable, if \[ \overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx = \underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx. \] In this cases, we define the integral of \(f\) over the interval \([a, b]\) as: \[ \int _{a}^{b} f \, dx \doteq \overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx = \underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx. \] We denote the collection of functions that are Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\) as \(\mathfrak{R}\big( [a, b] \big)\).

Remark. It is reasonable to assume that \(f\) is bounded on the closed interval \([a, b]\), in order that it is Riemann integrable. If \(f\) is unbounded, for each partition \(P\), there exists some \(i \in [n]\), s.t., \(M_i - m_i = \infty\). Hence it is impossible that \(\overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx = \underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx\).

Properties

It is straightforward to see the proposed construction of integral captures the area of rectangles, and demonstrates linearity and monotonicity.

Theorem. Assume that for each \(n \in \mathbb{N}^+\), \(f_n \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), and that \(f_n \rightarrow f\) uniformly on \([a, b]\) (i.e., \(\sup_{x \in [a, b]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \rightarrow 0\)). Then \(f\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), and \[ \int_a^b f \, dx = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \int_a^b f_n \, dx. \] (The existence of the limit is part of the conclusion.)

Proof. Choose \(\varepsilon > 0\). For large enough \(n\), we have \(|f_n - f| \le \varepsilon\).

Let \(P = \{ x_0 , x_1 , \ldots, x_n \}\) be a partition of \([a, b]\). \[ L(P, f_n) - \varepsilon \cdot (b - a) \le L(P, f) \le U(P, f) \le U(P, f_n) + \varepsilon \cdot (b - a) \] Taking sup and inf over all possible partitions of \(P\) on \([a, b]\), and given the Riemann integrability of \(f_n\), we have \[ \int_a^b f_n \, dx - \varepsilon \cdot (b - a) \le \underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx \le \overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx \le \int_a^b f_n \, dx + \varepsilon \cdot (b - a). \] Since \(\varepsilon\) is chosen arbitrarily, we have \(\underline{\int_{a}^{b}} f \, dx = \overline{\int _{a}^{b}} f \, dx\) and therefore \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Finally, \[ \int_a^b f_n \, dx - \varepsilon \cdot (b - a) \le \int_{a}^{b} f \, dx \le \int_a^b f_n \, dx + \varepsilon \cdot (b - a), \] implies that \(\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \int_a^b f_n \, dx\) exists, and equals \(\int_{a}^{b} f \, dx\).

\(\square\)

Integration and Differentiation

Theorem. Suppose that \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\). Define \[ F(t) \doteq \int_a^t f \, dx, \quad \forall t \in [a, b]. \] Then \(F\) is continuous on \([a, b]\); furthermore, if \(f\) is continuous at a point \(t \in [a, b]\), then \(F\) is differentiable at \(t\), and \(F'(t) = f(t)\).

Proof. Since \(f\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), it is bounded. Let \(M \doteq \max_{x \in [a, b]} |f(x)|\). It follows that \[ |F(x) - F(y)| \le M \cdot |x - y|, \quad \forall x, y \in [a, b]. \] which proves uniform continuity of \(F\).

With out lose of generality, assume that \(t \in (a, b)\). Since \(f\) is continuous at \(t\), \(\exists \delta > 0\), s.t., \([t - \delta, t + \delta] \subseteq [a, b]\) and \(\forall s \in [t - \delta, t + \delta]\), it holds that \(|f(s) - f(t)| \le \epsilon\). It follows that for all \(\ell, r \in [t - \delta, t + \delta]\), \[ \begin{aligned} \left| \frac{F(r) - F(\ell)}{r - \ell} - f(t) \right| &= \left| \frac{1}{r - \ell} \int_\ell^r [ f(x) - f(t) ] \, dx \right| \\ &\le \frac{1}{r - \ell} \int_\ell^r \left| f(x) - f(t) \right| \, dx \le 2 \cdot \epsilon. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Theorem (The fundamental theorem of calculus). Suppose that \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and that \(F\) are differentiable function on \([a, b]\) such that \(F' = f\), then \[ \int_a^b f \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \]

Proof. Let \(P = \{ x_0 , x_1 , \ldots, x_n \}\) be a partition of \([a, b]\). For each \(i \in [n]\), by mean value theorem, \(\exists t_i \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]\), s.t., \[ F(x_i) - F(x_{i - 1}) = f(t_i) \cdot \Delta x_i. \] Hence, \[ L(P, f) \le F(b) - F(a) = \sum_{i \in [n]} f(t_i) \cdot \Delta x_i \le U(P, f). \] Taking sup and inf over all partitions \(P\) finishes the proof.

\(\square\)

Corollary (Integration by parts). If \(f_1, f_2\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), and \(F_1\) and \(F_2\) are differentiable function on \([a, b]\) such that \(F_1' = f_1\) and \(F_2' = f_2\), then \[ \int_a^b F_1 \cdot f_2 \, dx = F_1(b) \cdot F_2(b) - F_1(a) \cdot F_2(a) - \int_a^b F_2 \cdot f_1 \, dx. \]

Existence

In this section, we delve into the functions are encompassed within \(\mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Theorem.
1. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) then \(f\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).
4. If \(f\) is monotonic on \([a, b]\), then \(f\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).
5. If \(f\) is bounded on \([a, b]\) and has only finitely many points of discontinuity on \([a, b]\), then \(f\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Theorem. Suppose 1. \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), and \(f([a, b]) \subseteq [m, M]\), 2. \(g: [m, M] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is continuous, and 3. \(h \doteq g \circ f\).

Then \(h \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Note that if \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), then it is bounded. Therefore, we can always find a closed interval \([m, M] \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) such that \(f([a, b]) \subseteq [m, M]\). The only requirement of the theorem is that, the continuous function \(g\) is defined over some of such intervals.

Proof. Consider a given \(\varepsilon > 0\).

Since \(g\) is continuous on \([m, M]\), there exists \(K \doteq \sup_{t \in [m, M]} | g(t) |\). Further, it is uniformly continuous on \([m, M]\). Hence,
\[ \exists \delta \in (0, \varepsilon), \, \quad \text{s.t.} | g(s) - g(t) | < \varepsilon, \quad \forall s, t \in [m, M] : |s - t| \le \delta. \]

Since \(f \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), there is a partition \(P = \{ x_0 , x_1 , \ldots, x_n \}\) of \([a, b]\) such that \[ U(P,f) - L(P,f) < \delta^2. \]

Let \[ M_i \doteq \sup_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} f(x), \qquad m_i \doteq \inf_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} f(x), \\ M_i^* \doteq \sup_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} h(x), \qquad m_i^* \doteq \inf_{x \in [x_{i - 1}, x_i]} h(x). \]

Clearly it holds that \(|M_i^*| \le K\) and \(|m_i^*| \le K\). Define \[ A \doteq \{ i \in [n]: M_i - m_i < \delta \}, \quad B \doteq \{ i \in [n]: M_i - m_i \ge \delta \}. \]

Then we have

  • \(\forall i \in A\), \(M_i^* - m_i^* \le \varepsilon\), and
  • \(\sum_{i \in B} \Delta x_i \le \delta\) since \[ \delta^2 > \sum_{i \in B} (M_i - m_i) \Delta x_i \ge \delta \sum_{i \in B} \Delta x_i. \]

It follows that \[ \begin{aligned} U(P, h)- L(P, h) &= \sum_{i \in A} (M_i^* - m_i^*) \Delta x_i + \sum_{i \in B} (M_i^* - m_i^*) \Delta x_i \\ &\le \varepsilon \cdot (b - a) + 2 \cdot K \cdot \delta \\ &< \varepsilon \cdot [b - a + 2 \cdot K]. \end{aligned} \]

\(\square\)

Corollary. If \(f_1, f_2 \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\), then 1. \(f_1 \cdot f_2\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\); 2. \(|f_1|\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\) and \[ \left| \int _{a}^{b} f_1 \, dx \right| \le \int_{a}^{b} | f_1 | \, dx. \]

Proof. Since the square function is continuous on \(\mathbb{R}\), it follows that \((f_1 + f_2)^2, (f_1 - f_2)^2 \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\). Claim 1 follows from that \(4 \cdot f_1 \cdot f_2 = (f_1 + f_2)^2 - (f_1 - f_2)^2\).

Since the absolute function is continuous on \(\mathbb{R}\), it follows that \(|f_1|\in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\). The inequality follows from monotonicity of Riemann integration.

\(\square\)

Integration of Vector-valued Functions

Let \(\vec{f} \doteq (f_1, \ldots , f_k): [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^k\) vector-valued function. We call it Riemann integrable if for each \(i \in [k]\), \(f_i \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\). In this case, we write \[ \int_a^b \vec{f} \, dx \doteq \left( \int_a^b f_1 \, dx, \ldots , \int_a^b f_k \, dx \right). \]

Properties

Theorem. If \(\vec{f} \doteq (f_1, \ldots , f_k): [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^k\) is Riemann integrable, then \[ \lVert \vec{f} \rVert_2 \doteq \sqrt{f_1^2 + \cdots + f_k^2} \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big). \] Further it holds that \[ \left\lVert \int_a^b \vec{f} \, dx \right\rVert_2 \le \int_a^b \lVert \vec{f} \rVert_2 \, dx. \]

Proof. Given the Riemann integrability of \(f_i\) on \([a, b]\) and the continuity of the square function on \(\mathbb{R}\), we can deduce that \(f_i^2 \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\). Thus, \(\lVert \vec{f} \rVert_2^2 \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Moreover, considering the Riemann integrability of each \(f_i\) on the interval \([a, b]\), we can infer that they are bounded. Hence, we can assume an upper bound \(M\) for each \(f_i^2\).

Furthermore, since the square root function is continuous on the interval \([0, M]\), we can conclude that \(\lVert \vec{f} \rVert_2 \in \mathfrak{R} \big( [a, b] \big)\).

Let \(\vec{y} = (y_1, \ldots, y_k)\) be a shorthand for \(\int_a^b \vec{f} \, dx\). Then \[ \lVert \vec{y} \rVert_2^2 = \sum_{i \in [k]} y_i^2 = \sum_{i \in [k]} y_i \int_a^b f_i \, dx = \int_a^b \sum_{i \in [k]} y_i f_i \, dx \le \int_a^b \lVert \vec{y} \rVert_2 \cdot \lVert \vec{f} \rVert_2 \, dx \]

\(\square\)
