Complex Numbers

Posted on 2020-12-18 Edited on 2020-12-21

I feel uncomfortable with complex numbers for a long time, partially because of the way they are introduced in the textbooks. They are considered necessary for solving equations such as

x2+1=0.x^2 + 1= 0. x2+1=0.

Somehow magically, a symbol iii is introduced, and its square i2i^2i2 is manually defined as −1-1−1. Following this, typically a set of operations defined on complex numbers as well as their properties are listed by the textbooks, leaving the question of why we can have such “magical symbol” unsolved.

In this blog, I am trying to introduce complex numbers in a more natural manner, by studying rotations, dilations and additions of points in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. I will show that these operations satisfy some properties, such that we can manipulate points in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 in a similar way as real numbers (by addition and multiplication).

We first reflect on the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R. They are concrete, since they can be represented by points on the real axis. We can perform addition (+++) and multiplication (⋅\cdot⋅) on them, that satisfy the following properties (referred to as field axioms). Let a,b,c∈Ra, b, c \in \mathbb{R}a,b,c∈R,

  1. Associativity of addition: a+(b+c)=(a+b)+ca + (b + c) = (a + b) + ca+(b+c)=(a+b)+c.
  2. Commutativity of addition: a+b=b+aa + b = b + aa+b=b+a.
  3. Additive identity: there exists a ‘zero’ element 000 such that a+0=aa + 0 = aa+0=a.
  4. Additive inverse: there is an element, denoted as −a-a−a, such that a+(−a)=0a + (-a) = 0a+(−a)=0.
  5. Associativity of multiplication: a⋅(b⋅c)=(a⋅b)⋅ca \cdot (b \cdot c) = (a \cdot b) \cdot ca⋅(b⋅c)=(a⋅b)⋅c.
  6. Commutativity of addition: a⋅b=b⋅aa \cdot b = b \cdot aa⋅b=b⋅a.
  7. Multiplicative identity: there exists a ‘one’ element 111 such that a⋅1=aa \cdot 1 = aa⋅1=a.
  8. Multiplicative inverse: there exists a element a−1a^{-1}a−1 for a≠0a \neq 0a​=0 such that a⋅a−1=1a \cdot a^{-1} = 1a⋅a−1=1.
  9. Distributivity: a⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅ca \cdot (b + c) = a \cdot b + a \cdot ca⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅c.

Any set with two operations that satisfy similar properties as above is called a field. In the remainder of the blog, we show how to construct a field from R2\mathbb{R}^2R2.

We can view R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 as the set of vectors that originates from the origin. Hence, each element in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 is considered as both a point and a vector. We will define addition and multiplication for R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. For the moment, we will use ⊕\oplus⊕ and ⊙\odot⊙ to denote addition and multiplications in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, to distinguish the operations +++ and ⋅\cdot⋅ for R\mathbb{R}R. Lastly, we show that the set system (R2,⊕,⊙)(\mathbb{R}^2, \oplus, \odot)(R2,⊕,⊙) constitutes a field.

In the remainder of the blog, when we refer to a vector, we refer to the one that originate from the origin.
